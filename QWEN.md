## Overview

The Honda H22 is a high-performance 2.2-liter inline-4 DOHC VTEC engine that debuted in September 1991 as part of Honda's H-series engine family. It became the signature powerplant for the 4th and 5th generation Honda Prelude (1992-2001) and was also used in various Accord models across different markets worldwide.

### Key Characteristics

- **Engine Family:** H-Series (derived from F-series architecture)
- **Production Period:** 1991-2001
- **Displacement:** 2,157 cc (2.2L)
- **Configuration:** Inline-4, DOHC, 16-valve with VTEC
- **Power Range:** 185-220 HP (depending on variant and market)
- **Torque:** 206-221 Nm (152-163 lb-ft)
- **Redline:** 7,200-8,200 RPM
- **VTEC Engagement:** ~5,500 RPM
- **Block Material:** Aluminum with FRM (Fiber-Reinforced Metal) cylinder liners
- **Weight:** ~385 lbs (long block)

### Historical Significance

The H22 represented Honda's commitment to high-revving, naturally aspirated performance during the 1990s golden era. It was:
- The first Honda engine with DOHC VTEC offered in the US market (1993 Prelude VTEC)
- The Prelude's signature engine for its entire 4th and 5th generation runs
- Successfully used in touring car racing (JTCC champion 1996-1997)
- One of the most torque-rich 4-cylinder Hondas of its era
- A popular swap candidate for lighter chassis (CR-X, Civic, Integra, Miata)
- Considered one of the best "bang-for-buck" tuning platforms

The H22 was discontinued in 2001 and replaced by the K20A and K24A engines as Honda transitioned to their new engine family.

## Development History & Timeline

### 1991: Introduction

- **September 1991:** 4th generation Honda Prelude (BB1 chassis) launched with new H-series engines
- H22 and H23 introduced as the flagship powerplants
- Aluminum block with FRM cylinder liners represented advanced technology
- DOHC VTEC design derived from F-series architecture

### 1992-1993: Early Production

- 1992: Initial JDM market release with H22A (200 PS)
- 1993: USDM introduction as H22A1 in Prelude VTEC (190 HP)
- First Honda in the US with DOHC VTEC technology
- 4WS (Four-Wheel Steering) option available on certain models

### 1994: Racing Development

- Honda Europe begins developing H22A-based Formula 3 engine
- Destroked from 2.2L to 2.0L (F3-2000cc) for European F3 series
- Neil Brown Engineering contracted to convert H22A for racing

### 1995-1997: Motorsport Success

- **1995-1997:** H22A-based F3 engine used in British Touring Car Championship (BTCC)
- **1996-1997:** Honda Japan uses H22A-based engine in Japan Touring Car Championship (JTCC)
- **1996-1997:** JTCC championship won both years with H22A-derived engine
- Mugen Motorsports develops F20B (MF204B) from H22A platform (1997-2001)

### 1997: Major Redesign

- 5th generation Prelude (BB6 chassis) introduced
- Block design changes: transition from closed deck (1992-1996) to open deck (1997-2001)
- Main journal diameter increased from 50mm to 55mm (1997+)
- H22A4 becomes the sole USDM variant (200 HP)
- Introduction of SH (Super Handling) model with torque-vectoring ATTS system

### 1998: European Market Expansion

- H22A7 introduced in European Accord Type R (CH1 chassis)
- H22A8 released in European Prelude 2.2VTi/VTi-S
- Rare JDM H23A DOHC VTEC variant produced (blue top)

### 1999-2001: Final Years

- H22Z1 variant for Australian VTi-R models (speculation of 203 PS output)
- Continued production through 2001
- Prelude production ends in 2001, marking end of H22 production
- Successor: K20A and K24A engines introduced

### Discontinuation Factors

- Physical size made it less suitable for smaller chassis
- K-series offered better power-to-weight ratio
- Changing emissions and safety regulations
- Shift toward more compact, efficient engine designs
- K-series had better aftermarket support and swap compatibility

## Mechanical Design & Components

### Cylinder Block Design

#### Block Architecture
- **Material:** Aluminum alloy (A356-class: Al-6.5–7.5%Si-0.25–0.45%Mg) with FRM (Fiber-Reinforced Metal) cylinder liners
- **Casting Process:** Sand casting (green-sand mold) with ceramic cores — not HPDC (unlike F-series)
- **Heat Treatment:** T6 (solutionize ~538°C → quench → age 155°C) — tensile strength 260–320 MPa, hardness 85–120 BHN
- **Deck Type:** 
  - 1992-1996: Closed deck (stronger, better for high boost)
  - 1997-2001: Open deck (lighter, more common)
- **Block Height:** 219.5 mm
- **Main Bearing Caps:** 5-bolt design (1992-1996: 50mm journals; 1997+: 55mm journals)
- **Balancer Shafts:** Integrated to reduce vibration
- **Oil Galleries:** Standard and VTEC-specific passages

#### FRM Liner Details
- **Composition:** Metal Matrix Composite (MMC) — Saffil alumina fibers (95–97% Al₂O₃, ~3 micron diameter) + carbon fiber infiltrated by aluminum matrix
- **Thickness:** ~0.5 mm
- **Formation:** In-situ during sand casting — fiber preforms placed in mold, molten aluminum penetrates preform forming MMC liner integral with block
- **Hardness:** ~9 on Mohs scale (approaching sapphire); requires diamond/BNB honing stones
- **Advantages:** Weight reduction (~4.5 kg vs cast iron), compact design, superior high-temp strength (factor of 4 improvement at 300°C), excellent wear resistance
- **Disadvantages:** Accelerated piston ring wear → oil consumption; cannot be machined conventionally; requires full resleeving for rebuild (RS Machine, Darton, Golden Eagle sleeves)
- **Applications:** H22A/H22A1/H22A4, H23A, F20C, F22C, C30A, C32B (NSX), B21A1
- **Note:** Unlike Nikasil/Alusil (BMW/VW), FRM is not sulfur-sensitive

#### Porosity Control
- **Degassing:** Rotary argon/nitrogen purging (target < 0.15 mL H₂/100g Al)
- **Shrinkage control:** Metal chills in thick sections, riser design, ceramic foam filters (30–60 PPI)
- **Quality gate:** Pressure leak testing of coolant/oil passages (2–4 bar coolant, 4–6 bar oil)

#### FRM Liner Characteristics
- **Material:** Fiber-Reinforced Metal coating
- **Advantages:** Lightweight, good heat transfer
- **Disadvantages:** Difficult to machine, requires special sleeving for rebuilds
- **Replacement:** RS Machine sleeves or similar aftermarket solutions
- **Note:** FRM sleeves are a known limitation for high-power builds

### Cylinder Head Design

#### Head Architecture
- **Configuration:** DOHC, 16-valve (4 valves per cylinder)
- **Material:** Aluminum alloy
- **Combustion Chamber:** Pent-roof design
- **Valve Angles:** Optimized for high-RPM flow
- **Port Design:** 
  - Redtop: Light factory porting
  - Blacktop: Standard porting

#### Valve Train
- **Intake Valves:** 35 mm diameter
- **Exhaust Valves:** 30 mm diameter
- **Valve Springs:** Dual springs (VTEC specific)
- **Retainers:** Steel alloy (standard) / Aftermarket options available
- **Keepers:** Standard Honda design

#### Camshaft Drive
- **Type:** Timing belt driven
- **Tensioner:** 
  - 1992-1996: Hydraulic auto tensioner (failure prone)
  - 1997-2001: Improved hydraulic tensioner
- **Recommended Upgrade:** H23 manual tensioner or KS Tuned aftermarket tensioner

### Internal Components

#### Crankshaft
- **Material:** Forged steel
- **Stroke:** 90.7 mm
- **Main Journals:** 50mm (1992-1996), 55mm (1997-2001)
- **Rod Journals:** 47.95 mm
- **Counterweights:** Balanced for high-RPM operation
- **Oil Passages:** Internal drilling for rod bearings

#### Connecting Rods
- **Material:** Forged steel
- **Length:** 143 mm
- **Width:** 23.75 mm
- **Bolt Type:** ARP-style (aftermarket upgrades available)
- **Big End:** Split design with cap
- **Small End:** Piston pin bore 21.97-21.98 mm

#### Pistons
- **Material:** Forged aluminum (aftermarket) / Cast (stock)
- **Design:** Dome or flat-top depending on variant
- **Compression Height:** 31 mm
- **Pin Bore:** 21.97-21.98 mm
- **Ring Pack:** Standard 3-ring design
- **Skirt Coating:** Anti-friction coating

#### Piston Rings
- **Top Ring:** Chromed moly
- **Second Ring:** Cast iron
- **Oil Ring:** Three-piece steel
- **Gap Specifications:** 
  - Top ring: 0.25-0.40 mm
  - Second ring: 0.30-0.45 mm
  - Oil ring: 0.15-0.40 mm

### Lubrication System

#### Oil Pump
- **Type:** Gear-type, rotor design
- **Location:** Bottom of block, driven by crankshaft
- **Capacity:** Standard flow for NA applications
- **Pressure:** 30+ psi at idle, 45-60 psi at 3000 RPM

#### Oil Pan & Crankcase Volume
- **Material:** Aluminum (OBD1: alloy sump; OBD2: standard)
- **Oil change capacity (no filter):** 3.8 L (4.0 US qt, 3.3 imp qt) — Honda TechInfo IT9696
- **Oil change capacity (with filter):** 4.0 L (4.2 US qt, 3.5 imp qt) — Honda TechInfo IT9696
- **Practical fill (manual transmission):** 5.1 US qt (4.8 L) per owner's manual
- **Total crankcase capacity:** 6.2 US qt (5.9 L) maximum — do NOT fill to this level during normal operation
- **Dipstick range:** ~500 mL between lower dot (~4.2–4.5 L) and upper/full dot (~5.1–5.4 L)
- **Torque spec:** 7 ft-lb (9.5 Nm) — aluminum pan, over-torquing causes leaks
- **OBD1 vs OBD2 geometry:** OBD1 alloy sump is shallower/lighter (weight reduction focus); OBD2 standard pan is deeper (~0.2–0.3 L more capacity)
- **Baffle plate / windage tray:** OEM part 11221-P5L-010 ($29.33), stamped steel plate between oil pan and main bearing cap bridge; reduces crank drag and manages air turbulence
- **Windage tray OEM part:** 11221-RAA-A00 ($41.06)
- **Scavenging:** Wet-sump architecture, single gear-type pickup at center of pan, gravity return only
- **Balance shaft seal note:** Seals blow out regardless of baffle plate presence — caused by PCV-induced crankcase pressure, not missing baffling
- **Aftermarket baffled pans:** Moroso Race Baffled Oil Pan (H22/H23) — thick steel core, fabricated extra-capacity sump, OEM pickup compatible, works with OEM windage tray and main girdle (~$350–450); 1320 Performance H2B Baffled Oil Pan (~$250–350)
- **Estimated crankcase volume (below crank centerline):** ~1.8–2.2 L (OBD1) / ~2.0–2.5 L (OBD2) — engineering estimates, not published by Honda
- **Oil distribution at operating condition:** Sump ~2.0–2.5 L, main galleries ~0.3–0.4 L, rod galleries ~0.2–0.3 L, head/VTEC galleries ~0.4–0.5 L, valve train splash ~0.2–0.3 L

## VTEC System Operation

### VTEC Technology Overview

VTEC (Variable Valve Timing and Lift Electronic Control) is Honda's proprietary variable valve timing system. The H22A uses a single-cam VTEC design that switches between two cam profiles at a specific RPM threshold.

### H22A VTEC System Components

#### Solenoid Assembly
- **Location:** Top of cylinder head, near valve cover
- **Type:** Electro-hydraulic solenoid
- **Wiring:** 2 connectors (solenoid + oil pressure sensor)
- **Pinout:** 
  - US ECU: Pin A8 (VTEC Solenoid Valve)
  - JDM ECU: Pin A17
- **Oil Pressure Sensor:** Integrated into solenoid assembly

#### Oil Pressure Switch
- **Function:** Confirms VTEC engagement
- **Location:** Inside VTEC solenoid assembly
- **Activation Pressure:** ~10-15 psi

#### Oil Passages
- **Main Galley:** Feeds VTEC system
- **Internal Passages:** Cylinder head oil galleries
- **Requirement:** Minimum 30 psi oil pressure for VTEC engagement

### VTEC Engagement Parameters

| Parameter | Specification |
|-----------|---------------|
| **RPM Threshold** | ~5,500 RPM (varies by variant) |
| **Oil Pressure** | 30+ psi |
| **Vehicle Speed** | > 5 km/h (safety interlock) |
| **Engine Temperature** | > 40°C (safety interlock) |
| **Throttle Position** | > 20% (load requirement) |

### VTEC Operation Sequence

1. **Below 5,500 RPM:** 
   - Low-lift cam lobes operate intake/exhaust valves
   - Standard valve timing for low-end torque
   - Fuel efficiency optimized

2. **At 5,500 RPM:**
   - ECU activates VTEC solenoid
   - Oil pressure engages VTEC piston
   - High-lift cam lobe takes over
   - Valve lift increases significantly

3. **Above 5,500 RPM:**
   - High-lift cam profile active
   - Increased valve duration and lift
   - Maximum power output achieved
   - Characteristic "VTEC kick" felt

### VTEC Troubleshooting

#### Common Issues
- **VTEC not engaging:**
  - Low oil pressure (< 30 psi)
  - Faulty VTEC solenoid
  - Clogged oil passages
  - Wiring/ECU issues
  - Oil pressure sensor failure

- **VTEC engagement problems:**
  - Dirty solenoid screen
  - Worn solenoid internals
  - Low oil level
  - Wrong oil viscosity

#### Diagnostic Steps
1. Check oil level and condition
2. Verify oil pressure (30+ psi at idle)
3. Test VTEC solenoid resistance (14-30 ohms)
4. Check wiring and grounds
5. Scan for error codes (P1259 - VTEC system malfunction)
6. Inspect solenoid screen for debris

#### Maintenance
- **VTEC Solenoid Gasket:** Replace at every timing belt service
- **Solenoid Screen:** Clean during major service
- **Oil Changes:** Critical for VTEC system longevity
- **Oil Type:** 5W-30 or 10W-30 (Honda Genuine or equivalent)

## Engine Management & ECU

### ECU Variants

#### OBD1 ECUs (1992-1996)
- **P5M:** USDM H22A1 (automatic)
  - 32-pin connector
  - **NOT chip-tunable** — EPROM soldered, missing 74HC373 latch circuitry
  - To tune: swap to P28/P06/P72 + OBD1-to-OBD2 harness if needed
- **P51:** USDM H22A1 (manual)
  - 32-pin connector
  - **NOT chip-tunable** — same hardware as P5M, different basemap calibration
  - Differences from P5M: AT shift logic/torque converter lockup absent; slightly higher rev limiter (~6,500 vs ~6,200 RPM)
- **P28:** JDM Civic Si/Ex (D16Z6) — most common chippable ECU for H22 swaps
  - Socketed 27C256 EPROM (tunable)
  - SOHC VTEC origin, works with H22 DOHC VTEC via wiring
- **P06:** USDM Civic DX (D15B) — budget chippable option
  - Socketed EPROM, non-VTEC origin, requires VTEC conversion for H22
- **P72:** JDM Integra GSR (B18C) — premium chippable option
  - Socketed EPROM, DOHC VTEC, includes knock sensor input
  - Best for boosted/aggressive builds
- **P61, P30, P75, PR4, P05:** Other chippable OBD1 ECUs (see chippable ECU reference table)
- **Characteristics (chippable ECUs):**
  - 32-pin connector
  - Socketed 27C256/27C512 EPROM (tunable with aftermarket chips)
  - Includes 74HC373 latch IC, C51/C52 capacitors, R54 resistor, J1 jumper point
  - Full fuel/ignition map editing via tuning software
  - No knock control on P28/P06; knock control on P72

#### OBD2 ECUs (1997-2001)
- **P13:** USDM H22A4 / 5th-gen Blacktop
  - Immobilizer integrated (1997+); 1996 P13-A12 is non-immobilizer (extremely rare)
  - Hondata S300 NOT compatible; FlashPro has no official H22 P13 support
  - Memory architecture similar to K-series ECUs — different from OBD1 H8/300 family
  - Fuel maps cannot be meaningfully modified even if chip is removable
  - Recommended path for full tuning: convert to P28 (OBD1) via harness converter + TD-60U distributor
- **P28:** JDM variants (OBD2a) — note: P28 is primarily an OBD1 ECU; "OBD2a P28" refers to swap usage
- **P72:** Later JDM variants (OBD2b) — Integra DC2 (B18C); dual butterflies
- **P5P:** OBD2 H22A; immobilizer may block chips
- **Characteristics:**
  - 32-pin + 2-pin connectors
  - Integrated immobilizer (most 1997+ variants)
  - More sophisticated maps
  - Better emissions control
  - Severely limited tuning potential vs OBD1 ECUs

### Blacktop vs Redtop ECU Basemap Differences

While the P28 physical hardware is identical between Blacktop and Redtop applications, the basemap burned into the EPROM differs:

| Parameter | Blacktop Basemap | Redtop Basemap | Reason |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|--------|
| **Fuel maps** | Leaner at mid-range | Richer at high-RPM | Blacktop's single runner + 60mm TB flows less air |
| **Ignition timing** | More aggressive advance | Conservative advance | Blacktop's 10.6:1 CR allows ~3–5° more timing before knock |
| **VTEC engagement** | ~5,200–5,500 RPM | ~5,200–5,500 RPM | Same threshold; different character due to cam profile |
| **IAB control** | Enabled (single runner) | Enabled (dual runner) | Different butterfly control strategy |
| **Knock sensor** | Enabled (disabled for chipped) | Enabled (disabled for chipped) | Same logic; Blacktop less likely to trigger knock |

**Blacktop tuning advantages:** Lower compression (10.6:1 vs 11.0:1) provides ~3–5° more ignition advance margin, making the Blacktop more forgiving of poor fuel quality and slightly more boost-tolerant (~6–8 psi stock bottom end vs Redtop's ~5–7 psi).

### ECU Tuning Considerations

#### Fuel Maps
- **NA:** Lean-out at high RPM can cause detonation
- **Turbo:** Richer under boost for cooling
- **VTEC Crossover:** Critical for smooth transition

#### Ignition Timing
- **NA:** 30-36° BTDC at high RPM
- **Turbo:** 20-28° BTDC (reduced under boost)
- **Knock Control:** Essential for turbo applications

#### Ignition System Architecture
- **OBD1 (1992-1996):** Distributor-based with internal coil (Hitachi or TEC)
  - Distributor part: 30100-P13-006 (also TD-60U, D8033N, 31-17429)
  - Internal coil: max current 5.5–7.5 A, dwell 3.6–4.1 ms, ramp slope 1.7–2.0 A/ms
  - Primary resistance: ~0.07–0.08 Ω; Secondary: 9,700–14,600 Ω (9.7–14.6 kΩ)
  - Connectors: 2-pin rectangular + 8-pin rectangular (OBD1)
  - Trigger: CKP sensor at crank; no cam sensor needed for ignition
- **OBD2 (1997-2001):** Distributorless wasted-spark DIS coil pack
  - NGK replacement: 49046 (HEI-style U1075 equivalent)
  - External coil (TEC): max current 6.75–8.75 A, dwell 3.6–4.1 ms, ramp slope 1.5–2.5 A/ms
  - Coil fires 2 plugs simultaneously (pairs: 1↔4, 2↔3)
  - Trigger: CKP at crank + CMP at cam (CMP used for knock control/fuel trim)
- **Base timing:** 15° ± 2° BTDC (both OBD1 and OBD2)
- **No factory COP:** Honda never implemented coil-on-plug on H-series; Hondata CPR retrofit available for aftermarket individual coils
- **High-RPM dwell limitation:** At 8,200 RPM redline, wasted-spark has ~3.66 ms min dwell — at eCtune specification lower bound (3.6–4.1 ms). COP conversion doubles available dwell time.
- **Spark plug wires:** Spiral-wound alloy conductor, Kevlar core, rated to 80,000 VAC (aftermarket); OEM NGK wire sets available
- **Distributor cap/rotor:** Replace every 60,000 miles (with timing belt service)
- **Common failure:** Distributor O-ring leak → oil on spark plugs; ignition module heat-related shutdown (OBD1)

#### VTEC Crossover
- **Stock:** 5,500 RPM
- **Tunable:** 4,500-6,500 RPM range
- **Consideration:** Match to cam profile and power band

## Common Failures & Reliability

### Known Failure Points

#### Timing Belt Tensioner (Critical)
- **Issue:** Hydraulic auto tensioner failure (1992-1996)
- **Symptoms:** Timing belt slack, noise, potential belt failure
- **Consequence:** If belt fails, interference engine = catastrophic damage
- **Solution:** 
  - Replace with H23 manual tensioner kit
  - KS Tuned aftermarket tensioner
  - Regular inspection and replacement (every 60,000 miles)

#### FRM Cylinder Liners
- **Issue:** FRM sleeves difficult to machine/rebuild
- **Symptoms:** Oil consumption, loss of compression
- **Solution:** 
  - RS Machine sleeves or similar aftermarket
  - Complete engine replacement (often more cost-effective)
  - Preventive: Avoid overheating, proper break-in

#### VTEC Solenoid
- **Issue:** Gasket leaks, solenoid failure
- **Symptoms:** VTEC not engaging, P1259 code, oil leaks
- **Solution:** 
  - Replace solenoid and gasket
  - Clean solenoid screen
  - Check oil pressure

#### Head Gasket
- **Issue:** Failure under high boost or overheating
- **Symptoms:** Coolant loss, oil contamination, compression loss
- **Solution:** 
  - MLS (Multi-Layer Steel) head gasket for boosted applications
  - Proper torque sequence and procedure
  - ARP head studs for high-power builds

#### Oil Consumption
- **Issue:** Some H22 engines consume oil
- **Causes:** 
  - Piston ring wear
  - Valve guide wear
  - PCV system issues
- **Solution:** 
  - Regular oil level checks
  - Quality oil changes (5,000 mile intervals)
  - PCV valve replacement

#### Distributor O-Ring
- **Issue:** Oil leaks from distributor
- **Symptoms:** Oil on spark plugs, oil puddle under engine
- **Solution:** Replace O-ring and gasket

#### Water Pump
- **Issue:** Bearing failure, seal leaks
- **Symptoms:** Coolant leak, noise, overheating
- **Solution:** Replace with quality aftermarket (Aisin, GMB)

| Metric | Value |

## Maintenance Procedures

### Routine Maintenance Schedule

| Interval | Maintenance Items |
|----------|-------------------|
| **Every 5,000 miles** | Oil & filter change, visual inspection |
| **Every 10,000 miles** | Spark plugs, PCV valve, fluid checks |
| **Every 30,000 miles** | Coolant flush, fuel filter, valve adjustment |
| **Every 60,000 miles** | Timing belt, tensioner, water pump, thermostat |
| **Every 100,000 miles** | Major service (all of above + distributor O-ring, etc.) |

### Valve Adjustment

#### Specifications
- **Intake (cold):** 0.008 in (0.20 mm)
- **Exhaust (cold):** 0.010 in (0.25 mm)
- **Intake (hot):** 0.010 in (0.25 mm)
- **Exhaust (hot):** 0.012 in (0.30 mm)

#### Procedure
1. Remove valve cover
2. Bring cylinder #1 to TDC (compression stroke)
3. Adjust valves for cylinder #1 and #3
4. Rotate crank 360° (bring #4 to TDC)
5. Adjust valves for cylinder #2 and #4
6. Recheck all adjustments
7. Replace valve cover gasket

### Timing Belt Replacement

#### Recommended Interval
- **Standard:** 60,000-90,000 miles
- **Performance use:** 60,000 miles
- **Time-based:** Every 5-7 years

#### Components to Replace
- Timing belt
- Tensioner (upgrade to H23 or KS Tuned)
- Water pump
- Idler pulleys
- Valve cover gasket
- Rear main seal (if leaking)

#### Torque Specifications
- **Tensioner bolt:** 33 ft-lb
- **Crank pulley bolt:** 140 ft-lb
- **Cam pulley bolts:** 43 ft-lb

### Oil Change

#### Specifications
- **Capacity:** 4.2 L (4.4 US qt) with filter
- **Type:** 5W-30 or 10W-30 (Honda Genuine or equivalent)
- **Filter:** Honda 15400-PLM-A02 or equivalent (Purolator, Wix, K&N)
- **Drain plug torque:** 29 ft-lb

#### Performance Oil Recommendations
- **Street:** Mobil 1 5W-30, Pennzoil Platinum 5W-30
- **Track:** Motul 300V 10W-40, Red Line 10W-40
- **High boost:** 10W-40 or 15W-50

### Coolant System

#### Specifications
- **Capacity:** 7.0 L (7.4 US qt) total
- **Type:** Honda Longlife Coolant (blue) or equivalent
- **Mix:** 50/50 coolant/water
- **Radiator cap:** 1.1 bar (16 psi)

#### Bleeding Procedure
1. Fill radiator with coolant
2. Run engine with radiator cap off
3. Squeeze upper hose to bleed air
4. Top off as needed
5. Replace cap when thermostat opens
