## Summary

The Honda H22A Blacktop (200 PS @ 7,000 RPM) and Redtop (220 PS @ 7,200 RPM) share the same 2,157 cc DOHC VTEC architecture but are tuned for fundamentally different character profiles. The Redtop is the higher-revving, peakier engine — more aggressive camshafts (+18°/+2° duration, +0.53/+0.58 mm lift), dome pistons for 11.0:1 compression, dual-runner intake, 62.5 mm throttle body, 57 mm exhaust, and light factory head porting. It delivers its power high in the rev range (peak torque at 6,500 RPM, redline 8,200 RPM on Type S) and demands premium fuel (98 RON). The Blacktop is the broader-torque, more tractable engine — milder cams (288°/308°), flat-top/small-dome pistons for 10.6:1 compression, single-runner intake, 60 mm throttle body, 51 mm exhaust, no factory porting. Its torque peaks earlier (5,500 RPM), redline is 7,400 RPM (cutoff ~7,600 RPM), and it runs on regular unleaded (91 RON). The 20 PS difference (~9%) comes almost entirely from airflow improvements and higher-RPM tuning, not from fundamental architectural differences. For daily driving and street use, the Blacktop is arguably the better engine due to its broader torque curve, lower fuel requirement, and easier low-RPM drivability. For track use and enthusiasts who enjoy high-RPM operation, the Redtop offers more peak performance and a more engaging character. Neither is objectively "better" — they serve different use cases. Both benefit enormously from the same aftermarket modifications (cam upgrade + head porting + intake/exhaust upgrades yield similar gains regardless of starting point).

## Power & Torque Curve Character

### How the Numbers Translate to Real-World Feel

#### Low-RPM Range (Idle – 3,000 RPM)

| Aspect | Blacktop | Redtop | Verdict |
|--------|----------|--------|---------|
| Idle smoothness | Smoother, less lumpy | Slightly firmer idle | **Blacktop wins** |
| Low-end torque | Stronger (peaks 1,000 RPM earlier) | Weaker below 5,500 RPM | **Blacktop wins** |
| City driving | Effortless; no need to downshift | Adequate but feels strained below 4,000 RPM | **Blacktop wins** |
| Fuel economy (city) | Better (less throttle effort needed) | Slightly worse (requires more throttle for same acceleration) | **Blacktop wins** |

The Blacktop's shorter-duration cam profile (288°/308° vs 306°/310°) means better cylinder scavenging at low RPM. This translates to noticeably stronger pull away from stops, smoother cruising, and less need to rev the engine for passing maneuvers in city traffic. The Redtop's longer-duration cams trade low-RPM torque for high-RPM airflow — a classic performance-engineering compromise.

#### Mid-RPM Range (3,000 – 5,500 RPM)

| Aspect | Blacktop | Redtop | Verdict |
|--------|----------|--------|---------|
| Power build | Linear and predictable | Gradual; building toward VTEC | **Even** |
| VTEC approach | Noticeable surge at ~5,500 RPM | Noticeable surge at ~5,200–5,500 RPM | **Slight edge Redtop** (earlier engagement) |
| Passing power | Adequate but needs downshift for max acceleration | Adequate but needs downshift for max acceleration | **Even** |
| Driveability | Smooth, linear throttle response | Slightly more abrupt as VTEC approaches | **Blacktop wins** |

Both engines feel similar in this range. The key difference is that the Blacktop is already near its torque peak while the Redtop is still climbing. Under hard acceleration, the Redtop's VTEC engagement (at slightly lower RPM) provides a more dramatic power surge.

#### High-RPM Range (5,500 – Redline)

| Aspect | Blacktop | Redtop | Verdict |
|--------|----------|--------|---------|
| Peak power | 200 PS @ 6,800–7,000 RPM | 220 PS @ 7,200 RPM | **Redtop wins decisively** |
| Power above VTEC | Sustained but tapering after 7,000 RPM | Continues climbing to 7,200+ RPM | **Redtop wins** |
| VTEC character | Deeper/louder exhaust tone change | Raspy, aggressive exhaust note | **Redtop wins** (more engaging) |
| Redline margin | 7,400 RPM (cutoff ~7,600 RPM) | 7,200–8,200 RPM (Type S cutoff ~9,100 RPM) | **Redtop wins** (wider usable band) |
| Track suitability | Adequate for street-driven track days | Superior for sustained high-RPM operation | **Redtop wins** |

This is where the Redtop earns its reputation. The +20 PS comes entirely from high-RPM improvements: larger throttle body, better exhaust, improved intake, and more aggressive cams. Above 6,500 RPM, the Redtop pulls noticeably harder. The Type S variant (with 8,200 RPM redline) extends this advantage even further.

## Which Is Better? — Use-Case Analysis

### Daily Driving / Commuting

**Winner: Blacktop**

The Blacktop is the clear choice for daily driving. Its broader torque curve means less revving for normal acceleration, smoother idle, better low-RPM fuel economy, and the ability to run on regular unleaded fuel (91 RON vs 98 RON). In stop-and-go traffic, the Blacktop feels more relaxed and less demanding. The Redtop's firmer idle and narrower powerband make it feel more strained at low speeds.

### Street Performance / Enthusiast Driving

**Winner: Redtop (for most drivers), Blacktop (for budget-conscious builders)**

For drivers who enjoy spirited driving and want to use the full rev range, the Redtop is the more engaging engine. The higher-revving character, more aggressive exhaust note, and sharper VTEC kick provide a more exciting driving experience. However, a well-modified Blacktop can close much of this gap — cam upgrade + head work + intake/exhaust upgrades yield similar results on both engines. As mywikimotors notes: "It doesn't matter, you will still have to replace camshafts and do the cylinder head porting, the result will be the same."

### Track Days / Circuit Use

**Winner: Redtop**

The Redtop's higher-revving nature, better cooling (larger exhaust, improved intake), and factory head porting give it an edge on track. The Type S variant's 8,200 RPM redline provides a wider powerband for sustained high-speed cornering. The Blacktop is still very capable for street-driven track days, but the Redtop's high-RPM advantage becomes more pronounced during repeated high-speed laps.

### Drag Racing

**Winner: Blacktop (stock), Redtop (modified)**

Stock, the Blacktop's broader low-end torque gives it better launches and stronger 60-foot times. The Redtop's power advantage only materializes above 5,500 RPM, which may not be reached in a quarter-mile run with stock gearing. However, once modified (cams + head work), the Redtop's higher peak power gives it an advantage in trap speed.

### Autocross / Time Attack

**Winner: Blacktop**

Autocross favors broad, linear power delivery over peak horsepower. The Blacktop's torque curve is more usable through the varied RPM ranges encountered in autocross courses. Combined with better low-RPM driveability (important for navigating slow transitions), the Blacktop is the more practical choice. The Redtop's narrow powerband means more gear changes and more time spent searching for power.

### Forced Induction (Turbo/Supercharger) Build

**Winner: Tie (with caveats)**

For a forced induction build, the base engine architecture matters more than the NA tuning. Both engines share the same block, bottom end, and valvetrain architecture. The Blacktop's lower compression ratio (10.6:1 vs 11.0:1) actually provides a slight advantage for boost tolerance, allowing more timing advance before knock. However, the Redtop's superior induction and exhaust architecture provides a higher ceiling for naturally aspirated power if the turbo build stays conservative. Most builders report no meaningful difference in forced-induction potential between the two.

### Budget Build / Value Proposition

**Winner: Blacktop**

The Blacktop offers better value for money:
- Lower used engine prices (less collector demand than Redtop)
- Runs on regular fuel (cheaper to operate)
- Lower insurance costs (less exotic perception)
- Same aftermarket support (camshafts, head work, intakes are identical upgrades)
- Easier to live with daily (smoother, more tractable)

### Key Insight

The critical finding from community sources is that **both engines converge to similar power levels after modification**. The mywikimotors article states plainly: "Let's decide what motor is best for starting tuning – H22A Redtop or H22A Blacktop. It doesn't matter, you will still have to replace camshafts and do the cylinder head porting, the result will be the same." Once both engines receive camshafts and head work, the remaining ~5–10 HP difference comes from the Redtop's superior induction/exhaust architecture — a smaller gap than the stock 20 PS suggests.

## Reliability Comparison

| Aspect | Blacktop | Redtop | Notes |
|--------|----------|--------|-------|
| **FRM liner oil consumption** | Same mechanism | Same mechanism | Identical block design |
| **Timing belt tensioner** | Same hydraulic auto (OBD1) | Same hydraulic auto (OBD1) | Critical failure mode on both |
| **Valve train wear** | Lower (milder cams, lower lift) | Higher (aggressive cams, higher lift) | Redtop may need valve adjustment sooner |
| **Knock/detonation risk** | Lower (10.6:1 CR, regular fuel) | Higher (11.0:1 CR, requires premium) | Blacktop more forgiving on fuel quality |
| **VTEC solenoid stress** | Lower (slightly later engagement) | Higher (slightly earlier engagement) | Negligible practical difference |
| **Thermal load** | Lower (lower CR, less aggressive timing) | Higher (higher CR, retarded timing) | Redtop runs hotter under load |
| **Long-term durability** | Slightly better | Slightly worse | Marginal difference; maintenance matters more |

Both engines share the same fundamental reliability concerns (FRM liner degradation, timing belt tensioner failure). The Redtop's higher compression and more aggressive tuning introduce slightly more thermal and mechanical stress, but these differences are marginal compared to the impact of proper maintenance. A well-maintained Redtop is just as reliable as a well-maintained Blacktop over the long term.

## Conflicts and Disputes

1. **Mywikimotors power figures discrepancy.** mywikimotors lists Blacktop at both 190 HP and 200 HP. Working assumption: 190 HP refers to the Accord SiR variant (H22A1-spec calibration), 200 HP refers to the Prelude SiR variant. This aligns with the existing h22a-blacktop-specifications.md research.

2. **VTEC engagement RPM varies 5,200–5,800 RPM across sources.** This reflects the load-dependent VTEC window, not a specification dispute. Engagement depends on oil temperature, throttle position, vehicle speed, and ECU calibration. Both engines share essentially the same VTEC calibration.

3. **"Which is better?" is inherently subjective.** Community forums consistently split along use-case lines: daily drivers prefer the Blacktop, track enthusiasts prefer the Redtop. There is no objective answer — only answers specific to a given use case.

4. **Honda-Tech dyno thread (T-161 source) is behind login wall.** The thread title "Euro R/Type S H22A Red Top VS. JDM H22A Black Top (discussion dyno's inside)" contains actual dyno comparisons but cannot be accessed without registration. The existence of such dyno data supports the consensus view that the ~20 PS gap holds up on the rollers, but specific numbers cannot be cited.

5. **Piston material ambiguity.** QWEN.md states "forged aluminum (aftermarket) / cast (stock)" while mywikimotors does not specify. Stock Redtop pistons were likely cast aluminum with a domed crown; forged pistons are aftermarket upgrades. This does not affect the comparison since both engines use cast pistons at the factory.

## Verdict

**There is no single "better" engine.** The Blacktop and Redtop are two expressions of the same architecture, optimized for different priorities:

| Priority | Recommended Engine |
|----------|-------------------|
| Daily driving / commuting | **Blacktop** — broader torque, regular fuel, smoother |
| Budget build | **Blacktop** — cheaper engine, cheaper fuel, same mod potential |
| Track days (street-driven) | **Blacktop** — adequate power, better driveability to/from track |
| Enthusiast driving / high-RPM enjoyment | **Redtop** — more peak power, more engaging character |
| Track days (dedicated) | **Redtop** — higher revving, better sustained performance |
| Drag racing (stock) | **Blacktop** — better low-end launch |
| Drag racing (modified) | **Redtop** — higher trap speed potential |
| Forced induction build | **Tie** — same block, Blacktop has slight boost tolerance edge |
| Collector / investment | **Redtop** — higher demand, stronger appreciation |
| First H-series engine | **Blacktop** — more forgiving, easier to learn on |

The 20 PS difference represents approximately 9% of the Blacktop's output — significant but not transformative. For most real-world driving scenarios, the Blacktop's broader torque curve and lower running costs make it the more practical choice. For enthusiasts who prioritize peak performance and driving engagement, the Redtop justifies its premium.
