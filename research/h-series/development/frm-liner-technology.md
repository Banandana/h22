## Summary

FRM (Fiber-Reinforced Metal), technically classified as a Metal Matrix Composite (MMC), is a cylinder liner technology pioneered by Honda and introduced in 1990 on the B21A1 engine in the third-generation Prelude Si. It replaces traditional cast iron cylinder liners with a thin (~0.5 mm) composite layer consisting of Saffil alumina fibers (95–97% Al₂O₃), carbon fibers, and an aluminum alloy matrix. The technology was developed in collaboration with Saffil Ltd. (a UK-based Pilkington subsidiary) and enabled Honda to eliminate cast iron liners entirely, reducing block weight by approximately 4.5 kg, tightening bore spacing, and allowing more compact block designs. FRM became one of Honda's most distinctive materials innovations of the 1990s, appearing in the B21A1, H22A, H23A, F20C, F22C, C30A, and C32A engines. While the liner itself is extraordinarily durable — often surviving catastrophic bottom-end failures without scoring — its extreme hardness causes accelerated piston ring wear, leading to widespread oil consumption across all FRM-equipped Honda engines. Aftermarket solutions for rebuilding FRM blocks include dry cast-iron sleeves from RS Machine, Darton, and Golden Eagle Manufacturing.

## Development History

### Origins: Saffil Fibers and MMC Research

Saffil Ltd., based in Widnes, Cheshire, UK (a subsidiary of Pilkington Group), has been actively involved in the development of metal matrix composite applications since 1980, producing grades of Saffil fiber tailored to meet the stringent technical requirements of reinforced aluminum components such as pistons and engine blocks.[1][2]

SAFFIL fibers contain 95 to 97 percent alumina, produced by a novel solution spinning process from the highest purity raw materials and an aqueous medium. The fiber diameter is strictly controlled with a median of approximately 3 microns and very low levels of fiber less than 1 micron in diameter. The fibers are subjected to a controlled heat treatment to develop a polycrystalline microstructure.[2][3]

In the broader MMC landscape, Toyota pioneered the first commercially produced MMC diesel piston in the early 1980s using squeeze infiltration in a ceramic preform. Kolben Schmidt, Mahle, AE, and Toyota also pioneered the use of Saffil fiber-reinforced pistons for diesel engines throughout the 1980s and early 1990s. Meanwhile, Honda Motor Company focused its efforts on the reinforcement of engine blocks rather than individual components.[4][5]

### Honda's Introduction: 1990 Prelude Si

In 1990, Honda launched a new generation of aluminum engine blocks with fiber-reinforced cylinder walls replacing traditional cast iron liners. The first model selected for production was the Prelude Si, a 16-valve inline-4-cylinder engine (the B21A1), using a new casting process to incorporate the Saffil-carbon fiber hybrid preforms.[4][5][6]

This was a landmark achievement in automotive materials science. The serial production of cylinder barrels made of aluminum MMC started in 1990 and was used for the first time on the 2.3-liter B21A1 engine of the Honda Prelude.[6]

Honda subsequently expanded the use of MMC engine blocks to include models of the Accord, Ascot Innova, and the S2000. Honda also developed a high-pressure die casting (HPDC) process for manufacturing the MMC engine blocks, which reduced process costs and enabled widely available equipment to be employed.[4][5]

## Composition and Manufacturing

### Material Composition

FRM is a Metal Matrix Composite consisting of three primary components:[1][7][8]

| Component | Role | Description |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **Saffil fibers** | Reinforcement | 95–97% alumina (Al₂O₃) polycrystalline fibers, ~3 micron diameter |
| **Carbon fibers** | Secondary reinforcement | Hybrid preform with Saffil fibers |
| **Aluminum alloy matrix** | Binder | Standard piston-grade aluminum alloy |

The composite forms a thin (~0.5 mm) layer on the cylinder bore surface. In the NSX C32B engine specifically, Honda documentation describes it as "an 0.5-mm-thick layer with fibers of carbon and alumina (aluminum oxide, or Al₂O₃) in the aluminum alloy."[7]

### Manufacturing Process

The Saffil-carbon fiber hybrid preforms are incorporated into the cylinder casting during the molding phase. The process works as follows:[4][5]

1. **Preform placement:** Saffil-carbon fiber hybrid preforms are positioned within the mold at the cylinder bore locations
2. **Pouring:** Molten aluminum alloy is poured around the preforms
3. **Absorption:** The aluminum cores absorb molten aluminum during casting, creating an integral bond between the composite layer and the block
4. **Boring:** After casting, the cylinders are bored to a slightly smaller diameter than the preforms, leaving a tough, wear-resistant composite cylinder wall integral with the block but reinforced by the fibers
5. **Honing:** Final surface finish applied via honing (this step is critical — see Limitations section)

Honda later developed a high-pressure die casting (HPDC) process for manufacturing MMC blocks, which reduced process costs and enabled the use of widely available equipment.[4][5]

The NSX C32B process description notes: "The cores absorb the molten aluminum during the casting. After casting, the cylinders are bored to a slightly smaller diameter than the cores, leaving a tough, wear-resistant, composite cylinder wall integral with the block but reinforced by the fibers."[7]

### Why Honda Chose FRM

The elimination of cast iron liners using MMC technology provided several engineering advantages:[4][5][7]

- **Weight reduction:** Approximately 4.5 kg saved on the block alone
- **Compact design:** Tighter cylinder spacing allowed reduction in overall engine length
- **Larger bores:** The fiber-reinforced structure allowed larger bore diameters within the same external block dimensions
- **Open-deck construction:** The reinforced walls made open-deck block construction viable for high-performance applications
- **Thermal performance:** Better heat transfer characteristics compared to cast iron liners

| Property | FRM MMC | Unreinforced Alloy | Improvement |
|----------|---------|--------------------|-------------|

| Maximum use temperature | ~500°C | Lower | Extended to ~500°C peak temperature[4] |
| Modulus (stiffness) | Higher than theory predicts | Baseline | Exceeds theoretical predictions for random planar fiber array[4] |

### Thermal Properties

| Property | FRM MMC | Niresist Iron (traditional alternative) |
|----------|---------|----------------------------------------|
| Thermal conductivity | Lower than unreinforced metal; decreases as fiber level rises[4] | Baseline reference |
| Conductivity directionality | Higher parallel to fiber orientation planes than normal direction[4] | Isotropic |

### Wear Resistance

The wear resistance of the alloy is greatly improved by including only a small volume fraction of fiber (as little as 4.4% v/v of RG grade Saffil fiber).[4] However, the mating component (in this case, chromium-steel piston rings) may experience increased wear, which can be mitigated by using RG grade fiber with the appropriate alpha alumina level.[4]

## Benefits

### Advantages of FRM Over Cast Iron Liners

1. **Weight reduction:** ~4.5 kg per block by eliminating cast iron liners and allowing tighter bore spacing.[4][5]
2. **Compact engine architecture:** Elimination of cast iron liner using MMC technology allows reduction in material thickness between adjacent bores. Tightening cylinder spacing results in reduction in overall engine length.[4][5]
3. **Superior high-temperature strength:** At 300°C, ultimate tensile strength improves by a factor of 4 over the unreinforced alloy. Maximum use limit extends to ~500°C.[4]
4. **Excellent wear resistance:** Greatly improved by small fiber additions (4.4% v/v minimum). The liner itself is extremely durable — multiple forum sources report cross-hatch marks still visible after 200,000+ miles even after catastrophic rod failure.[9][10]
5. **Lower coefficient of thermal expansion:** Reduces thermal distortion under extreme operating conditions.[4]
6. **Higher modulus (stiffness):** At 300°C, composites with 18–24% fiber have higher modulus than the unreinforced alloy at 25°C.[4]
7. **No Nikasil-style sulfur sensitivity:** Unlike German Nickel-Silicon (Nikasil/Alusil) liners used by BMW and Jaguar, FRM does not react adversely to sulfur in low-quality fuel.[7]
8. **Structural rigidity:** The fiber matrix creates an ultra-rigid wall allowing larger bores and lighter castings, making open-deck construction viable for high-performance engines.[7]
9. **Integral bonding:** The FRM layer is cast integral with the block — no separate sleeves to sink, crack, or oscillate (a problem with some aftermarket dry sleeve installations).[11]

### Evidence of Durability

Forum members who have inspected FRM blocks after catastrophic failures consistently report that the cylinder walls remain undamaged even when connecting rods snap and blow holes through the block skirt below the cylinder wall. One owner reported: "my cyl walls were not scratched up or anything...still could see the cross hatch after 200k miles" after a rod snapped.[9] Another noted: "when rods snap in the b21, they usually break the block below the sleeves...you can still see cross-hatch marks after 250k miles." The FRM liner itself is described as "basically indestructible" by experienced builders.[7][10]

## Limitations

### The Oil Consumption Problem

The primary and most well-documented limitation of FRM technology is its effect on piston ring longevity:[1][8][9][12]

> "Because of the material's strength and composition, it's easy for oil to pass by the piston rings, up into the combustion chamber where it has nothing left to do but burn and cause you headaches. Besides the smoking stench of billowing oil from the tailpipe, the process can also cause premature piston ring wear — never a good thing when performance is a concern."[1]

The mechanism is straightforward: the FRM liner is so hard that it does not wear appreciably, but the mating piston rings (typically chromed moly or cast iron) wear relatively quickly against it. As the rings wear, they lose their ability to seal effectively, allowing oil to pass into the combustion chamber. This is not a flaw in the liner itself — the liner survives — but it is a fundamental incompatibility between the liner material and conventional ring materials.

Multiple owners confirm: "Replace the rings and you will still burn oil. It is the FRM that is the problem. Anyone who has looked at the scoring present in a B21 block can attest to this."[9] The consensus among experienced builders is that the rings fail, not the sleeves — but replacing rings alone does not solve the underlying issue because the new rings will wear against the FRM at the same accelerated rate.[9][10]

### Honing and Machining Challenges

FRM is notoriously difficult to machine and hone properly:[1][8][12]

- **Specialized equipment required:** Standard honing tools and techniques designed for cast iron or Nikasil liners do not work correctly on FRM
- **Factory honing issues:** Some sources claim early blocks suffered from improper factory honing that exacerbated oil consumption.[13] One forum member cited a Honda TSB suggesting blocks made after June 1991 had improved honing, though other members dispute this, noting that H23A and later H22A blocks exhibited the same oil consumption problem.[13][14]
- **Cannot be overbored conventionally:** The extreme hardness of FRM makes standard overbore procedures impractical. There is no reboring limit listed in Service Manuals for the C32B FRM liners, unlike the iron liners on the earlier C30A — suggesting Honda considered the FRM layer non-serviceable by conventional means.[7]
- **Requires complete resleeving:** When FRM blocks need rebuilding, the standard approach is to install new dry cast-iron sleeves rather than attempt to re-hone the existing FRM surface.[1][8][12]

### Thermal Conductivity Trade-off

While FRM has excellent mechanical properties at elevated temperatures, its thermal conductivity is lower than that of the unreinforced aluminum alloy and decreases as the fiber level rises.[4] This is a deliberate trade-off: the fiber reinforcement improves mechanical strength and wear resistance at the cost of some heat transfer capability. In practice, this was not a significant issue for naturally aspirated Honda engines but could become relevant in boosted applications.

### Cost

While Honda developed HPDC processes to reduce manufacturing costs, MMC blocks are still more expensive to produce than conventional cast-iron-lined aluminum blocks. The Saffil fiber supply chain adds complexity and cost.[4][5]

## Aftermarket Solutions

### Resleeving Options for FRM Blocks

When FRM blocks require rebuilding, the standard approach is to replace the FRM liner with a conventional cast-iron sleeve. Several companies offer this service:[1][8][11][15]

#### RS Machine

RS Machine is the most commonly referenced aftermarket sleeve provider for Honda FRM blocks. They produce dry cast-iron sleeves specifically for H22/H23 applications.[1][8]

#### Darton Sleeves

Darton International (incorporated 1978, Portugal-based manufacturing) offers dry block sleeve kits for Honda H22/H23 applications. Their sleeves are made from centrifugally cast nodular iron, providing unexcelled surface toughness. Darton pioneered modular sleeve designs and specialty ductile iron material beginning with top fuel sleeves in 1978.[15][16]

Golden Eagle Mfg produces custom sleeves for Honda H22/H23 blocks, marketing them as "actually a whole new cylinder" rather than a traditional sleeve. They emphasize that their sleeves are manufactured in-house from raw material to control quality from start to finish. Basic sleeve turnaround time is typically 6–8 weeks.[11]

### Important Considerations for Resleeving

- **FRM cannot be machined conventionally:** Attempting to hone or bore an FRM surface with standard tools is ineffective and potentially dangerous to equipment.[1][8]
- **Complete replacement is standard:** The industry consensus is that when FRM blocks need rebuilding, full sleeve replacement is the correct approach, not attempted re-honing.[1][8][12]
- **Ring material matters:** Even with new sleeves, ring selection should account for the original FRM surface if the block is not being sleeved. Chromoly rings have been discussed in forums but are not a widely adopted solution.[17]

## Legacy and Impact

### Influence on Honda Engineering

FRM technology became a hallmark of Honda's materials science leadership in the 1990s. Its deployment across multiple engine families — from the B21A1 Prelude to the F20C S2000 and the C30A/C32B NSX — demonstrated Honda's commitment to lightweight, high-performance engineering. The technology allowed Honda to achieve specific outputs approaching 100 hp/liter while maintaining structural integrity and reliability.

### End of FRM in Production

By the mid-2000s, Honda had largely moved away from FRM liners in new production engines. The K-series engines (K20, K24) introduced in the early 2000s use traditional cast-iron or modified iron-lined aluminum blocks. The reasons for this transition are not explicitly documented by Honda but likely include:

1. **Cost reduction:** FRM production, even with HPDC, remained more complex and expensive than conventional lining methods
2. **Aftermarket compatibility:** The FRM oil consumption problem and difficulty of rebuilding created negative perceptions among enthusiasts and builders
3. **Alternative technologies:** Improvements in cast-iron liner processes and coatings provided adequate performance at lower cost
4. **Emissions regulations:** Stricter emissions standards may have favored different engine architectures

### Community Knowledge

FRM technology generated extensive discussion in enthusiast communities, particularly on PreludePower.com, Honda-Tech, NSXPrime, and other Honda-focused forums. The combination of extraordinary durability (surviving catastrophic failures) and persistent oil consumption created a unique reputation: FRM blocks are simultaneously praised for their indestructibility and criticized for their tendency to burn oil. This duality has made FRM one of the most discussed materials technologies in Honda engineering history.[9][10][12][17]
