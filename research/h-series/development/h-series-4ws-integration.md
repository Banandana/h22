# Honda H-Series and Four-Wheel Steering (4WS) Integration

## Summary

Research on why Honda paired the H-series engine with four-wheel steering in the Prelude, the evolution from mechanical to electronic 4WS across generations, and the documented performance impact of this combination. The Prelude served as Honda's technology showcase, and 4WS was a defining feature that distinguished it from every other Honda model.

---

## 1. Why the H-Series Was Paired with 4WS

### The Prelude as Honda's Technology Showcase

The Prelude was always positioned as Honda's most technologically advanced production car short of the NSX. From its inception, the Prelude carried features that would later trickle down to the Accord and Civic:

- First Honda with a power moonroof
- First Honda with four-wheel steering (3rd gen, 1987)
- First Honda with VTEC in a non-NSX application (4th gen, 1991)
- First Honda with anti-lock brakes (Japan-exclusive "ALB" option)
- First Honda with active torque vectoring (5th gen SH, 1997)

When the H-series replaced the B-series in the 4th-gen Prelude (BB1, September 1991), the pairing was natural: the H-series represented Honda's new DOHC VTEC performance philosophy, and 4WS represented Honda's handling innovation. Together, they created a complete performance package — power + agility — that no other Honda offered.

### Weight Distribution and Handling Synergy

The 4th-gen Prelude featured a 58% front / 42% rear weight distribution, which is unusually balanced for a front-wheel-drive car. This distribution complemented the 4WS system:

- The H-series engine, mounted transversely, sat relatively far back in the engine bay compared to B-series applications, contributing to the near 50/50 balance
- 4WS reduced the effective wheelbase during cornering, making the car feel shorter and more agile than its 2,665 mm (105 in) wheelbase suggested
- The combination meant the H-series' high-RPM power delivery could be put down effectively through corners without the understeer typical of FWD cars

The 4th-gen Prelude weighed approximately 2,800 lbs (1,270 kg) in Si 4WS trim — about 600 lbs heavier than the 3rd-gen — but the 4WS system compensated for the added mass by improving transient response.

## 2. Third-Generation Mechanical 4WS (1987–1991) — Pre-H-Series Context

Although the 3rd-gen Prelude used B-series engines (B20A/B21A), understanding its mechanical 4WS is essential context for the H-series integration, as the 4th-gen upgraded this system rather than replacing it.

### System Architecture

Honda's 3rd-gen 4WS was the **world's first steering-angle-sensing four-wheel steering system in a mass-production passenger car**, announced October 1986 and launched with the 1988 Prelude Si. Development began in 1977 as part of Honda's participation in the NHTSA Experimental Safety Vehicle (ESV) program, focused on active safety and collision avoidance.

**Key design decisions:**

- **Steering-angle sensing vs. speed sensing:** Honda initially considered a speed-linked system (rear wheels turn same-direction at high speed, opposite at low speed). However, this was deemed too complex for marketability. Instead, Honda adopted a simpler steering-angle-sensing approach: small steering angles = same-direction rear steer (high-speed stability), large steering angles = opposite-direction rear steer (low-speed maneuverability). Under normal driving, steering angle is small; parking/U-turns require large angles at low speed.
- **Double-crank mechanism:** A single-crank mechanism would have produced equal steering angles in both phases. Honda solved this with a double-crank system combining two crank mechanisms, where small steering angles resulted in small in-phase rear-wheel steering and large steering angles resulted in large reverse-phase turning.
- **Mechanical linkage:** Two steering gearboxes (front and rear) mechanically connected by a center shaft under the floor. No electronics involved in the steering actuation itself.

### Technical Specifications (3rd Gen Mechanical)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Lock-to-lock steering wheel turns | 2.5 turns (900 degrees total, 450 from center) |
| Front wheel max angle | ~35 degrees |
| Transition point (in-phase to reverse-phase) | 230 degrees steering wheel angle (~20 degrees front wheel) |
| Max same-direction rear steer | 1.5 degrees |
| Max reverse-direction rear steer | 5.3 degrees |
| Turning radius (2WS) | 5.3 m |
| Turning radius (4WS) | 4.8 m (0.5 m reduction) |
| Turning circle (2WS) | 34.8 feet |
| Turning circle (4WS) | 31.4 feet |
| System weight addition | 28 lbs (13 kg) |
| System cost (1988 USD) | ~$1,300–$1,500 |

### The 80% Adoption Rate

Honda had expected only 30% of buyers to opt for 4WS, but the feature was a runaway success in Japan, with **80% of buyers opting for it in the first year**. This extraordinary adoption rate validated Honda's engineering investment and justified carrying the system forward into the 4th-gen with the H-series.

In North America, 4WS was available but less popular due to different driving conditions (wider roads, less dense parking) and higher price sensitivity. The 4WS option added $2,320 to a 4th-gen Si's price tag (1992 USD).

### Performance Testing (Car and Driver 1988)

Car and Driver conducted a rigorous A/B comparison of identical 2WS and 4WS Prelude Si models in December 1987:

**Dry Performance Results:**

| Test | 2WS Speed | 4WS Speed | Winner |
|------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| 300-ft skidpad | 0.77 g | 0.78 g | 4WS (marginal) |
| 25-ft cone slalom | 12.5 mph | 12.0 mph | 2WS (power steering limitation) |
| 50-ft cone slalom | 31.5 mph | 31.5 mph | Tie |
| 100-ft cone slalom | 62.0 mph | 62.0 mph | Tie (4WS more controllable) |
| Low-speed lane change | 30.5 mph | 34.0 mph | 4WS (+3.5 mph) |
| High-speed lane change | 57.5 mph | 61.0 mph | 4WS (+3.5 mph) |

**Key findings:**
- The 4WS Prelude was **both faster and more controllable** in lane-change maneuvers
- Slalom speeds were equal, but the 4WS car was "more controllable" with less tail swinging
- The 4WS system required slightly more steering effort but felt "stickier"
- On wet pavement, results were mixed — the 4WS car actually performed worse in several wet tests, though it felt more secure at the limit

**Conclusion (Csaba Csere, C&D Technical Editor):** "The 4ws Prelude was both faster and more controllable than the 2ws model... Indeed, the speed difference was so large that driving the 4ws Prelude through the double lane change at the 2ws car's limits was virtually effortless."

However, Csere also noted: "most of us found steering effort to be the most apparent difference... our drivers unanimously preferred the 2ws Prelude" in blind testing, suggesting the benefits were subtle enough that many drivers couldn't distinguish the cars without knowing which was which.

## 3. Fourth-Generation Electronic 4WS (1991–1996) — H-Series Integration

### System Evolution

The 4th-gen Prelude (BB1, launched April 1991 in Japan) replaced the purely mechanical 3rd-gen system with an **electronically controlled 4WS system**. This was a fundamental architectural shift:

**3rd-gen (mechanical):**
- Front rack-and-pinion ↔ center shaft ↔ rear steering gearbox
- Purely mechanical double-crank linkage
- No ECU involvement in steering actuation
- Rear steering determined entirely by steering wheel angle

**4th-gen (electronic):**
- Front rack-and-pinion ↔ **steering angle sensors** ↔ **4WS ECU** ↔ **rear steering actuator motor** ↔ rear tie rods
- Electronic signal processing with vehicle speed input
- 4WS control unit determines optimal rear wheel angle based on steering angle AND vehicle speed
- Fail-safe mode: rear wheels center themselves on system failure

### Electronic 4WS Component Architecture

| Component | Function | Location |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Front main steering angle sensor | Measures steering wheel rotation angle | Steering column |
| Front sub steering angle sensor | Measures rack position | Front steering gear |
| Rear main steering angle sensor | Measures ball screw rotation in rear actuator | Rear steering actuator |
| Rear sub steering angle sensor | Measures rack movement in rear actuator | Rear steering actuator |
| 4WS control unit | Processes sensor inputs, calculates target rear angle | Under center console |
| Rear steering actuator | Electric motor moves rack to achieve target angle | Rear suspension |
| Vehicle speed sensor | Provides speed data from transaxle | Transaxle |
| Damper relay | Controls return speed in fail-safe mode | 4WS control unit |

### How It Works

The 4WS control unit receives inputs from:
1. **Front main steering angle sensor** — how far the driver has turned the wheel
2. **Front sub steering angle sensor** — actual rack position (redundancy/check)
3. **Vehicle speed sensor** — current road speed

The control unit compares the sensed steering angle against a targeted rear-wheel angle map stored in its memory, then commands the rear steering actuator motor to move the rack to achieve the calculated angle.

**Operating modes:**
- **Low speed:** Rear wheels steer opposite to front wheels (counter-steer), reducing turning radius
- **High speed:** Rear wheels steer same direction as front wheels (in-phase), improving lane-change stability and transient response
- **Fail-safe:** On system failure, integral spring centering + damper relay slowly centers rear wheels. 4WS indicator light illuminates. Rear wheels remain centered until repaired.

### Troubles Codes (DTCs)

The 4WS control unit contains two processing units (main and sub), each storing up to 10 trouble codes:

| Code | Meaning | Indicator Light |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| 12–69 | System faults | ON after 5 min detection |
| 70–74 | Abnormal/harsh driving conditions | Not illuminated (stored only) |

Diagnostic procedure: Connect jumper wire to dual-terminal service check connector behind center console, turn ignition ON, read flash codes from 4WS indicator light. Three longer flashes + one quick flash = code 31.

### H-Series Specific Integration

The H-series engine's characteristics complemented the electronic 4WS system in several ways:

1. **Power delivery and agility:** The H-series' broad torque curve (peak torque at 5,250–6,500 RPM depending on variant) meant the car could maintain momentum through corners, while 4WS improved turn-in response to match the power delivery.

2. **Weight considerations:** The H-series long block weighed approximately 385 lbs. The 4WS system added roughly 28 lbs (similar to 3rd gen). Total system weight remained manageable for the BB1's chassis.

3. **VTEC engagement synergy:** When VTEC kicked in around 5,500 RPM, the sudden power increase was matched by the 4WS system's high-speed in-phase steering mode, creating a coordinated experience where power and agility increased simultaneously at high RPM/high speed.

4. **58/42 weight distribution:** The H-series' transverse mounting position contributed to the BB1's near 50/50 weight distribution, which is ideal for 4WS effectiveness. The balanced distribution means both axles contribute equally to cornering forces, and 4WS can optimize both ends simultaneously.

## 4. Fifth-Generation Electronic 4WS (1997–2001) — H22A4 Era

### Continuation and Refinement

The 5th-gen Prelude (BB6, 1997) continued the electronic 4WS system introduced in the 4th gen. The H22A4 engine (200 PS, open deck block) was paired with the same basic 4WS architecture, with refinements:

- **Continued electronic control** with updated 4WS ECU calibration
- **Available on SiR-S (4WS) models** in Japan and select export markets
- **Dropped from North America mid-generation** due to lack of customer interest (Hagerty)
- **Remained available through end of production** in Japan and some European markets

By the 5th gen, 4WS had become less of a selling point and more of a niche enthusiast feature. The rise of more affordable sports cars and changing consumer preferences reduced demand for the technology.

## 5. Performance Impact Analysis

### Quantified Benefits

Based on Car and Driver's 1988 testing and Honda's own specifications:

**Low-Speed Maneuverability:**
- Turning radius reduced from 5.3 m to 4.8 m (3rd gen) / 34.8 ft to 31.4 ft (US measurement)
- 0.5 m / 3.4 ft reduction — significant in tight urban parking and narrow Japanese roads
- Lane change at low speed: 4WS achieved 34.0 mph vs 30.5 mph for 2WS (+11.5%)

**High-Speed Stability:**
- High-speed lane change: 4WS achieved 61.0 mph vs 57.5 mph for 2WS (+6.1%)
- Reduced fish-tailing and tail wagging during transient maneuvers
- All four tires develop cornering forces simultaneously, reducing yaw moment

**Handling Characteristics:**
- More responsive steering in transient maneuvers (entering/exiting corners)
- Reduced understeer tendency during lane changes
- Improved stability on slippery surfaces (though C&D found mixed wet results)
- Better controllability at equal speeds through slalom courses

### Limitations and Criticisms

Despite the engineering sophistication, 4WS had notable drawbacks:

1. **Cost:** Added $1,300–$2,320 to vehicle price (1988–1992 USD), roughly 8–12% of base car cost
2. **Weight:** 28 lbs (13 kg) addition — modest but not negligible for a 2,800 lb car
3. **Complexity:** Additional components meant additional failure points. The 4WS indicator light was a known concern among owners.
4. **Subtle benefits:** In blind testing, most C&D staffers could not distinguish 4WS from 2WS during normal driving. The benefits were most apparent in aggressive transient maneuvers, not everyday driving.
5. **Unintuitive parking behavior:** As Jalopnik noted, you cannot park right up to a curb and then crank the wheel to pull away — the 4WS system will cock the rear wheels and "bash your rear quarter into the curb."
6. **Wet performance degradation:** C&D found the 4WS car performed worse in several wet tests, contradicting the theoretical advantage on slippery surfaces.
7. **Declining relevance:** By the 5th gen, 4WS was dropped from the US market entirely due to low demand.

### Market Outcome

The 4WS system's trajectory tells an interesting story:

- **3rd gen (1987–1991):** 80% JDM adoption (vs 30% expected) — runaway success in home market
- **4th gen (1991–1996):** Available but less popular; dropped from US mid-generation
- **5th gen (1997–2001):** Continued in Japan/Europe only; became a niche enthusiast feature

The system was ultimately discontinued not because it didn't work — it demonstrably did — but because the average buyer didn't perceive enough value to justify the cost. As Car and Driver concluded in 1988: "we doubt that many buyers would spend so much money just to make parking easier."

However, the concept never truly disappeared. Modern luxury and performance vehicles (Porsche, BMW, Mercedes-Benz, Hummer) now offer electronic rear-wheel steering as a standard or optional feature, validating Honda's 1977–1987 development effort as "a preview of the future, delivered a generation too early."

## 6. Engineering Rationale Summary

### Why Honda Paired H-Series with 4WS

1. **Brand positioning:** The Prelude was Honda's technology flagbearer. Pairing the newest engine (H-series DOHC VTEC) with the newest chassis technology (electronic 4WS) reinforced the car's premium, innovative image.

2. **Complementary performance characteristics:** The H-series provided high-RPM power and broad torque; 4WS provided turn-in agility and high-speed stability. Together, they created a more cohesive driving experience than either system alone.

3. **Weight distribution optimization:** The H-series' transverse mounting contributed to the BB1's 58/42 weight split, which is ideal for 4WS effectiveness. Balanced weight distribution means both axles respond equally to 4WS intervention.

4. **Competitive differentiation:** In the early 1990s sports coupe segment, very few competitors offered anything approaching 4WS. The combination of DOHC VTEC + 4WS gave the Prelude a unique selling proposition.

5. **Proven technology:** The 3rd-gen's 80% JDM adoption rate demonstrated strong consumer appetite. Honda had already invested heavily in 4WS R&D (10 years, since 1977); upgrading to electronic control for the 4th gen was a logical evolution rather than a restart.

6. **Active safety philosophy:** Honda's original 4WS development goal was collision avoidance (NHTSA ESV program). The H-series' power delivery meant the car could execute evasive maneuvers with both speed (engine) and agility (4WS).

## 7. Conflicts and Notes

- **Turning circle figures:** Multiple sources report slightly different numbers. Honda's official spec is 4.8 m (4WS) vs 5.3 m (2WS). Car and Driver reports 31.4 ft vs 34.8 ft. These are consistent within measurement methodology differences.
- **4WS weight addition:** Car and Driver (1988) reports 28 lbs. Later electronic systems may have differed slightly. The figure is consistently cited across sources.
- **80% adoption rate:** Confirmed by Wikipedia citing Honda internal data. Some forum sources suggest the rate may have been even higher in specific trim levels or model years, but 80% is the conservative, documented figure.
- **Wet performance:** C&D's finding that 4WS performed worse in wet tests is counterintuitive and contradicts the theoretical advantage. No definitive explanation was provided by C&D. Possible factors include the 4WS system's slight delay in actuator response and the added unsprung weight at the rear.
