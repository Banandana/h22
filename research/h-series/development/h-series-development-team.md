## Summary

The Honda H-series engine family was developed by a tightly coordinated team at Honda's Tochigi R&D Center under the New Concept Engine (NCE) program and its successor D-development project. The most prominently documented figure is **Ikuo Kajitani**, known as the "Father of VTEC," who served as Lead Project Leader (LPL) and pushed the power density target from 90 to 100 hp/liter. Corporate sponsorship came primarily through **Mugen Motorsports**, founded by Hirotoshi Honda (son of founder Soichiro Honda), which provided motorsport development and aftermarket support. The fourth-generation Prelude body was designed by **Yukio Kurosu**. Many supporting engineers remain unnamed due to Japanese corporate documentation practices.

## Key Development Personnel

### Ikuo Kajitani — Lead Project Leader (LPL), "Father of VTEC"

| Significance | Called "Father of VTEC" by multiple sources | [DrivingLine](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/civic-nsx-s2000-beyond-how-honda-vtec-changed-the-engine-game/), retrieved 2026-05-15 |

Kajitani was the central engineering figure behind VTEC technology, which became the defining feature of the H-series. He began work on the NCE (New Concept Engine) program in March 1984, which aimed to produce engines with high torque across both low and high RPM ranges. When the NCE taskforce was reassigned to the D-development project in November 1986, Kajitani became LPL. His initial specification called for 90 hp/liter, but Honda R&D president Nobuhiko Kawamoto encouraged him to aim higher. Kajitani raised the target to 100 hp/liter, and the team ultimately exceeded it with the B16A (160 hp from 1.6L = 100 hp/liter) in 1989.

The H-series inherited this VTEC architecture and was built upon the same engineering philosophy. Kajitani's team conducted "malicious tests" targeting 400,000 VTEC switching events to ensure absolute reliability in mass production. All components — gearboxes, crankshafts, camshafts — were engineered for extreme durability.

Kawamoto's intervention was pivotal. Without his encouragement, the VTEC engine may have been released at the more conservative 90 hp/liter target, which would have represented only a 10 hp increase over the existing DOHC engine. His leadership set the tone for Honda's entire high-performance engine strategy through the 1990s.

Uehara did not directly lead H-series development, but his influence on Honda's performance engineering culture was profound. As Large Project Leader for the mid-ship research project (1985) that led to the NSX, he helped establish the engineering standards that influenced all subsequent Honda performance vehicles. The NSX's C30A V6 was the first DOHC VTEC engine in a production car (1991), predating the H-series debut by two years. Uehara's emphasis on chassis-engine integration and driver feedback directly informed how the H-series was paired with 4WS in the Prelude.

## Known Gaps in Documentation

The following areas have limited or no publicly documented information:

1. **Individual block designers** — No named engineers are publicly identified as having designed the H-series aluminum block specifically. The FRM liner technology development team is similarly undocumented.
2. **Head porting specialists** — While factory porting is mentioned for the H22A Type S, no specific engineers are named.
3. **ECU calibration team** — The P28/P06 OBD2a ECU calibrations for JDM models were likely done by a team at Honda's electronics division, but no names surface in public sources.
4. **Camshaft profile designers** — The specific lift/duration profiles for Redtop vs. Blacktop variants are undocumented at the individual level.
5. **VTEC mechanism detail engineers** — While Kajitani invented VTEC, the precision engineers who designed the locking pins, rocker arms, and oil passages remain anonymous.

This anonymity is typical of Japanese automotive engineering culture, where team achievement is emphasized over individual recognition. Kajitani is the notable exception, having been prominently featured in Honda's own heritage documentation and international press.

## Notes

- **Cross-check status:** Kajitani's role as "Father of VTEC" and LPL of the D-development project is confirmed by 8+ independent sources including Honda's own heritage website, MotorTrend, Wikipedia, and Piston.my. Kawamoto's encouragement of the higher target is confirmed by Honda's official heritage documentation. Mugen's relationship to Honda is confirmed by Wikipedia, GrandPrix.com, and RacingSpirits.
- **Conflict note:** Some sources vary on whether Kajitani was formally titled "Chief Engineer" or "Lead Project Leader (LPL)." Honda's official documentation uses "LPL" consistently. The term "Father of VTEC" appears in non-official sources (YouTube, DrivingLine, SlashGear) but not in Honda's internal documentation.
- **Limitation:** Japanese corporate culture emphasizes team achievement over individual recognition. Many engineers who contributed to the H-series will never be publicly named. This is a known limitation of the available source material, not a gap in research effort.
