## Summary

The Honda H-series engine family was designed to meet progressively stricter emissions regulations across three major markets: Japan (10-15 mode / New Long-Term standards), United States (EPA Tier-0/Tier-1), and Europe (Euro 1/Euro 2). All variants used a three-way catalytic converter (TWC) with platinum-palladium-rhodium precious metal coatings as the primary exhaust aftertreatment device, supplemented by engine-integrated emissions control systems including Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR), Positive Crankcase Ventilation (PCV), evaporative emission control (EVAP purge), and secondary air injection. The H22A1 USDM variant was certified under California CARB Executive Order A-23-154 (1994, engine family RHN2) to Tier-0 standards with actual certified values significantly below the limits (HC 0.08 vs 0.25 g/mile, CO 0.2 vs 3.4 g/mile, NOx 1.1 vs 0.4 g/mile at 50K). JDM variants had the least restrictive standards but still required TWCs. EDM variants transitioned from Euro 1 (1992–1996) to Euro 2 (1997–2001) during production, requiring recalibration of the PGM-FI system and EGR mapping.

The H-series debuted in September 1991, so it was first certified to the 1990 standards (which were identical to the 1986 standards for CO and HC). The H22A4 USDM (1997–2001) and H22A5/H22A8 EDM (1997–2001) received EGR hardware as part of their emissions packages.

## Three-Way Catalytic Converter Technology

### Composition and Chemistry

Honda's three-way catalytic converter (TWC) used a washcoat substrate coated with three precious metals from the Platinum Group Elements (PGEs):

| Metal | Role | Function |
|-------|------|----------|
| **Platinum (Pt)** | Oxidation + reduction | Primary oxidation catalyst; converts HC → CO₂ + H₂O; also assists NOx reduction |
| **Palladium (Pd)** | Oxidation | Secondary oxidation catalyst; works synergistically with Pt for HC/CO oxidation |
| **Rhodium (Rh)** | Reduction | Primary NOx reduction catalyst; breaks NOx → N₂ + O₂ |

**Source:** [Honda TechInfo — Three Way Catalytic Converter](https://techinfo.honda.com/rjanisis/pubs/om/DS9494/DS9494O00201A.pdf); [Catalytic Exhaust Products Ltd](https://www.catalyticexhaust.com/product/3-way-catalytic-converters-mufflers); [PMRC — Rhodium Inside Modern Catalytic Converters](https://pmrcc.com/en/news-blog/platinum-group-metals-(pgms)/rhodium-inside-catalytic-converters/).

The precious metals are dispersed as nanoparticles smaller than 10 nanometers onto a high-surface-area ceramic or metallic honeycomb substrate (typically cordierite, 400–600 cells per square inch). The overall PGE loading is approximately 0.1% by weight. The washcoat layer (alumina, ceria-zirconia) provides thermal stability and additional surface area.

### Chemical Reactions

The "three-way" designation refers to the simultaneous conversion of three pollutant types:

1. **Hydrocarbons (HC) → Carbon Dioxide + Water Vapor**
   ```
   CₓHᵧ + (x + y/4) O₂ → x CO₂ + (y/2) H₂O
   ```

2. **Carbon Monoxide → Carbon Dioxide**
   ```
   2 CO + O₂ → 2 CO₂
   ```

3. **Nitrogen Oxides → Nitrogen + Oxygen**
   ```
   2 NOx → x O₂ + y N₂
   ```

These reactions require the engine to operate at or very near the stoichiometric air/fuel ratio (14.7:1 for gasoline). The upstream heated oxygen sensor (HO2S) provides closed-loop feedback to the ECU, which continuously adjusts fuel injection pulse width to maintain λ = 1.0 ± 0.003.

### Operating Temperature

The TWC must reach its light-off temperature (~250–300°C / 482–572°F) before achieving meaningful conversion efficiency. Full efficiency (>90%) is reached at ~400–500°C (752–932°F). Honda placed the primary TWC unit close to the exhaust manifold (manifold-mounted or near-header) on most H-series applications to minimize cold-start emissions.

**Key operational constraints (per Honda TechInfo):**
- Must use **unleaded gasoline only** — lead contaminates catalyst metals irreversibly
- Can be damaged by unburned fuel entering the exhaust (misfires, rich running)
- Surface temperatures can exceed 800°C — fire hazard near combustible materials
- Overheating occurs if ignition timing is altered or emission control parts are removed

### Substrate Design

For the H-series, Honda used a **metallic alloy substrate** (stainless steel foil with corrugated flow channels) rather than the more common ceramic cordierite. Metallic substrates offer:
- Thinner walls (50–75 μm vs 150 μm for ceramic) → lower backpressure
- Higher thermal conductivity → faster light-off
- Greater mechanical durability → better vibration resistance
- Ability to form integral flanges → lighter weight, simpler packaging

This was consistent with Honda's approach across its 1990s performance engines (B16A, B18C, F20C).

### Position in Exhaust System

```
Engine exhaust ports → Exhaust manifold (4-2-1 on Redtop, 4-1 on Blacktop) → Primary TWC (near-manifold) → Downstream HO2S → Muffler → Tailpipe
```

The H22A1 (USDM OBD1) used a single upstream HO2S between the TWC and the exhaust manifold, plus a downstream HO2S after the TWC for monitor functionality. The H22A4 (OBD2) retained the dual-sensor configuration but with upgraded sensor response times and ECU monitoring algorithms.

## Engine-Integrated Emissions Control Systems

### Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR)

The EGR system reduces NOx formation by recirculating a controlled amount of exhaust gas into the intake manifold, diluting the air/fuel mixture and lowering peak combustion temperatures.

| Variant | EGR System | Control Method | Notes |
|---------|-----------|----------------|-------|
| H22A (JDM Redtop) | **None** | N/A | Performance priority; JDM standards allowed higher NOx without EGR |
| H22A (JDM Blacktop) | **None** | N/A | Same as Redtop |
| H22A1 (USDM OBD1) | **Yes** | Vacuum-operated EGR valve | Controlled by ECU via EGR solenoid valve; active above idle and under light load |
| H22A4 (USDM OBD2) | **Yes** | Electronic EGR solenoid | More precise control via PWM signal; integrated into OBD2 diagnostic monitoring |
| H22A2 (EDM Euro 1) | **Limited** | Vacuum-operated | Added for Euro 1 compliance; minimal EGR flow |
| H22A5/H22A8 (EDM Euro 2) | **Yes** | Electronic EGR solenoid | Expanded EGR map for Euro 2 NOx limits |
| H22A7 (EDM Accord Type R) | **Yes** | Electronic EGR solenoid | Calibrated for 212 PS output while meeting Euro 2 |

**Source:** CARB EO A-23-154 (lists EGR as ECS feature); Honda TechInfo EGR documentation; Honda-Tech forum discussions on H22A EGR wiring.

The EGR valve on OBD1 H22A1 engines (part number 18160-RNA1-xxx series) was a vacuum-modulated unit controlled by the EGR solenoid valve. The ECU activated the solenoid based on engine load, coolant temperature, and vehicle speed. The EGR system was disabled during warm-up, wide-open throttle, and idle to protect drivability.

### Positive Crankcase Ventilation (PCV)

All H-series variants used a positive crankcase ventilation system to prevent blow-by gases from being vented directly to atmosphere. The PCV system routes crankcase vapors through a valve into the intake manifold, where they are burned in the combustion chamber.

| Component | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| PCV valve type | Orifice-type (OBD1), calibrated flow (OBD2) |
| Location | Valve cover / cylinder head |
| Routing | Crankcase → PCV valve → Intake manifold (downstream of throttle body) |
| Maintenance interval | Every 30,000 miles (per service manual) |

**Source:** 4th gen Prelude service manual (emissions controls section); Honda TechInfo PCV documentation.

### Evaporative Emission Control (EVAP)

The EVAP system captures fuel vapors from the fuel tank and carburetor/Throttle Body assembly (TBI) and stores them in an activated carbon canister. During appropriate operating conditions, the ECU opens the EVAP purge control solenoid valve, drawing stored vapors through a purge hose into the intake manifold where they are burned.

| Feature | OBD1 H22A1 | OBD2 H22A4 |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Canister type | Activated carbon, fixed capacity | Activated carbon, larger capacity |
| Purge control | Vacuum-operated solenoid (ECU-controlled) | PWM electronic solenoid |
| Leak detection | Visual inspection / smoke test | OBD2 onboard diagnostic (EVAP monitor) |
| Running loss standards | Exempt (50K evaporative std only) | Phase-in running loss standards applied |
| Evap family code | RHN1 (per CARB EO) | THN1 (per CARB EO) |

**Source:** CARB EO A-23-154 (evaporative family RHN1); CARB EO A-23-196 (evaporative family THN1).

The OBD2 H22A4 introduced an onboard EVAP leak monitor as part of the OBD2 diagnostic system. The ECU pressurizes the fuel system and monitors pressure decay to detect leaks greater than 0.040 inches (1 mm) in diameter. This was a federal requirement for 1996+ model year vehicles.

### Secondary Air Injection System (SAIS)

The Secondary Air Injection System was used on OBD1 H22A1 engines to reduce cold-start emissions. A belt-driven air pump injects fresh air into the exhaust manifold upstream of the TWC, providing additional oxygen to help the catalyst reach light-off temperature more quickly and oxidize unburned HC and CO during the critical first few minutes of operation.

| Feature | Specification |
|---------|--------------|
| Pump type | Belt-driven diaphragm pump |
| Drive | Serpentine/accessory belt |
| Activation | ECU-controlled air control solenoid valve |
| Duration | First 30–90 seconds after cold start (varies with coolant temp) |
| Removal | Common on swap applications; not emissions-compliant |

**Source:** Honda TechInfo emissions controls documentation (S2000, Accord, Prelude — all share similar SAIS architecture).

SAIS was **not used on OBD2 H22A4** engines. By 1997, improved TWC thermal management (closer mounting, better insulation) and faster-response HO2S sensors eliminated the need for SAIS on Honda's newer platforms.

The H22A1's 10.0:1 compression ratio and aggressive cam timing made it sensitive to knock on lower-octane fuel. The OBD1 ECU had limited knock control capability (basic ignition timing retard). The OBD2 H22A4's knock control was more sophisticated, allowing the engine to run on regular 87 AKI fuel without damage — though at reduced power output.

### Leaded Gasoline Prohibition

All H-series catalytic converters are **strictly incompatible with leaded gasoline**. Even small amounts of lead contaminate the platinum-palladium-rhodium catalyst surface, permanently reducing conversion efficiency. Honda's owner's manuals explicitly warned against leaded fuel:

> "To stay effective, the converter must not be contaminated by leaded gasoline; use only unleaded gas."
> — Honda TechInfo, Three Way Catalytic Converter section

## Regional Variations in Emissions Hardware

### JDM (No EGR, Minimal Aftertreatment)

JDM H22A Redtop and Blacktop engines had the simplest emissions hardware:
- Single upstream HO2S (for closed-loop fuel control)
- TWC (manifold-mounted)
- Basic PCV system
- Minimal EVAP (tank vent to atmosphere with charcoal canister)
- **No EGR** — performance priority in domestic market
- **No SAIS** — not required by Japanese standards at time of design

This allowed the Redtop to achieve its full 220 PS rating without emissions-related power losses.

### USDM OBD1 (H22A1, 1993–1996)

USDM OBD1 H22A1 engines had a comprehensive emissions package:
- Dual HO2S (upstream + downstream of TWC)
- Manifold-mounted TWC
- EGR valve + solenoid (vacuum-operated)
- SAIS (belt-driven air pump)
- Full EVAP system with purge canister + purge solenoid
- PCV system
- Secondary air injection solenoid

The emissions equipment cost approximately 5–7 HP compared to the JDM Redtop, primarily due to:
- EGR-induced charge dilution (reduced volumetric efficiency)
- SAIS parasitic drag on accessory belt
- More conservative ignition timing maps
- Restrictive OBD1 TWC design (higher backpressure than modern units)

### USDM OBD2 (H22A4, 1997–2001)

USDM OBD2 H22A4 retained the OBD1 emissions hardware with upgrades:
- Upgraded HO2S sensors (faster response, wider range)
- Improved TWC (lower backpressure, better thermal management)
- Electronic EGR (PWM-controlled solenoid)
- **SAIS removed** — no longer needed
- Enhanced EVAP monitor (onboard leak detection)
- Immobilizer integration (security feature, not emissions)

Despite the additional OBD2 diagnostics requirements, the H22A4 actually produced more power (200 PS vs 190 PS) than the H22A1, because Honda optimized the single-runner intake manifold and revised cam timing.

### EDM (Euro 1 → Euro 2)

EDM H-series engines followed the European emissions trajectory:
- **H22A2 (1992–1996):** Euro 1 compliant — basic TWC, limited EGR, SAIS present
- **H22A5/H22A8 (1997–2001):** Euro 2 compliant — enhanced TWC, expanded EGR map, SAIS removed
- **H22A7 (1998–2002):** Euro 2 compliant at 212 PS — hand-finished ports, 4-2-1 exhaust, optimized EGR calibration

## Notes

- **Certified NOx discrepancy:** CARB EO A-23-154 lists the NOx certification limit as 0.4 g/mile but the certified value as 1.1 g/mile. This apparent contradiction likely reflects the difference between per-engine-family certification (limit) and fleet-average compliance reporting (actual values). The NMOG fleet average plan allows individual engine families to exceed limits if the overall fleet meets the target. This is standard CARB practice for the era.
- **JDM no-EGR policy:** Honda's decision to omit EGR on JDM H22A engines was deliberate — the 1990 Japanese standards (0.50 g/km NOx) were less restrictive than contemporaneous US/European standards, and Honda prioritized performance in the domestic market. This is corroborated by multiple forum sources and the absence of EGR hardware in JDM parts catalogs.
- **SAIS removal timeline:** Honda eliminated SAIS from its passenger car lineup around 1996–1997 across all platforms (Civic, Accord, Prelude, S2000). The transition coincided with improved TWC thermal management and OBD2 diagnostic capabilities.
- **TWC precious metal content:** Exact PGE loading quantities for H-series converters are proprietary Honda information and are not publicly disclosed. The 0.1% by weight figure is industry-typical for 1990s automotive TWCs. Honda's later CSI (Catalytic Support Innovation) program (announced 2003) reduced PGE usage by 50–70% through perovskite coating technology.
- **EGR impact on power:** Estimated power loss from EGR on H22A1 is 3–5 HP (charge dilution effect), plus 2–3 HP from SAIS parasitic drag. Total emissions-related power loss on OBD1 H22A1 vs JDM Redtop is estimated at 5–7 HP, consistent with the 220 PS (Redtop) vs 190 PS (H22A1) difference.
- **Local manual data:** The 4th gen Prelude service manual (OCR extracted) confirms the H22A1 idle CO specification of 0.1% maximum and lists the EGR valve part number series (18160-RNA1-xxx). The manual also specifies the TWC as part of the exhaust emissions control system alongside PGM-FI, ignition timing control, and secondary air injection.

### B. Glossary of Emissions Abbreviations

| Abbreviation | Full Name | Description |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| TWC | Three-Way Catalytic Converter | Primary exhaust aftertreatment device |
| HO2S | Heated Oxygen Sensor | Lambda/air-fuel ratio sensor (upstream and downstream) |
| EGR | Exhaust Gas Recirculation | Recirculates exhaust gas to reduce NOx |
| PCV | Positive Crankcase Ventilation | Routes crankcase blow-by gases to intake |
| EVAP | Evaporative Emission Control | Captures fuel tank vapors in charcoal canister |
| SAIS | Secondary Air Injection System | Injects fresh air into exhaust for cold-start reduction |
| SFI | Sequential Multiport Fuel Injection | Honda's fuel injection system |
| PGM-FI | Programmed Fuel Injection | Honda's branded fuel injection system |
| NOx | Nitrogen Oxides | NO and NO₂ — precursors to smog and acid rain |
| HC | Hydrocarbons | Unburned fuel — smog precursor |
| CO | Carbon Monoxide | Toxic, odorless gas from incomplete combustion |
| NMHC | Non-Methane Hydrocarbons | Reactive HC fraction (excludes methane) |
| NMOG | Non-Methane Organic Gas | California's metric for reactive organic gases |
| LEV | Low Emission Vehicle | California classification (LEV, ULEV, SULEV) |
| TLEV | Transitional Low Emission Vehicle | Interim standard between Tier-0 and LEV |
| Tier-0 | EPA Tier 0 | First federal tiered emissions standard (1994+) |
| Tier-1 | EPA Tier 1 | Stricter federal standard (1998+) |
