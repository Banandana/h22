## Summary

The Honda H-series engine family was produced using a vertically integrated supply chain centered on Honda's own foundry and component manufacturing subsidiaries, supplemented by established Tier 1 Japanese suppliers. The aluminum block casting was handled by Honda Foundry Co., Ltd. (本田金属工業 / Honda Kinzoku), Honda's dedicated light-metal casting subsidiary. Camshafts, gears, and suspension components were supplied by Musashi Seimitsu Industry Co., Ltd., a long-standing Honda partner since 1956. Engine assembly occurred at Honda's Tochigi Plant in Japan (primary) with vehicle-level production also at Swindon UK HUM and Anna Engine Plant Ohio USA. Aftermarket support is provided by Mahle Motorsport (pistons designed for FRM liners), Golden Eagle Manufacturing (block resleeving), and RS Machine (FRM sleeve replacement).

**Role:** Consists of Yorii Automobile Plant and Ogawa Plant (Engine Plant, established 2009).

### Tochigi Plant (Powertrain Unit Manufacturing Department)

**Role:** Primary H-series engine component manufacturing.

Located in Moka-shi, Tochigi Prefecture, this plant manufactured engine parts, chassis parts, and transmission parts for automobiles. It began production in December 1970 and was Honda's dedicated powertrain parts facility. Honda announced plans to end production here by the end of 2025.

The Tochigi Plant is the most likely primary source of H-series engine block castings and machining, given its specialization in powertrain components and its location in Japan during the H-series production period.

**Source:** [Honda Global History Digest](https://global.honda/en/about/history-digest/75years-history/pdf/chapter3_1.pdf) — Retrieved 2026-05-15

### Anna Engine Plant (Ohio, USA)

**Role:** Engine manufacturing for North American market.

Built in 1985, began producing motorcycle engines in 1985 and automobile engines in September 1986. Designed to serve as the engine provider for Honda of Canada Manufacturing. Produced a 2.2L Accord engine (likely the F22B rather than H-series) as the first automobile engine at this facility.

While the H-series was primarily a JDM product, the Anna Plant represents Honda's North American engine manufacturing capability during the H-series era, though direct H-series production there is unconfirmed.

## Aftermarket & Replacement Parts Suppliers

### Mahle Motorsport

**Role:** Performance piston manufacturer. Mahle Gold Series pistons are specifically designed for H-series engines with factory FRM (Fiber-Reinforced Metal) cylinder liners.

Key details:
- **Gold Series** pistons use proprietary anti-friction skirt coating compatible with Honda's factory FRM cylinders, eliminating the need to re-sleeve the block
- Available in 4032 aluminum alloy (street/race) and 2618 aluminum alloy (race)
- Bore sizes: 87.00mm, 87.25mm (oversize options)
- Part numbers: 930081525, 930081535, 930082325, 930082335, 930083125, 930083135
- Compression ratios available: 9.2:1, 10.0:1, 10.1:1, 12.0:1, 12.1:1
- Mahle states 20 psi boost safety for H22 applications
- Also supplies bearings (ACL/King brand)

Mahle is the only major aftermarket piston manufacturer whose stock pistons work directly with factory FRM-lined H22 blocks without sleeving.

### Golden Eagle Manufacturing

**Role:** Aftermarket block resleeving specialist. Manufactures new cylinder sleeves for H22A1/H22A4 blocks.

Key details:
- Based in California, USA
- Turnaround time: 6-8 weeks for basic sleeving

**Role:** Aftermarket FRM sleeve replacement solution.

## FRM Liner Technology Supply Chain

### Development

FRM (Fiber-Reinforced Metal) cylinder liner technology was developed jointly by Honda Engineering Co., Ltd. and Honda R&D Co., Ltd. The technology was described in SAE Technical Paper 910835, presented at the International Congress & Exposition in Detroit, Michigan, February 25, 1991.

**Authors:** Masuo Ebisawa, Takashige Hara (Honda Engineering Co., Ltd.); Tadayoshi Hayashi, Hideaki Ushio (Honda R&D Co., Ltd.)

**Composition:** Carbon fiber + alumina (aluminum oxide, Al₂O₃) fibers embedded in aluminum alloy matrix. The cylinder bore surfaces consist of a 0.5mm-thick layer with these fibers. During casting, molten aluminum alloy is poured around cylinder cores composed of these two fibers. The cores absorb molten aluminum during casting, leaving a tough, wear-resistant composite cylinder wall integral with the block.

**Benefits over cast iron:**
- Superior heat transfer to water jackets (allows smaller cooling system)
- Enables open-deck block construction (used in 1997-2001 H22 variants)
- Weight reduction (2.4 kg lighter than equivalent cast-iron sleeve design)
- Allows larger bores within same external block dimensions and bore spacing

**Limitations:**
- Oil consumption at high mileage (FRM wears faster than cast iron under certain conditions)
- Requires special piston coatings (Mahle Gold Series, Weisco coated) for high-performance rebuilds
- Most aftermarket pistons require block sleeving before installation

**Source:** [SAE Paper 910835](https://saemobilus.sae.org/papers/production-process-metal-matrix-composite-mmc-engine-block-910835) — Retrieved 2026-05-15

## Key Findings

1. **Vertically integrated core:** H-series manufacturing was dominated by Honda's own subsidiaries — Honda Foundry Co., Ltd. (casting), Tochigi Plant (machining/assembly), and Musashi Seimitsu (camshafts/gears). This vertical integration allowed tight quality control and rapid iteration during the H-series development period.

2. **Musashi Seimitsu is critical:** With a 70-year relationship with Honda dating to 1956, Musashi supplied camshafts (directly relevant to VTEC operation), gears, and differential assemblies (for ATTS). Their Toyohashi headquarters and global manufacturing network make them the single most important external supplier for H-series performance components.

3. **FRM technology is unique to Honda:** The Fiber-Reinforced Metal cylinder liner was an internally developed Honda/R&D technology (SAE 910835). No external supplier was involved in the core FRM formulation — it was a proprietary Honda metallurgical innovation.

4. **Aftermarket ecosystem is mature:** By the time the H-series ended production in 2001, a robust aftermarket had already formed around FRM liner maintenance (RS Machine, Golden Eagle) and performance rebuilding (Mahle, Manley, Eagle, ARP, Cometic). This aftermarket ecosystem continues to support H-series engines today.

5. **Limited overseas H-series production:** The H-series was primarily a Japanese-manufactured engine. While Honda had engine plants in Ohio (Anna) and vehicle assembly in the UK (Swindon), confirmed H-series production outside Japan is uncertain. The Swindon HUM plant may have received H-series blocks for final assembly in European-market vehicles (H22A7 Accord Type R).

## Notes

- **Honda Foundry Co., Ltd.** is also known as Honda Kinzoku (本田金属工業). The company name appears in English as "Honda Foundry" on their corporate website. Exact founding date and full corporate history are not publicly detailed on their English-language site.
- **Tochigi Plant** is sometimes referred to as the "Powertrain Unit Manufacturing Department" in Honda corporate documents. Its exact address is Moka-shi, Tochigi Prefecture.
- **Musashi Seimitsu's** full product catalog for the H-series era is not publicly documented. The camshaft/gear/ball joint categorization is based on their general product lines and the 80th anniversary history document.
- **Swindon HUM (UK)** engine casting capability for H-series is inferred from HUM's general engine production capacity but not directly confirmed. The H22A7 Accord Type R (European market) could have been assembled with locally-cast blocks.
- **Anna Engine Plant** Ohio produced a 2.2L Accord engine starting September 1986, but this was almost certainly the F22B (SOHC) rather than the H22A (DOHC VTEC). The F22B shared the 87mm bore with the H-series but had different stroke and valvetrain.
- **FRM composition details** (exact fiber percentages, alumina particle size, aluminum alloy grade) are proprietary to Honda and not publicly disclosed. The SAE 910835 paper describes the process but not the exact material specifications.
- **No public documentation** exists for the specific allocation of H-series variant production across Honda's Japanese plants. The Tochigi Plant is the most likely primary source based on its powertrain specialization.
