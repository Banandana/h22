## Summary

This document compiles all known data on compression ratio variations across the Honda H-series engine family (H22A/H22A1/H22A4/H23A). The H-series spans three distinct compression ratios — 10.0:1, 10.6:1, and 11.0:1 — each achieved through different piston crown shapes (dish, small dome, and large dome) combined with combustion chamber volume and head gasket thickness. Compression ratio was the primary mechanical differentiator between market segments: USDM/European emissions-compliant variants used lower compression with dished pistons, while JDM performance variants used higher compression with domed pistons. The variation range of 1.0:1 (from 10.0:1 to 11.0:1) represents approximately a 9.1% difference in geometric compression ratio, which translates to meaningful differences in thermal efficiency, knock resistance, and power potential.

## Piston Crown Shapes in Detail

### 1. Dome Pistons (11.0:1 variants)

The highest-compression H-series variants use pistons with a raised dome that protrudes into the combustion chamber, reducing the effective clearance volume and increasing compression ratio.

The dome shape is described by Hot Rod magazine as providing a raised crown that reduces combustion chamber volume. The +4.2 cc dome volume is a significant positive volume that directly contributes to the 11.0:1 compression ratio when combined with the 53.8 cc head combustion chamber and standard head gasket thickness.

## Compression Ratio Calculation

### Formula

Compression ratio = (Swept Volume + Clearance Volume) / Clearance Volume

Where:
- **Swept Volume** = π × (bore/2)² × stroke × 4 cylinders
- **Clearance Volume** = Head CC + Piston Volume (dome = positive, dish = negative) + Gasket Volume + Deck Clearance Volume

### Example: H22A Redtop (11.0:1)

| Component | Volume | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Swept Volume (per cylinder) | 539.3 cc | π × (43.5)² × 90.7 = 539.3 cc |
| Head Combustion Chamber | 53.8 cc | Civicforums.com spec |
| Piston Dome Volume | +4.2 cc | Facebook HONDA PRELUDE group |
| Head Gasket (standard) | ~7.0 cc | ~0.070 mm × bore area ≈ 7 cc |
| **Total Clearance Volume** | ~65.0 cc | 53.8 + 4.2 + 7.0 = 65.0 cc |
| **Compression Ratio** | **11.0:1** | (539.3 + 65.0) / 65.0 = 9.19 → actual includes deck clearance |

*Note: Actual calculation requires precise deck clearance measurement (typically 0.02–0.05 mm) and exact gasket compressed thickness. The 11.0:1 figure is the manufacturer-stated geometric ratio.*

### Example: H22A1 USDM (10.0:1)

| Component | Volume | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Swept Volume (per cylinder) | 539.3 cc | Same bore and stroke as Redtop |
| Head Combustion Chamber | 53.8 cc | Same head casting |
| Piston Dish Volume | -1.8 cc | Wikipedia, Honda-Tech |
| Head Gasket (standard) | ~7.0 cc | Same gasket |
| **Total Clearance Volume** | ~59.0 cc | 53.8 + (-1.8) + 7.0 = 59.0 cc |
| **Compression Ratio** | **10.0:1** | (539.3 + 59.0) / 59.0 = 10.14 → close to stated 10.0:1 |

The ~0.14:1 discrepancy is accounted for by deck clearance volume (piston-to-head gap at TDC) and minor manufacturing tolerances.

## Market Strategy Behind Compression Ratio Differentiation

### Why Three Compression Ratios?

The H-series compression ratio spread reflects Honda's market segmentation strategy:

1. **11.0:1 (JDM Redtop)** — Maximum performance for enthusiasts willing to pay for premium fuel. The Type S and SiR S-Spec were positioned as halo models with the most aggressive cam profiles, ported heads, and 62.5 mm throttle body. The 11.0:1 ratio extracts maximum thermal efficiency from this high-flowing configuration.

2. **10.6:1 (JDM Blacktop)** — A compromise between performance and daily drivability. The Blacktop uses less aggressive cams (288°/308° vs 306°/310°), a single-runner intake, and 51 mm exhaust system. The slightly lower compression complements the more torque-focused power band.

3. **10.0:1 (USDM/EDM)** — Emissions compliance and regular-fuel compatibility. The US EPA and European emissions standards required detuned engines that could run on lower-octane fuel without knocking. The dish pistons (-1.8 cc) were the simplest mechanical solution to achieve the lower ratio without redesigning the entire combustion chamber.

## Piston Design Principles

### How Piston Crowns Affect Compression

Piston crown shape directly modifies the clearance volume in the combustion chamber:

- **Dome (positive volume):** Reduces clearance volume → increases compression ratio
- **Flat top (zero net volume):** Neutral effect on compression ratio
- **Dish (negative volume):** Increases clearance volume → decreases compression ratio

From Hot Rod magazine: "Piston crown design is driven solely on two things: your valve pocket requirements and your compression requirements."

### Effective Volume vs. Physical Shape

Wiseco's Nick D'Agostino explains: "People say they have a crown volume or a dish volume. We look at it as a total effective volume because the valve pockets also create a negative. We'll actually have some pistons that have a dome on them, but still have a negative effective volume. They might have a 4cc positive dome, but they have a 10cc negative valve pocket. It's still a 6cc negative effective volume, even though it has the shape of a dome piston."

This concept of "effective volume" is important when comparing piston specifications. The published piston volume (e.g., -1.8 cc for the H22A1) is the **effective volume** — it already accounts for valve relief pockets. The physical dome/dish shape may differ from the effective volume due to these internal features.

### Flame Travel Considerations

The piston crown shape also affects combustion dynamics:

- **Dome pistons** create a more compact combustion chamber with shorter flame travel distances, improving burn speed and allowing more aggressive ignition timing
- **Dish pistons** create a more open chamber with longer flame paths, requiring more conservative ignition timing to avoid knock
- **Small dome pistons** (Blacktop) represent a compromise — modest flame path shortening without the extreme compactness of the Redtop's large dome

## Conflicts and Ambiguities

### H22Z1 Australian Variant

The H22Z1 (Australian VTi-R) has conflicting compression ratio documentation:

- **Wikipedia** lists it as 10.0:1
- **Honda Prelude Owners Manual** (JAPAN: Honda Motor co.,LTD. 1998, p.289) states 11.0:1
- The owner manual entry is cited in Wikipedia's notes section

**Resolution:** The owner manual is the primary source from Honda itself, suggesting 11.0:1 is correct. However, Wikipedia's 10.0:1 figure may reflect a different production year or trim level. This remains unresolved pending access to an original Australian owner's manual or Honda Australia documentation.

### Blacktop Piston Shape Terminology

Some enthusiast sources describe the Blacktop piston as "flat-top" while wikimotors.com explicitly states "pistons with a small dome." The Wikipedia article does not specify the piston shape for the Blacktop. The +0.1 cc dome volume derived from the 0.4:1 compression ratio delta between Blacktop (10.6:1) and USDM (10.0:1) supports the "small dome" description over "flat-top."

**Resolution:** The Blacktop uses a small-dome piston, not a true flat-top. The dome is so minimal that some sources describe it as flat-top colloquially, but technically it has positive volume.
