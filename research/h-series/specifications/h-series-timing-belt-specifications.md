## Summary

This document covers the complete timing belt specifications for the Honda H-series engine family
(H22A, H22A1–H22A8, H22Z1, H23A): main timing belt and balance shaft belt part numbers, tooth
count, width, material composition, tooth profile, replacement intervals, tensioner types, and
aftermarket alternatives. The H-series uses **two separate belts**: a main timing belt driving the
camshafts and water pump, and a shorter balance shaft belt driving the dual counter-rotating
balance shafts. Both are critical components — the H-series is an interference engine, and belt
failure results in catastrophic valve-to-piston contact.

## Replacement Interval & Service Recommendations

### Official Intervals

| Application | Interval | Source | Notes |
|-------------|----------|--------|-------|
| **Normal driving** | 60,000–90,000 miles (96,000–144,000 km) | QWEN.md, Helms Manual (1997–2001 BB6 p. 6-13) | Honda factory recommendation. 60K conservative, 90K maximum. |
| **High-RPM / track use** | 60,000 miles (96,000 km) | QWEN.md | Conservative interval recommended for performance applications. |
| **With manual tensioner conversion** | 60,000 miles | Community best practice | Even with upgraded tensioner, belt material degrades with age regardless of mileage. |
| **Time-based** | Every 5–6 years regardless of mileage | Industry best practice | Urethane and rubber materials degrade with age, heat cycling, and ozone exposure even at low mileage. |

### Service Best Practices

| Practice | Detail | Source |
|----------|--------|--------|
| **Replace water pump simultaneously** | Water pump driven by timing belt; labor overlap makes simultaneous replacement cost-effective | Helms Manual recommendation, community consensus |
| **Replace VTEC solenoid gasket** | Cheap part; replace every timing belt service to prevent future oil leaks | QWEN.md maintenance section |
| **Replace crankshaft/cam seals** | If any oil leakage present, replace seals during belt service | Helms Manual p. 6-11 |
| **Inspect belt condition** | Check for cracks, oil/coolant soaking, fraying, missing teeth | Helms Manual p. 6-13 |
| **Never reuse old belt** | Belt stretches and fatigues; reinstallation risks immediate failure | Community consensus, Helms Manual |
| **Rotate crank CCW during install** | Take up belt slack on tension side by rotating backward | Helms Manual, eXtremePrelude, PreludePower |
| **Verify all timing marks after install** | Rotate crank 2 full revolutions CCW; recheck all marks align | Helms Manual, multiple forum sources |

## Notes

### Key Findings

1. **Two Belts, Not One:** The H-series uses two separate timing belts — a 155-tooth main timing belt (24mm wide, urethane) and a 70-tooth balance shaft belt (16mm wide, rubber). Both must be replaced during timing service. The Gates Racing T226RB kit includes both belts.

2. **OEM Part Number Confirmed:** Honda part 14400-P13-014 (designated 155Yu24 in Honda catalog nomenclature) is the definitive OEM main timing belt for all H-series variants from 1993–2001. The designation breaks down as: 155 teeth, Urethane material, 24mm width.

3. **Balance Shaft Belt Is Shared Across Platforms:** The 70-tooth balance shaft belt (13405-PT0-004, designated 70Ru16) is shared between H-series, B-series, and F-series engines due to their common architecture. This is one of the few interchangeable timing components across Honda engine families.

4. **H23A Timing Belt Is NOT Compatible:** Despite sharing the same balance shaft belt, the H23A uses a completely different main timing belt with different tooth pitch AND length. HondaSwap forum user Mulepick confirmed purchasing a Goodyear belt listed for H23A that had teeth too deep and too widely spaced for H22A fitment.

5. **OBD1 Hydraulic Tensioner Is the Achilles' Heel:** The hydraulic auto-tensioner on OBD1 (1992–1996) engines is the single most common cause of catastrophic H22 timing failure. The community-standard fix is the H23 manual tensioner conversion using 9 distinct OEM parts or a KS Tuned aftermarket kit.

6. **Gates Racing T226RB Is Premium Choice:** At $150–200, the Gates Racing belt is significantly more expensive than OEM urethane (~$98) but offers 300% more strength, HNBR material with 3× heat resistance, aramid-reinforced teeth, and includes both the main timing belt AND balance shaft belt. Recommended for boosted builds or track use.

### Conflicts and Discrepancies

- **Belt length variance:** HondaPartsNow/Hybrid Racing list 1240mm (48.82"), while HondaSwap forum user Mulepick reports 49.25". This 0.43" (11mm) difference is within manufacturing tolerance for rubber/urethane belts and does not indicate a different belt. Both refer to the same 155-tooth part.

- **BadassTuning F22B vs H22A belt confusion:** The BadassTuning page lists a 126-tooth belt (126RU24, Honda 14400-PT2-004, Gates T132) for the F22B DOHC. This is NOT the H22A belt — it is specific to the F22B. The comment from reader John confirms this belt was for an F22B swap into a '96 Prelude, and the commenter noted it was exactly 1 tooth shorter than the original installed belt (which was the incorrect belt). The H22A uses 155 teeth, not 126.

- **Goodyear Gatorback compatibility confusion:** HondaSwap user Mulepick initially purchased Goodyear Gatorback 40216 listed for '95 Prelude VTEC and found it had wrong tooth pitch and was too short (127 teeth). Later found that Gatorback 40132 was the correct size. This highlights the importance of verifying tooth count before purchase — aftermarket listings can be incorrect.

### Implications for Service and Modification

- **Timing Belt Replacement:** Always replace both the main timing belt AND the balance shaft belt. The Gates Racing T226RB kit is the most convenient option as it includes both. For budget builds, OEM urethane (14400-P13-014) + Unitta balance belt (13405-PT0-004) is adequate for stock NA operation.

- **OBD1 Tensioner Upgrade:** Any OBD1 (1992–1996) H22 engine should receive the H23 manual tensioner conversion during timing belt service. The 9-part kit costs ~$50–80 in OEM parts but saves thousands in potential engine damage.

- **Interference Engine Risk:** The H-series is an interference engine. Belt failure at any RPM results in valve-to-piston contact and catastrophic engine damage. Never stretch the replacement interval beyond 60,000 miles for performance applications.

- **Water Pump Replacement:** The water pump is driven by the timing belt. Labor overlap makes simultaneous replacement cost-effective. The OEM water pump part number is 19200-P13-003.
