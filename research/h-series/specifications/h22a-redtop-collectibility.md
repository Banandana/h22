## Summary

This document covers the collectibility of the Honda H22A Redtop (JDM 1992–1996) engine and its host vehicles — primarily the 4th-generation Prelude SiR / Type S — examining current used prices across global markets (Japan, US, UK, Europe), rarity factors that drive value, condition premiums, and trend analysis. The H22A Redtop-powered Preludes are experiencing significant appreciation as 1990s VTEC icons, with mint-condition examples commanding prices rivaling new Civic Type Rs ($43,000+). A 998-mile JDM Redtop Type S sold for $40,000 in March 2025 and was relisted at $45,000. Hagerty reports condition #1 (concours) fifth-gen Type SH values at ~$36,000, while fourth-gen VTEC models in good condition average $16,000–$18,000 and low-mileage mint examples reach $40,000–$45,000. In Japan, Goo-net Exchange lists SiR Type S examples from ¥1,610,000–¥3,275,000 (~$10,500–$21,500). The UK market shows average prices around £6,137 with high-end sales up to £12,995. Engine-only values range from $900–$2,500 depending on mileage and completeness. Key appreciation drivers include the 25-year import rule (passed for 1997–2001 models), millennial/Gen-Z nostalgia, the discontinuation of manual-transmission Hondas, and the H22A's reputation as one of Honda's finest DOHC VTEC engines. Cross-referenced against Hagerty Media, CarBuzz, Kelley Blue Book, Classic.com, Edmunds, JD Power, Goo-net Exchange, Honda-Tech forums, and community auction data.

## Rarity Factors

### Production Numbers (All Markets)

| Variant | Years | Estimated Production | Rarity Level |
|---------|-------|---------------------|--------------|
| 4th Gen Prelude (all trims) | 1992–1996 | 98,627 | Moderate |
| 4th Gen Prelude VTEC (H22A1 Redtop) | 1993–1996 | ~25,000–30,000 | Low-Moderate |
| 4th Gen Prelude Type S / SiR S-spec (JDM) | 1995–1996 | ~5,000–8,000 | Low |
| 5th Gen Prelude (all trims) | 1997–2001 | 58,118 | Low |
| 5th Gen Prelude Type SH (ATTS, H22A4) | 1997–2001 | ~15,000–20,000 | Low-Moderate |
| Accord Type R CH1 (H22A7, EDM) | 1998–2002 | <2,000 (UK only) | Very Low |
| JDM Accord Euro R CL1 (H22A) | 2000–2002 | <2,000 | Very Low |

**Key rarity drivers:**

1. **Attrition rate.** Like most sports coupes of the era, a significant percentage of Preludes were totaled through accidents, theft, or enthusiast abuse. Hagerty notes that "many of the ones that made it out of the factory were totaled through theft or drivers whose enthusiasm exceeded their ability." Heavy weight (3,038 lbs for Type SH vs. ~2,500 lbs for Integra Type R) made them less likely to be track-killed than lighter rivals, but also less likely to be preserved by cautious owners.

2. **Low overall production.** Total 4th + 5th gen combined: ~156,745 units. Compared to contemporaries like the Mazda RX-7 (~130,000 FD), Nissan Skyline R32/R33 (~115,000 combined), and Toyota Supra Mk IV (~115,000), the Prelude's numbers are comparable but the coupe body style and lack of a wagon/sedan variant limited its appeal to a narrower buyer pool.

3. **Unique technology.** The ATTS (Active Torque Transfer System) on the Type SH was Honda's world-first mass-market torque-vectoring system — an early precursor to the current SH-AWD. This makes the Type SH uniquely interesting to collectors as a technology pioneer, even if the system adds complexity.

4. **JDM exclusivity.** The SiR Type S and SiR S-spec with the highest-output H22A Redtop variants (220 PS) were Japan-only. These cars carry a "unicorn" premium when imported to Western markets, particularly in original, unmodified condition with verified low mileage.

5. **25-Year Import Rule.** 5th generation models (1997+) became legally importable to the US in 2022. 4th generation (1992–1996) has been importable since 2017. This opened new demand from US collectors who previously had no legal avenue to acquire these cars.

6. **Manual transmission scarcity.** The upcoming 6th-gen Prelude (2026) has no manual transmission option. This retroactively increases the value of all previous generations with manual gearboxes, particularly the 5-speeds found in VTEC and Type SH models.

### Demand Drivers

1. **Nostalgia cycle.** Millennials (40% of Hagerty's Prelude buyer interest vs. 22% of collector car market overall) and Gen Z (21%) are entering prime buying age. Hagerty notes: "only older millennials would have been of driving age during the fifth-gens' production, many lower-specs were conscripted as moderately used first-cars, leading to later nostalgia for the clean, high-spec SH."

2. **New Prelude announcement (2025).** The reintroduction of the Prelude nameplate with the 6th generation (hybrid, no manual, $43,195 MSRP) drove massive awareness. While enthusiasts largely criticized the new Prelude, the announcement itself served as free advertising for the original. CarBuzz noted: "the light being shined has got bigger" on used 4th and 5th gen values.

3. **End of manual Hondas.** The new Prelude's e-CVT direct-drive system (simulated 8-speed via paddle shifters) confirmed the death of the manual Honda. This retroactively elevates all previous generations with actual manual transmissions.

4. **VTEC icon status.** The H22A Redtop is widely considered one of Honda's best DOHC VTEC engines. Its combination of 220 PS from 2.2L, 11.0:1 compression, aggressive cam profiles (306°/310° duration), and the celebrated VTEC crossover at ~5,500 RPM gives it legendary status among enthusiasts.

5. **Comparison to pricier rivals.** Hagerty notes that the 5th-gen Type SH at $36,000 (condition #1) is "not just beating a new Honda Civic Type R for price but even its original $26,000 MSRP when adjusted for inflation, a conversion that works out to some $48,000 in 2025 bucks." Meanwhile, the 1998–2001 Integra Type R commands ~$91,000 for condition #1 — making the Type SH a relative bargain.

6. **Aftermarket support.** While not as extensive as Civic/Integra aftermarket, the H22A has robust swap culture (H22 swaps into EG/EK Civics, DA/DC Integras, S-chassis Silvias, NA/NB Miatas). This keeps parts available and maintains mechanical knowledge.

### Risk Factors

1. **Market saturation risk.** If too many low-effort imports flood the US market post-25-year rule, prices could soften. However, Hagerty notes that "total production around 58,000 tamps the aftermarket far below other Hondas of the era," limiting oversupply.

2. **FRM liner degradation.** The H-series FRM (Fiber-Reinforced Metal) cylinder liners are known to degrade over time, leading to oil consumption and compression loss. This affects long-term reliability and could deter some buyers if repair costs become prohibitive.

3. **Age-related issues.** At 29–33 years old (2025), rubber components, seals, and electrical connections are degrading regardless of mileage. Low-mileage examples may actually require more attention (dry-rotted hoses, degraded fuel lines, stale fluids).

4. **Economic sensitivity.** Collector car values are sensitive to economic conditions. The 2024 market correction showed that common examples can drop 5–10% even while mint examples hold steady.

5. **Insurance costs.** Hagerty quotes average ~$9,500 for the 5th-gen, which translates to meaningful insurance premiums for younger buyers.

## Notes

### Conflicts and Discrepancies

1. **Kelley Blue Book vs. auction data.** KBB values for 4th-gen Preludes appear significantly lower than actual auction results. KBB's 1994 Si fair market value of $2,900–$4,128 (good condition) contrasts sharply with BaT/Cars&Bids averages of $11,500–$14,000. This discrepancy likely stems from KBB's methodology weighting trade-in and private-party values heavily, while auction platforms capture collector-premium pricing. KBB should be treated as a floor value; auction data better reflects true collector market value.

2. **US vs. UK pricing divergence.** UK prices (£6,137 average, ~$7,800) are notably lower than US prices ($11,000 average). This may reflect lower collector car culture penetration in the UK for JDM vehicles, or simply different supply/demand dynamics. The highest UK sale (£12,995 / ~$16,500) still falls below the US ceiling ($45,000).

3. **Condition #1 definition ambiguity.** Different sources use slightly different condition definitions. Hagerty's #1 (concours) is the most rigorous; KBB's "excellent" is more lenient. When comparing values across sources, always verify the condition tier being referenced.

4. **Engine-only pricing volatility.** H22A engine prices fluctuate based on swap demand. The Reddit thread (2019) suggested $2–3k for a complete swap package, while 2025 Facebook group data shows $900–$1,200 for longblocks. This suggests either a market correction or that the 2019 figures included transmission and harness upgrades. Current consensus: $900–$2,500 depending on completeness and mileage.

### Assumptions

- All USD conversions use approximate exchange rates of 1 USD = 153 JPY and 1 USD = 0.79 GBP (May 2025 averages). Actual rates vary.
- Inflation-adjusted original MSRPs use the US Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI inflation calculator.
- "Mint condition" is defined as Hagerty Condition #1: showroom-perfect, documented, under 20,000 miles.
- JDM prices assume legal import to the US (post-25-year rule); actual landed cost includes shipping (~$1,500–$3,000), import duties, and compliance modifications.

### Open Questions

1. Will the new Prelude's commercial failure (174 units in first full month, per Carscoops January 2026) dampen or amplify interest in the original? Initial hypothesis: short-term confusion, long-term positive (scarcity narrative).
2. How will FRM liner replacement costs affect long-term values? If sleeving becomes prohibitively expensive, it could create a bifurcated market (sleeved vs. unsleeved).
3. What is the impact of increasing EV adoption on ICE collector values? General trend suggests continued appreciation for analog, manually-shifted sports cars.
4. Are there untapped regional markets (South America, Southeast Asia, Middle East) where H22A Preludes could see new demand?
