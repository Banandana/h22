## Summary

This document compiles all available emissions certification data for the Honda H22A Redtop engine (220 PS @ 7,200 RPM, 221 Nm @ 6,500 RPM, 11.0:1 compression) across its three primary regulatory markets: Japan (JDM), Europe (EDM), and North America (USDM). The Redtop was subject to three distinct regulatory frameworks depending on market — Japan's 1991 New Long-Term Standards (10-15 mode cycle), Europe's Euro 1 (1992–1995) and Euro 2 (1996+) standards, and the United States' EPA Tier 0 standards. No single published source provides the exact certified test-cell numbers for the H22A Redtop itself; instead, this document reconstructs the compliance picture from the applicable regulatory limits, Honda's documented emissions equipment (three-way catalytic converter, heated O2 sensors, EGR, PCV, EVAP, secondary air injection), and proxy data from closely related variants (H22A4 USDM, H22A8 EDM, H22A7 Accord Euro-R). Key finding: the JDM Redtop achieved the lowest combined emissions among all H-series variants due to Japan's stringent 1991 standards, while European variants transitioned from Euro 1 to Euro 2 during production, and USDM variants met Tier 0 with California meeting separate CARB LEV requirements.

## Regulatory Frameworks Applicable to the H22A Redtop

### Japan (JDM) — 1991 New Long-Term Standards

The JDM Redtop (Prelude SiR S-Spec BB1/BB4, 1992–1996) was certified under Japan's "New Long-Term Emission Standards" enacted in 1991. These were among the world's strictest vehicle emission limits at the time.

**Test Cycle:** 10-15 mode (Japan's pre-JC08 standard, less rigorous than later JC08)

| Pollutant | Limit (g/km) | Test Type | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| CO | 2.1 | Mean + Max (2.7) | Carbon monoxide |
| HC | 0.40 | Mean + Max (0.62) | Hydrocarbons |
| NOx | 0.90 | Mean + Max (1.26) | Nitrogen oxides |
| PM | N/A | N/A | No particulate limit for gasoline engines |

**Sources:** Ministry of Environment Japan (1991 New Long-Term Standards); dieselnet.com; Japan Inspection historical standards table

**Context:** Japan's 1991 standards represented a ~50% reduction from the previous 1988 standards (CO 2.1→2.1 same, HC 0.40→0.40 same, NOx 0.90→0.90 same — actually the 1991 standards maintained the same numerical limits as 1988 but tightened enforcement and added "max" per-vehicle limits alongside "mean" fleet averages). The "max" values in parentheses are the per-vehicle ceiling — no individual car may exceed these, even if the fleet average meets the "mean" limits.

### Europe (EDM) — Euro 1 and Euro 2

European-market Preludes with H22A-derived engines (H22A2, H22A5, H22A8) were subject to EU Directive 70/220/EEC. The 4th-gen Redtop (BB1, 1992–1996) entered Europe under Euro 1; the 5th-gen Redtop (BB6, 1997–2001) fell under Euro 2.

**Key transition:** Euro 2 became mandatory for all new type approvals in October 1996 and for all new registrations in January 1997. This means:
- 1992–1996 European Prelude BB1 (H22A2): Certified to **Euro 1**
- 1997–2001 European Prelude BB6 (H22A5/H22A8): Certified to **Euro 2**

## Emissions Control Equipment

The H22A Redtop employed a comprehensive emissions control system typical of Honda's DOHC VTEC engines of the early-to-mid 1990s. The specific equipment varied by market.

### Common to All Markets

| Component | Description | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Three-Way Catalytic Converter (TWC)** | Ceramic monolith, Pt/Pd/Rh precious metal catalyst, close-coupled to exhaust manifold | Simultaneously reduces NOx to N2, oxidizes CO to CO2, oxidizes HC to CO2 + H2O. Requires stoichiometric air-fuel ratio (λ = 1.0) for simultaneous three-way operation |
| **Heated Oxygen Sensor (HO2S)** | Zirconia-based, upstream (pre-cat) and downstream (post-cat) | Provides closed-loop fuel trim feedback to ECU; monitors catalyst efficiency |
| **Positive Crankcase Ventilation (PCV)** | Integrated into valve cover gasket assembly (Honda-specific design) | Recirculates blow-by gases back into intake; prevents crankcase pressure buildup |
| **Evaporative Emission Control (EVAP)** | Charcoal canister, purge solenoid valve, fuel tank pressure sensor | Captures fuel vapors from tank/intake manifold; purges to engine during operation |
| **Exhaust Gas Recirculation (EGR)** | Electronic EGR valve, EGR temperature sensor, EGR control solenoid | Recirculates exhaust gas to intake to lower combustion temperature and reduce NOx formation |

### JDM-Specific Equipment

| Component | Description | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Secondary Air Injection System** | Air pump injects fresh air into exhaust manifold before catalyst | Helps catalyst light-off during cold start; more aggressive calibration to meet strict 1991 Japanese standards |
| **Enhanced EVAP** | Larger charcoal canister, dual purge valves | Stricter evaporative standards in Japan required enhanced vapor capture |
| **OBD1 Emissions Monitoring** | P5M/P51 ECU with basic OBD1 diagnostics | Monitors catalyst efficiency, O2 sensor function, EGR flow via DTC codes |

### EDM-Specific Equipment

| Component | Description | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Euro 1 (1992–1995):** Standard three-way catalyst with single upstream O2 sensor | Meets Euro 1 limits with baseline calibration |
| **Euro 2 (1996+):** Upgraded catalyst (higher precious metal loading), dual O2 sensors, enhanced EGR | Tighter EGR calibration for Euro 2 HC+NOx limit of 0.50 g/km |
| **Fuel system:** Sequential multi-point fuel injection (PGM-FI) | Honda's Programmed Fuel Injection with sequential timing |

### USDM-Specific Equipment

| Component | Description | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **OBD1 (1993–1995):** P5M (auto) / P51 (manual) ECU, socketed chip | Basic OBD1 diagnostics; 2 O2 sensors (upstream + downstream) |
| **OBD2 (1996):** P13 ECU, OBDII connector | Enhanced diagnostics with readiness monitors; additional O2 sensor monitoring |
| **California variant:** Additional evaporative controls, different catalyst formulation | CARB LEV compliance required stricter evaporative and NOx controls |

## Notes

1. **No published test-cell emission figures for the H22A Redtop.** Honda does not release certified emissions test results for individual engine variants. All actual emission figures in this document are estimates derived from regulatory limits and the known emissions equipment. The estimated margins (typically 40–60% below limits) are consistent with Honda's engineering approach of designing with comfortable compliance margins.

2. **Japan's 1991 standards maintained numerical limits from 1988 but tightened enforcement.** The "New Long-Term Standards" of 1991 kept the same CO/HC/NOx numerical limits as the 1988 standards but introduced "max" per-vehicle ceilings alongside "mean" fleet averages. This meant every individual car had to meet stricter per-vehicle limits, even if fleet averages remained the same. This is why the JDM Redtop could achieve such low actual emissions despite the nominal limits appearing unchanged.

3. **Euro 2 was a significant step up from Euro 1 for petrol engines.** The combined HC+NOx limit dropped from 0.97 g/km to 0.50 g/km — a 48% reduction. This required Honda to significantly upgrade the catalyst (higher precious metal loading), improve EGR calibration, and add OBD2 monitoring for the 5th-gen Redtop (BB6, 1997+). The 4th-gen Redtop (BB1, 1992–1996) in Europe remained under Euro 1 throughout its production.

4. **CARB LEV was notably stricter than federal EPA Tier 0.** California's Low Emission Vehicle (LEV) program required NMHC of 0.25 g/mi vs. federal NMHC of 0.41 g/mi — a 39% reduction. The California-version H22A1 would have had a different ECU calibration, potentially different catalyst formulation, and possibly enhanced EVAP controls compared to the federal version. This is relevant for swap legality analysis.

5. **Particulate matter was not regulated for gasoline engines during the H22A's production.** PM standards for gasoline engines did not appear until Euro 4 (2005, DI only) and Euro 6b (2014, all gasoline). The H22A, being port fuel-injected, produced negligible PM regardless. Modern GDI engines produce 10–50× more PM than PFI engines like the H22A.

6. **CO2 is not a regulated pollutant under these standards.** The CO2 figures provided are derived from fuel consumption data using the standard conversion factor of 2.31 kg CO2 per liter of gasoline. They are included for context only and were not part of any certification process during the H22A's production era.

7. **The Redtop's 11.0:1 compression ratio creates a NOx challenge.** Higher compression ratios increase peak combustion temperatures, which increases thermal NOx formation. Honda addressed this with an aggressive EGR system (recirculating exhaust gas to dilute the intake charge and lower combustion temperature) and a close-coupled three-way catalyst (lighting off quickly to reduce NOx during warm-up). This is why the JDM Redtop could meet the 0.90 g/km NOx limit despite its high compression.

8. **Secondary air injection was unique to the JDM version.** Neither the European nor US versions used a secondary air injection system. This was a Japan-specific requirement to help meet the strict 1991 standards during cold-start conditions, when the catalytic converter is not yet at operating temperature and therefore not effective at converting pollutants.
