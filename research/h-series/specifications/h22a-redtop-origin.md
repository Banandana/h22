## Summary

The Honda H22A Redtop was introduced in September 1991 alongside the fourth-generation Honda Prelude (BB1 chassis) as the flagship powerplant for Honda's new H-series engine family. Developed to meet president Nobuhiko Kawamoto's engineering target of 100 PS/L specific output, the Redtop produced 220 PS (162 kW; 217 hp) at 7,200 RPM and 221 Nm (163 lb-ft) of torque at 6,500 RPM — the highest output of any production inline-4 from Honda at the time. It was exclusive to Japanese Domestic Market applications: the Prelude SiR S-Spec, Prelude Type S, and later the Accord Euro R / Torneo Euro-R. The Redtop distinguished itself from the concurrent H22A Blacktop (200 PS) through dome pistons (11.0:1 compression vs. 10.6:1), aggressive camshafts (306°/310° vs. 288°/308°), a larger 62.5 mm throttle body, improved exhaust manifolds, a 57 mm exhaust system, and light factory head porting. Built on a closed-deck aluminum block with FRM cylinder liners and 50 mm main journals, the Redtop was produced from 1992 through 1996 in the 4th-gen Prelude, with its spirit carried forward into the 5th-gen Type S (1997–2001) and the Accord Euro R (1999–2001).

## Development Background

### The 100 PS/L Target

In the late 1980s, Honda president Nobuhiko Kawamoto issued a directive that Honda's next-generation sports car engine must achieve 100 PS per liter of displacement. This target was driven by several factors:

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Competitive pressure | Toyota's 5S-GE (2.0L, 160 PS = 80 PS/L) and Mazda's 13B-MSP rotary (1.3L, 160 PS = 123 PS/L) were pushing specific output higher |
| Technology showcase | DOHC VTEC was Honda's crown jewel — the engine needed to demonstrate the technology's potential |
| Market positioning | The Prelude was Honda's flagship sports coupe, competing with the Nissan 240SX, Toyota Celica, and Mitsubishi GTO |
| Engineering philosophy | High specific output meant smaller, lighter engines with less internal friction per unit of power |

The H22A achieved this target at 100 PS/L (220 PS / 2.157 L), meeting Kawamoto's original vision.

### H-Series Derivation from F-Series

The H-series was largely derived from the F-series architecture, sharing many design features:

| Feature | F-Series | H-Series | Notes |
|---------|----------|----------|-------|
| Block material | Aluminum + FRM liners | Aluminum + FRM liners | Direct carryover |
| Valvetrain | DOHC 16V VTEC | DOHC 16V VTEC | Evolved cam profiles |
| Cylinder bore | 81.0 mm (F20C) | 87.0 mm | Increased for torque |
| Stroke | 75.0 mm (F20C) | 90.7 mm | Longer stroke for low-end torque |
| Displacement | 1.997 L (F20C) | 2.157 L | 8% larger displacement |
| Rod ratio | 1.33:1 (F20C) | 1.58:1 | Higher ratio = better low-RPM torque |

The longer stroke and higher rod ratio of the H22A sacrificed some high-RPM revving ability compared to the F20C but gained significant low-to-mid range torque — making it more suitable for grand touring and daily driving.

## Distinguishing Features vs. Blacktop

The Redtop and Blacktop shared the same base block architecture but differed significantly in performance-oriented components:

| Component | Redtop | Blacktop | Impact |
|-----------|--------|----------|--------|
| Valve cover | Red | Black | Visual identification only |
| Piston type | Dome | Flat-top | +1.0:1 CR (11.0 vs. 10.6) |
| Compression ratio | 11.0:1 | 10.6:1 | Higher CR = more power, requires premium fuel |
| Camshaft duration (intake/exhaust) | 306° / 310° | 288° / 308° | More overlap = more high-RPM airflow |
| Camshaft lift (intake/exhaust) | 11.91 mm / 11.12 mm | 11.38 mm / 10.54 mm | Higher lift = more valve opening |
| Throttle body | 62.5 mm | 60 mm | Better airflow at high RPM |
| Intake manifold | Dual runner (Prelude) / Single runner (Accord) | Single runner | Dual runner improves mid-range torque |
| Exhaust system | 57 mm diameter | 51 mm diameter | Less backpressure |
| Exhaust manifolds | Improved cast iron | Standard cast iron | Better flow |
| Factory head porting | Light porting | Stock condition | Marginal flow improvement |
| Power output | 220 PS @ 7,200 RPM | 200 PS @ 7,000 RPM | 20 PS difference, primarily high-RPM |
| Torque | 221 Nm @ 6,500 RPM | 219 Nm @ 5,500 RPM | Blacktop peaks earlier; Redtop peaks higher |

## Production Locations

The H22A was manufactured at Honda's Suzuka Engine Plant in Mie Prefecture, Japan — the same facility that produced the F20C for the Integra Type R and later the K20A for the Civic Type R. The Suzuka plant was Honda's primary performance engine facility in the 1990s, known for hand-assembled engines and strict quality control.

| Plant | Location | Engines Produced | Notes |
|-------|----------|-----------------|-------|
| Suzuka Engine Plant | Mie Prefecture, Japan | H22A, H23A, F20B, F20C | Primary performance engine facility |
| Sayama Plant | Saitama Prefecture, Japan | H22A1 (USDM), H22A2 (EDM) | Volume production for export markets |

## Notes

### Key Observations

1. **The Redtop was JDM-exclusive.** It was never officially sold in the US, Europe, or Australia. All Redtop Preludes and Accords were imported through unofficial channels or as OEM parts cars. This exclusivity has contributed to their collector status and premium pricing.

2. **The 100 PS/L target was a cultural milestone.** President Kawamoto's directive was not just an engineering goal — it was a statement of Honda's technological ambition. Achieving it with a mass-produced engine (not a race engine) demonstrated Honda's manufacturing excellence.

3. **The Redtop/Blacktop distinction is visual and mechanical.** The red valve cover is the most obvious identifier, but the underlying differences (pistons, cams, intake, exhaust) account for the full 20 PS power gap. Simply swapping a Redtop head onto a Blacktop block will not produce 220 PS — the pistons and ECU calibration are equally important.

4. **The Accord Euro R used the Redtop with a different intake.** While the Prelude Type S received the dual-runner intake manifold, the Accord Euro R (CL1) used a single-runner manifold tuned for a slightly different power band. Both produced 220 PS, but the torque curve differs between the two applications.

5. **Production numbers for the Redtop specifically are not published.** Total 4th-gen Prelude production was approximately 230,000 units worldwide, but the fraction equipped with the 220 PS Redtop (as opposed to the 200 PS Blacktop) is unknown. The Redtop was reserved for top-trim SiR S-Spec and Type S models only.
