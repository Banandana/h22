## Summary

The Honda H22A Redtop is the highest-output naturally aspirated inline-4 engine in Honda's H-series family, producing 220 PS (162 kW; 217 hp) at 7,200 RPM and 221 Nm (163 lb-ft) of torque at 6,500 RPM. Its distinguishing features include an 11.0:1 compression ratio achieved through dome pistons, aggressive camshafts (306° intake / 310° exhaust duration), a 62.5 mm throttle body, dual runner intake manifold, 57 mm exhaust system, and light factory head porting. Built on a closed-deck aluminum block with FRM cylinder liners and 50 mm main journals (pre-1998), the Redtop was exclusive to JDM applications including the Prelude SiR S-Spec, Type S, Accord SiR, and Euro-R/Torneo Euro-R. It achieved Honda's original engineering target of 100 PS/L specific output — meeting the benchmark set by then-president Nobuhiko Kawamoto.

## Notes

### Key Observations

1. **The H22A Redtop achieved 100 PS/L specific output**, meeting Honda president Nobuhiko Kawamoto's original engineering target for the H-series. This was a significant milestone for a mass-produced inline-4 in the early 1990s.

2. **The 11.0:1 compression ratio was unique among mass-produced Honda engines of its era.** Only the later F20C (S2000, 11.3:1) exceeded it. This required premium fuel (100 RON minimum in Japan).

3. **The Redtop's camshaft profile (306°/310°) was significantly more aggressive than the Blacktop (288°/308°).** This accounts for much of the 20 PS power difference between the two variants, particularly at high RPM.

4. **The closed-deck block (1992–1996) makes the Redtop more desirable for boosting** than the later open-deck 1997+ variants. The 50 mm main journals (pre-1998) are also a point of contention — some builders prefer them for shorter crankshaft length.

5. **VTEC engagement varies between 5,200–5,500 RPM across sources.** This likely reflects the load-dependent VTEC window — engagement can occur as low as 5,200 RPM under heavy throttle or as high as 5,500 RPM under light load.

6. **The Redtop was used in touring car racing.** Honda of Europe destroke the H22A block to 2.0L for Formula 3 (1994), then used it in BTCC (1995–1997) and JTCC (1996–1997), winning both championships in 1996 and 1997.

## Conflicts and Ambiguities

1. **Accord SiR (CD6/CF2) power output:** Some sources (Wikipedia, QWEN.md) list the CD6 Accord SiR and CF2 Accord SiR Wagon as receiving the 220 PS Redtop, while others list them at 190 PS. This conflict may reflect different model years within the CD6/CF2 production run — earlier units may have been 190 PS Blacktop, later units upgraded to 220 PS Redtop. Further verification needed against Honda service bulletins.

2. **VTEC engagement RPM:** Sources vary between 5,200 RPM and 5,500 RPM for Redtop VTEC engagement. This likely reflects the load-dependent VTEC window — engagement can occur as low as 5,200 RPM under heavy throttle or as high as 5,500 RPM under light load.

3. **Type S vs. SiR S-Spec distinction:** Both received the 220 PS Redtop. The Type S included ATTS (Active Torque Transfer System) and a hood spoiler; the SiR S-Spec had the same engine but lacked ATTS in most model years. Some sources conflate the two.

4. **Rev limiter:** Wikipedia states "engine rev cut of 9100rpm" for the Type S, while other sources cite 7,200–7,600 RPM. This may reflect different model years or a misunderstanding — the 9,100 RPM figure may apply to a specific tuning mode or may be incorrect.

5. **Piston material:** QWEN.md states "forged aluminum (aftermarket) / cast (stock)" while mywikimotors does not specify. Stock Redtop pistons were likely cast aluminum with a domed crown; forged pistons are aftermarket upgrades. This needs clarification.
