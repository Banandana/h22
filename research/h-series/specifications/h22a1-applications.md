## Summary

The Honda H22A1 engine was deployed across three primary market applications during its 1993–1996 production run: the USDM Prelude VTEC (BB1 chassis), the Canadian Prelude SR-V (BB1 chassis), and the Australian Prelude VTi-R (BB1 chassis). All three markets received the same H22A1 engine — 2,157 cc DOHC 16-valve, 190 PS @ 6,800 rpm, 10.0:1 compression — but with market-specific equipment, trim features, and naming conventions. The USDM VTEC was manual-only at ~$23,000 MSRP; Canada received it as the SR-V with standard heated seats and a larger washer reservoir; Australia received it as the VTi-R with 4WS standard, leather interior, and dual airbags. The VTEC name was shortened from "Si-VTEC" in 1994 across all markets. This document covers model year evolution, feature differentiation, pricing, market positioning, and regional specifications for each application.

### Canada Prelude SR-V (BB1) — 1993–1996

#### Overview

The Canadian-market SR-V was the direct equivalent of the USDM VTEC, using the identical H22A1 engine and BB1 chassis. The "SR" prefix replaced "Si" in Canada (the "S" denoted the base SOHC model, "SR" the mid-range DOHC, and "SR-V" the top-tier DOHC VTEC). Canada received the same vehicle lineup as the US but with minor equipment differences.

- Canadian-market Preludes were very similar to US models overall.

### Australia Prelude VTi-R (BB1) — 1994–1996

Unlike the USDM and Canadian markets where the VTEC was 2WS-only, Australian VTi-R models came with 4WS (four-wheel steering) as standard equipment. The VTi-R replaced the previous generation's Si SRS trim in 1994.

## Conflicts & Uncertencies

1. **Torque figure discrepancy (Australia vs USDM):** drive.com.au lists VTi-R torque at 212 Nm @ 5,250 rpm while USDM sources consistently list 207 Nm @ 5,500 rpm. The difference may reflect different testing conventions (JIS vs SAE) or calibration variations for Australian fuel quality (95 RON premium). The 212 Nm figure appears consistently on Australian spec sheets; the 207 Nm figure appears on USDM spec sheets. Both refer to the same H22A1 engine.

2. **Power output convention:** Wikipedia lists 190 PS (140 kW; 187 hp) for all H22A1 applications. drive.com.au lists 142 kW @ 6,800 rpm for the VTi-R. These are consistent — 190 PS ≈ 140 kW ≈ 187 hp SAE net. The 142 kW figure on drive.com.au may reflect DIN measurement convention (142 kW DIN ≈ 193 PS ≈ 190 PS JIS).

3. **Australian VTi-R weight:** Wikipedia lists 1,268 kg (2,795 lb) for the VTi-R manual. CarExpert's 1996 spec sheet lists 1,325 kg (2,921 lb) as "kerb weight." The difference (57 kg / 126 lb) may reflect different measurement standards (DIN kerb weight vs. actual road-ready weight with fluids). Both figures are cited from different sources.

4. **4WS on VTi-R:** Wikipedia's application table shows the VTi-R with 4WS. CarsGuide confirms 4WS as standard on the VTi-R. However, some sources suggest 4WS was optional rather than standard. The consensus across multiple sources is that 4WS was standard on the VTi-R in Australia, distinguishing it from the USDM VTEC which never offered 4WS.

5. **Automatic transmission on VTi-R:** GoAuto states "A five-speed manual or new four-speed automatic are the transmission choices." drive.com.au also lists both 5MT and 4AT options. However, some enthusiast forums suggest the 4AT was rare on VTi-R models. The 5MT was clearly the preferred choice among enthusiasts.

6. **Heated seats in Australia:** Not explicitly documented for Australian VTi-R models. Heated seats are confirmed as standard on all Canadian Preludes but there is no clear evidence they were standard on Australian models. Australian climate is generally milder than Canada's.

## Notes

### Key Takeaways

1. **Same engine, different packaging.** All three markets (USDM VTEC, Canada SR-V, Australia VTi-R) received the identical H22A1 engine. Differentiation came through trim features, equipment packages, and naming conventions — not engine modifications.

2. **4WS was the biggest mechanical differentiator.** Australian VTi-R models came with 4WS standard, while USDM and Canadian VTEC/SR-V models never offered 4WS. This is the single most significant mechanical difference between the markets.

3. **Weight savings in Australia.** Australian VTi-R models weighed approximately 64 kg (141 lb) less than USDM VTEC models, likely due to different bumper/emissions standards and reduced sound insulation.

4. **Transmission availability.** USDM and Canadian VTEC/SR-V were manual-only. Australian VTi-R offered both 5MT and 4AT, though the 5MT was the enthusiast choice.

5. **Name simplification in 1994.** "Si VTEC" was shortened to "VTEC" starting in 1994 across all markets, reflecting Honda's confidence in the VTEC brand recognition.

6. **Declining sales despite critical acclaim.** The 4th gen Prelude earned seven consecutive C/D Ten Best awards (1992–1998) but sales declined 38.7% from the 3rd to 4th generation, and fell another 41.0% for the 5th generation.
