## Summary

The Honda H22A1 engine debuted in 1993 as the centerpiece of the USDM fourth-generation Prelude VTEC (BB1 chassis), marking Honda's first DOHC VTEC offering in the United States market. The H22A1 produced 190 PS (187 hp SAE net) @ 6,800 rpm and 207 Nm (153 lb-ft) @ 5,500 rpm with a 10.0:1 compression ratio — significantly detuned from its JDM Redtop sibling (H22A, 220 PS @ 11.0:1 CR) to meet US emissions standards and fuel quality realities. The VTEC system architecture was derived from the Acura NSX (first US VTEC exposure in 1991) and the Integra GS-R's SOHC VTEC (1992), but the H22A1 was the first DOHC VTEC engine sold new in America. Regulatory drivers included EPA Tier-0/Tier-1 emissions standards (1993 model year), California LEV requirements, and the need for regular unleaded fuel compatibility (87 AKI minimum). The engine was available only with a 5-speed manual transmission in the US, positioned at approximately $23,000 MSRP — a ~$4,000 premium over the Si trim. Canada received the same engine as the SR-V trim; Australia received it as the VTi-R. The H22A1 remained in production through 1996 before being replaced by the H22A4 (open deck, OBD2) in 1997.

### Why the H22A1 Was Significant

Honda officially stated: "The first Honda-branded vehicle in the U.S. to use a DOHC version of VTEC was the 1993 Honda Prelude VTEC." — [Honda News](https://hondanews.com/en-US/honda-automobiles/releases/release-b50efa689496a1943cdccc46490f00df-vtec), retrieved 2026-05-15.

This positioning was deliberate:

1. **Technology Showcase:** The Prelude was positioned as "the most technologically advanced, well-equipped, and highest performance Honda then available short of the Acura NSX" — MotorTrend. The H22A1 DOHC VTEC was the crown jewel of this positioning.

2. **VTEC Maturity:** By 1993, VTEC had proven itself in the NSX (supercar credibility) and Integra GS-R (mass-market acceptance). The H22A1 represented the next step — applying DOHC VTEC to a larger-displacement, higher-torque application.

3. **Competitive Pressure:** The early 1990s sport compact segment was heating up:
   - Toyota Celica GT-S (3S-GE 2.2L, 145 hp) — competitive but no VTEC equivalent
   - Nissan 240SX (KA24DE 2.4L, 140 hp) — RWD, but lower power density
   - Mazda MX-5 (1.8L, 140 hp) — lightweight RWD, but not direct competitor
   - Mitsubishi Eclipse GS-T (4G63T 2.0L turbo, 145 hp) — forced induction alternative

4. **Performance Gap:** The H22A1's 190 hp from 2.2L (86.4 hp/L specific output) outpaced the Si's H23A1 (160 hp from 2.3L = 69.6 hp/L) by nearly 25%, despite the H23A1 having 100cc more displacement. This demonstrated VTEC's effectiveness.

### VTEC System Architecture (H22A1)

The H22A1 VTEC system uses a three-cam-lobe design: two low-lift/short-duration lobes operate the rocker arms at low-to-mid RPM, while a third high-lift/long-duration lobe engages at approximately 4,800–5,200 rpm. An electronic controller opens a valve to direct engine-oil pressure against a split shaft running through the rocker arms. As oil pressure shifts the split shaft position, it locks the outer rockers (which open the valves) to the center rocker (which rides on the radical cam lobe). Below the engagement threshold, this high-RPM mechanism freewheels harmlessly.

This differs from the Integra GS-R SOHC VTEC, where Road & Track noted the intervention is "almost imperceptible" — the Prelude VTEC produces a distinct "power bump" at the transition point, described by Car and Driver as "a subtle 'power bump' to look forward to at about 4,800 rpm."

*Sources: Car and Driver, Road & Track via PreludePower, Honda official release, retrieved 2026-05-15*

*Sources: ACEEE emissions history document, CARB regulations, Wikipedia H engine, retrieved 2026-05-15*

### Detuning Measures for US Compliance

The H22A1 incorporated several detuning measures compared to the JDM Redtop (H22A):

1. **Compression Ratio Reduction:** 11.0:1 (JDM) → 10.0:1 (USDM). Lower CR reduces combustion temperature and NOx formation, and allows regular unleaded fuel.

2. **Cam Profile Detuning:** Less aggressive cam lobes reduce overlap and improve low-end torque at the expense of top-end power. This also helps emissions by improving combustion stability at part load.

3. **Throttle Body Size:** 62.5 mm (JDM) → 60 mm (USDM). Smaller TB reduces high-RPM airflow, limiting peak power while improving part-load efficiency.

4. **Intake Manifold:** Single runner (USDM) vs dual runner (JDM Type S). Different plenum volume and runner length affect torque curve shape for US driving conditions.

5. **Exhaust Diameter:** 57 mm (JDM) → 51 mm (USDM). Narrower exhaust increases backpressure slightly, trading top-end power for mid-range torque and emissions control.

6. **ECU Calibration:** P51 (manual) / P5M (automatic reference) ECUs feature US-specific fuel/ignition maps optimized for 87 AKI fuel and emissions compliance.

### OBD1 Era Context

The H22A1 operated under OBD1 diagnostic standards (pre-OBDII mandate of 1996). Key OBD1 characteristics:

- **Diagnostic connector:** Round 16-pin + rectangular 16-pin (A/B/D connectors)
- **Trouble codes:** Blink-code based (no standardized P-codes)
- **Fuel system:** PGM-FI with port fuel injection, saturated injectors
- **Emissions equipment:** EGR, PCV, evaporative emission control (EVAP), three-way catalytic converter
- **Oxygen sensors:** Upstream and downstream O2 sensors for closed-loop fuel control

## Conflicts & Uncertainties

1. **HP rating discrepancy:** Car and Driver lists 190 hp @ 6,800 rpm (SAE net) while Wikipedia lists 190 PS (187 hp) @ 6,800 rpm (JIS). Both refer to the same engine; PS is the JIS measurement convention, hp is SAE net. The C&D figure of 190 hp is consistent with SAE net testing.

2. **Torque figure:** Car and Driver lists 158 lb-ft @ 5,300 rpm; Wikipedia lists 153 lb-ft @ 5,500 rpm. The difference may reflect different testing methods (SAE gross vs. net) or different calibration versions across model years. The C&D figure of 158 lb-ft is from their own testing of a pre-production unit.

3. **VTEC engagement RPM:** Car and Driver states the VTEC mechanism engages "about 4,800 rpm" while later sources cite 5,200–5,500 rpm. The system is variable by throttle position — the exact threshold depends on load. The 5,500 rpm figure commonly cited applies to heavier throttle loads; lighter throttle may engage earlier (~4,800 rpm).

4. **VTEC pricing:** The "~$23,000" figure is from Car and Driver's estimate ("about $23,000 (from just under $20,000 for last year's Prelude Si)"). Exact MSRP figures for the 1993 VTEC are not consistently documented in public sources.

5. **Dealer training specifics:** No publicly available documentation exists for Acura dealer training programs related to the 1993 Prelude VTEC. Honda Verno's specialized training in Japan had no US equivalent.

6. **1993 radiator change:** Honda-Tech forum notes that 1993 received a different radiator and fan design "for no specific reason" (credit: Glenn Shiu). The reason is undocumented.

7. **Automatic transmission availability:** Multiple sources confirm the VTEC was manual-only. However, some sources reference "P5M (automatic)" ECU — this appears to be an internal Honda designation for the A/T variant ECU that was never actually deployed in a production VTEC vehicle. The P5M may have been developed for a planned A/T VTEC that was cancelled.
