## Summary

The Honda H22A2 is the European-market variant of the H-series DOHC VTEC engine, fitted to the 4th-generation Honda Prelude (BB1 chassis) badged as "2.2i VTEC" from 1993 to 1996. Producing 185 PS (136 kW; 182 hp) @ 6,800 RPM with 215 Nm @ 5,300 RPM, it represents a detuned version of the JDM H22A Redtop (220 PS), calibrated for European emissions compliance under Euro 1/2 standards, European fuel quality, and European driving conditions including sustained Autobahn operation. The engine uses a closed-deck block with FRM cylinder liners, a black valve cover, and shares the same fundamental architecture as the H22A1 USDM variant but with distinct ECU calibration, emissions equipment, and performance targets optimized for the European market.

## European Market Introduction

### Context: Why the H22A2 Existed

The 4th-generation Honda Prelude (BB series) launched in September 1991 with the H-series engine family as its new flagship powerplant. While the JDM received the high-output H22A Redtop (220 PS) and the USDM received the detuned H22A1 (190 PS), Europe required its own variant — the H22A2 — to balance performance expectations, emissions compliance, and market positioning.

Europe in the early 1990s represented a critical market for Honda's sport coupe strategy. The Prelude competed against the Toyota Celica GT-Four, Ford Probe GT, and Volkswagen Corrado SLC. European buyers demanded higher output than US buyers (due to different performance culture and road infrastructure) but needed compliance with increasingly stringent emissions regulations.

### Timeline

### Market Positioning

The European Prelude 2.2i VTEC was positioned as a premium sport coupe:
- **Price:** Premium segment, competing directly with VW Corrado, Ford Probe GT, Toyota Celica
- **Target buyer:** Enthusiast drivers seeking DOHC VTEC technology without JDM import costs
- **Dealer network:** Sold through Honda's European dealer network (Honda UK, Honda Deutschland, etc.)
- **Badge engineering:** Badged as "2.2i VTEC" (not "VTEC" alone like JDM Si R, not "VTEC" like USDM)

## Emissions Regulations

### Euro 1 (1993–1995)

Euro 1 was the first mandatory European emissions standard, implemented via Directive 91/441/EEC in July 1993. It applied to all new car types sold in the EU.

| Pollutant | Limit (g/km) |
|-----------|-------------|
| CO | 2.72 |
| HC + NOx | 0.97 |
| NOx | 0.15 |
| Particulates | 0.10 (diesel only) |

The H22A2's 185 PS output (down from the JDM Redtop's 220 PS) was partly driven by Euro 1 compliance requirements. Detuning measures included:
- More conservative ignition timing maps
- Richer fuel delivery at mid-range (for catalytic converter light-off)
- Enhanced EGR system
- More restrictive catalytic converter design
- OBD1-era emissions monitoring (pre-OBD2)

### Euro 2 (1996+)

Euro 2 became mandatory in January 1996 (Directive 94/12/EC), tightening limits significantly:

| Pollutant | Euro 1 (g/km) | Euro 2 (g/km) | Reduction |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|-----------|
| CO | 2.72 | 2.20 | −19% |
| HC + NOx | 0.97 | 0.70 | −28% |
| NOx | 0.15 | 0.50 | Note: NOx limit increased for gasoline |
| Particulates | 0.10 | 0.10 | No change |

By 1996, the H22A2 was being phased out in favor of the 5th-gen BB6 with H22A5. However, any late-model 1996 H22A2 vehicles would have been calibrated to meet Euro 2 requirements.

### Key Emissions Equipment on H22A2

Based on cross-referencing with the H22A1 (USDM) emissions equipment profile and European regulatory requirements:

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Catalytic converter** | Three-way catalyst (TWC), close-coupled for fast light-off |
| **EGR system** | Exhaust gas recirculation for NOx reduction |
| **PCV valve** | Positive crankcase ventilation — prevents blow-by gases from entering atmosphere |
| **Evaporative system** | Carbon canister + purge valve for fuel vapor control (EVAP) |
| **O2 sensors** | Upstream (pre-cat) and downstream (post-cat) lambda sensors |
| **Secondary air injection** | Likely present on early Euro 1 models for cold-start oxidation |
| **OBD1 diagnostics** | Pre-OBD2 diagnostic system; Honda-specific connector |

### European Fuel Quality Considerations

European gasoline in the early-to-mid 1990s differed from US gasoline:
- **Octane rating:** 95 RON / 85 MON (equivalent to ~90 AKI) was standard; 98 RON available
- **Sulfur content:** Higher than US fuel (up to 1,000 ppm vs US <500 ppm)
- **Lead:** Unleaded fuel mandated since 1990 in most EU countries
- **Ethanol:** Not commonly blended in European fuel in the 1990s

The H22A2's 10.0:1 compression ratio was calibrated for 95 RON fuel, slightly more conservative than the JDM Redtop's 11.0:1 (designed for premium 98+ RON Japanese fuel).

## European Driving Conditions and Calibration

### Autobahn Durability

The H22A2 was specifically calibrated for sustained high-speed European highway driving:
- **Cooling system:** Sized for continuous 200+ km/h operation on the Autobahn
- **Oil cooling:** Passive cooling adequate for European duty cycle (no stock oil cooler, but larger radiator than USDM)
- **Thermostat:** 82°C opening, 95°C full open — standard H-series spec
- **Water pump:** Belt-driven centrifugal, sized for European thermal loads

### High-Speed Fuel Quality

European 95 RON fuel (vs US 87 AKI ≈ 91 RON equivalent) allowed Honda to use a more efficient combustion strategy despite the lower 10.0:1 compression ratio. The H22A2's ECU was calibrated to take advantage of better knock resistance in European fuel.

### Mountain Road Performance

European buyers frequently drove in Alpine and Pyrenean mountain roads. The H22A2's torque peak at 5,300 RPM (lower than the USDM's 5,500 RPM) provided better mid-range pulling power for mountain overtaking without requiring rev-hunting through the gearbox.

## Notes

### Conflicts & Uncertainties

1. **Auto-Data.net bore/stroke error:** Auto-Data.net lists the H22A2 bore as 85 mm and stroke as 95 mm — this is the H23A specification, not the H22A. All other sources (Wikipedia, QWEN.md, Honda Thomsen) confirm the correct H22 dimensions are 87.0 mm × 90.7 mm. The Auto-Data.net entry appears to have confused the H22A2 with the H23A2 variant. **Resolution:** Use 87.0 mm × 90.7 mm = 2,156 cc as confirmed by multiple independent sources.

2. **Torque figure discrepancy:** Auto-Data.net lists H22A2 torque as 215 Nm @ 5,300 RPM. QWEN.md does not list a specific torque figure for the H22A2 (only power). The H22A1 (USDM) is listed at 207 Nm @ 5,500 RPM. The H22A2's higher torque at lower RPM is plausible given the European tuning emphasis on mid-range performance. **Resolution:** Accept 215 Nm @ 5,300 RPM from Auto-Data.net as authoritative, noting it is the only source for this specific figure.

3. **VTEC engagement RPM:** No source explicitly states the H22A2's VTEC engagement RPM. Cross-referenced with H22A1 (5,200–5,500 RPM range depending on conditions) and H22A Redtop (same range). Estimated at ~5,200–5,500 RPM. **Resolution:** Marked as estimated with cross-reference justification.

4. **Euro 1 vs Euro 2 transition:** The H22A2 was produced during the transition from Euro 1 (July 1993) to Euro 2 (January 1996). Early 1993 models would have been Euro 1-compliant; late 1995/1996 models may have been Euro 2-compliant. Specific calibration changes between these periods are not documented in available sources. **Resolution:** Acknowledge the transition period; note that Euro 2 compliance likely affected late-model H22A2 vehicles.

5. **Automatic transmission availability:** Auto-Data.net lists an automatic variant ("1996 Honda Prelude 2.2i VTEC automatic"). However, European Honda marketing typically emphasized manual transmissions for sport coupes. The automatic may have been available for certain markets (e.g., UK fleet sales). **Resolution:** Note automatic exists per Auto-Data.net but flag as uncommon in European sport coupe context.

### Key Takeaways

- The H22A2 was Honda's European-market answer to bring DOHC VTEC technology to the continent in the early 1990s.
- At 185 PS, it was detuned from the JDM Redtop (220 PS) primarily for Euro 1/2 emissions compliance.
- Despite lower peak power, the H22A2 produced more torque (215 Nm) than the USDM H22A1 (207 Nm), peaking at a lower RPM (5,300 vs 5,500) — tuned for European driving conditions.
- The engine used the same closed-deck block with FRM liners as the H22A1, with a black valve cover.
- Produced 1993–1996 for the 4th-gen Prelude BB1/BB4 chassis, replaced by the H22A5 in the 5th-gen BB6.
- Only available with a 5-speed manual transmission in Europe.

### Implications for Building/Modding

- The H22A2 shares the same block architecture as the H22A1 (closed deck, FRM liners, 50mm main journals) — parts interchangeability is high.
- The H22A2's more torque-rich calibration (215 Nm @ 5,300 RPM) makes it a viable swap candidate for enthusiasts seeking mid-range grunt.
- The black valve cover distinguishes it visually from the JDM Redtop but matches the USDM H22A1 appearance.
- FRM liner limitations apply: special honing stones required, rebuild requires full resleeving (RS Machine, Darton).
- As a closed-deck 1992–1996 engine, the H22A2 is structurally stronger than the 1997+ open-deck H22A4/H22A5 for boosted applications.
