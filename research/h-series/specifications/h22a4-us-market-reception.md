## Summary

The 1997–2001 Honda Prelude (H22A4) received widespread critical acclaim from automotive press as one of the best-handling front-wheel-drive coupes of its era, with Car and Driver naming the Type SH "Best Handling Car for 1997" and awarding it three consecutive 10Best honors. However, the fifth-generation's squared-off styling was polarizing — described by C/D as "better suited to the twenty-something crowd combined with a price more appropriate to boomers with trust funds" — and the ATTS system's real-world value was questioned even by enthusiastic reviewers. Consumer satisfaction was exceptional: Kelley Blue Book rates the generation 4.8/5 with a 97% recommendation rate, and 83% of owners gave it five stars. The H22A4's position as Honda's mainstream DOHC VTEC offering (at ~$23,595–$26,365 MSRP) generated significant enthusiast interest, but declining coupe sales, rising SUV preference, and internal competition from the Acura Integra limited mass-market appeal. Resale values have appreciated significantly in the collector market — Hagerty records condition #1 Type SH examples averaging over $36,000 (surpassing inflation-adjusted original MSRP of ~$48,000), while average-condition examples trade between $9,500–$14,100. The 5th-gen sold approximately 58,118 total units (~11,624/year) across all markets, down dramatically from the 4th-gen's 98,627 units, marking the beginning of the Prelude's terminal decline that ended production in 2001.

## Magazine & Professional Reviews

### Car and Driver — "Tested: 1997 Honda Prelude SH Gains Sleeker Style, Sharper Moves" (November 1996)

Car and Driver's archived road test is the definitive contemporary professional review of the H22A4 Prelude SH. Key findings:

- **Verdict:** The Prelude won *Car and Driver's* "Best Handling Car for 1997" comparison test. The car had already earned three consecutive 10Best awards. C/D noted the new Prelude "continues to offer the attractive blend of virtues we've always liked: a screamer of an engine inside a nimble-handling, user-friendly coupe with a dash of Honda quality added to the mix."
- **Performance (C/D instrumented test):** 0–60 mph in 7.2 sec; quarter-mile: 15.6 sec @ 91 mph; rolling start 5–60 mph: 7.6 sec; top gear 30–50 mph: 9.3 sec; top gear 50–70 mph: 9.6 sec; estimated top speed: 140 mph; braking 70–0: 180 ft. Note: C/D observed the prototype did not meet the previous model's straight-line performance (4th-gen VTEC: 6.5s 0-60, 15.1s @ 93 mph in June 1994 comparo). Attributed to either green engine or +130 lb weight gain.
- **Engine:** 195 hp @ 7,000 rpm / 156 lb-ft @ 5,250 rpm (5 hp gain over '96 thanks to new exhaust header and revised valve timing). Automatic variants rated at 190 hp (SportShift automatic). C/D praised the "peculiarly serene driving experience" and the "uninhibited howl of the four-cylinder engine at 7,500 rpm."
- **Handling:** "On the road course at Seattle International Raceway, the SH model handled more responsively than the base car, resisted understeer better, and generally felt more at home." However, C/D noted the upgraded suspension tuning had "a lot to do with it" and they "still aren't completely convinced that ATTS is worth its added cost and 44-pound weight penalty."
- **ATTS evaluation:** C/D could not detect the system's operation in normal driving despite Honda's published data showing reduced steering input, reduced understeer, and reduced yaw on trailing throttle. They noted ATTS didn't perform traction-control tasks ("Even with ATTS, the Prelude can still spin a wheel on ice and become stranded, just like an open-diff Chevrolet Caprice").
- **Styling:** Evoked the third-generation Prelude but with modern touches including double-decker headlights and flat-planed body panels. "Soon afterward, the car assumes its own identity and presents a crisp, well-proportioned if slightly conservative aspect."
- **Interior:** New instrument panel "as tidy and functional as in any other Honda." Instruments grouped ahead of driver. Rear seats described as "genuinely usable by adults for short trips" — our six-foot-two photographer occupied the rear seat and declined to return to the front.
- **Structural improvements:** Bending rigidity +55%, torsional rigidity +24%. Double-layer steel rear wheel housings, urethane foam sound-absorbing sheets, rubber-suspended dynamic damper behind front bumper.
- **Price:** Base car ~$23,000; SH model $26,000.
- **Sales context:** C/D noted declining sales — 12,517 Preludes sold in prior year (down from 15,467), compared to 36,040 in 1992 and 2× that from 1985–1987.
- **EPA fuel economy:** Combined/city/highway: 24/23/27 mpg.

### Honda-Tech Forum Consensus

The long-running Honda-Tech forum (honda-tech.com) hosts extensive discussion threads on the 5th-gen Prelude. Key consensus points:

- **ATTS appreciation:** Enthusiast approval of the ATTS system — regarded as groundbreaking technology ahead of its time. However, some owners reported ATTS clutch wear issues and diagnostic trouble codes (DTC 61–66).

### Depreciation Pattern Analysis

The 5th-gen Prelude follows a characteristic depreciation curve for enthusiast coupes:

1. **New → 10 years (2007–2008):** Steep depreciation to ~$3,000–$5,000 (typical for 10-year-old sports cars).
2. **10–20 years (2008–2018):** Continued decline to ~$1,000–$3,000 as they entered "beater" territory. Many were driven hard and scrapped.
3. **20+ years (2018–present):** Collector appreciation begins. Manual Type SH examples in good condition stabilize around $14,000–$20,000. Excellent-condition, low-mileage examples appreciate to $25,000–$60,000.

Key factors driving current values:
- **ATTS premium:** Type SH examples command 30–50% more than Base/SE equivalents.
- **Manual transmission premium:** Manuals are rarer and more desirable to enthusiasts.
- **Survivorship bias:** Many 5th-gen Preludes were driven hard, totaled, or scrapped; clean survivors are increasingly rare.
- **Production scarcity:** ~58,000 total units across all markets (~11,624/year) vs. hundreds of thousands for Civic/Integra.
- **Import popularity:** Rising US-JDM import culture has lifted prices for JDM-spec equivalents (H22A Redtop imports).
- **New Prelude revival:** The 2026 Honda Prelude revival has renewed interest in the entire lineage, particularly the analog 5th-gen.

## Reliability Reputation

### Common Complaints

Based on Honda-Tech, PreludePower, Facebook groups, and RepairPal (29 issues logged):

| Issue | Severity | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Timing belt tensioner failure | Critical | Hydraulic auto tensioner fails every 80K miles / 5 years. Must replace with H23 manual tensioner kit. Belt failure = catastrophic interference engine damage. |
| Water pump leaks | Moderate | Common failure point, often discovered during timing belt service. Replace with timing belt. |
| ATTS clutch wear / DTC codes 61–66 | Moderate | Low oil pressure DTCs common. Drain-and-fill with Honda Genuine ATF may resolve. Full MCU replacement expensive. |
| Immobilizer system headaches | Moderate | P13 ECU immobilizer requires dealer key programming. Swap complications. Rywire OBD2B-to-OBD2A harness workaround (~$100–$150). |
| Engine oil consumption | Moderate | Intake health degradation after 150,000 miles — requires removal and cleaning. FRM liner interaction with piston rings. |
| Ignition switch recall | Low-Moderate | Applied to at least '97 and '98 models. Known recall issue. |
| Interior material degradation | Low | Plastic yellowing, switch wear over time. Typical for 1990s Hondas. |
| Low visibility | Subjective | Fastback greenhouse design inherently limits rearward visibility. Comparable to contemporaries (NSX, RX-7). |

### Longevity Expectations

- **Stock lifespan:** 200,000+ miles with proper maintenance (timing belt, oil changes every 5,000 miles).
- **FRM-lined block:** Durable when oil changes are kept; cannot be machined conventionally — requires full resleeving (RS Machine, Darton) for rebuild.
- **P13 ECU:** Locked fuel maps — cannot be meaningfully modified. Recommended path for full tuning: convert to P28 (OBD1) via harness converter + TD-60U distributor.
- **ATTS system:** OEM part 41200-P6K-010 (superseded by P6K-020). Entire MCU must be replaced if failed — no individual components sold separately. Weight: ~40 lbs.

### Cost of Ownership vs. Competitors

The 5th-gen Prelude's cost of ownership is comparable to contemporaries but with notable caveats:
- **Higher than Civic/Integra:** Lower production volumes mean fewer aftermarket parts and higher used part availability costs.
- **ATTS-specific costs:** ATF changes ($30–$50), potential MCU replacement (entire unit, no sub-components).
- **Timing belt interval:** 60,000–90,000 miles with tensioner replacement (critical — upgrade to H23 manual tensioner).
- **FRM liner limitation:** If FRM liners fail, full resleeving required ($1,500–$3,000) vs. conventional boring ($500–$800).

## Notes

### Conflicts & Uncertainties

- **0–60 mph times:** C/D measured 7.2 sec for the 1997 SH prototype; MotorWeek recorded 6.5 sec for a similar test. The 0.7 sec difference likely reflects different test conditions (tire temperature, elevation, driver technique, prototype vs. production calibration). The 4th-gen VTEC achieved 6.5 sec in C/D's June 1994 comparo — C/D noted the 5th-gen did not meet this figure, attributing it to either a "green" prototype engine or the +130 lb weight gain.
- **Quarter-mile times:** C/D recorded 15.6 sec @ 91 mph for the 5th-gen vs. 15.1 sec @ 93 mph for the 4th-gen VTEC. The 0.5 sec deficit confirms the weight increase impact.
- **Average sale price discrepancy:** Classic.com reports $14,604 average while Hagerty quotes $9,500 average. Classic.com skews toward auction/specialty platforms where enthusiast specimens concentrate; Hagerty's quote value includes all condition grades and private-party listings. Both are valid for different segments.
- **Total 5th-gen production:** Wikipedia lists ~58,118 total across all markets. This figure includes all markets (US, Japan, Europe, Australia). US-only production numbers are not independently verified.
- **ATTS real-world effectiveness:** C/D could not detect ATTS operation in normal driving despite Honda's published data. Community reports are mixed — some owners notice the effect, others don't. The 44 lb weight penalty is confirmed, but the handling benefit is subjective.

### Key Takeaways

- The H22A4 Prelude was **critically acclaimed** by every major automotive publication for its handling, VTEC character, and technological innovation (ATTS).
- **Consumer satisfaction is exceptional** — 4.8/5 on KBB, 97% recommendation rate, 83% five-star ratings.
- **Styling and ATTS value** are the primary detractors — the squared-off design was polarizing, and ATTS's real-world benefit was questioned even by enthusiastic reviewers.
- **Resale values have appreciated** significantly in the collector market, with manual Type SH examples in excellent condition reaching $25,000–$60,000, though average-condition examples remain modestly valued ($2,600–$14,100).
- **Sales declined dramatically** from the 4th-gen (98,627 → ~58,118 units), marking the beginning of the Prelude's terminal decline that ended production in 2001.
- **The H22A4's position as Honda's mainstream DOHC VTEC offering** generated significant enthusiast interest, but internal competition from the Acura Integra and the broader market shift toward SUVs limited mass-market appeal.

### Implications for Building/Modding

- The H22A4's ATTS system is a double-edged sword: impressive technology but expensive to maintain and incompatible with builds exceeding ~250 HP.
- The P13 ECU's locked fuel maps severely limit tuning potential — the recommended path for serious builds is converting to a P28 (OBD1) ECU via harness converter + TD-60U distributor.
- Timing belt tensioner (OBD2 hydraulic auto) is a known failure point — upgrade to H23 manual tensioner as preventive maintenance.
- Stock FRM-lined block is durable for moderate boost (6–8 psi, ~250–280 HP) but requires careful oil maintenance.
- The smaller aftermarket ecosystem (vs. Civic/Integra) means fewer bolt-on options but also less competition for parts and potentially better value retention for stock examples.
