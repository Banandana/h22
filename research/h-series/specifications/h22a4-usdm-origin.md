## Summary

The H22A4 is the sole engine offered in the USDM fifth-generation Honda Prelude (BB6 chassis, 1997–2001). It represents a significant engineering transition from the fourth-gen H22A1: Honda moved from a closed-deck to an open-deck aluminum block, increased main journal diameter from 50 mm to 55 mm (1998+), and adopted OBD2 emissions management with immobilizer integration. The 5th-gen Prelude was Honda's attempt to revitalize a declining sports coupe nameplate through aggressive styling (return to angular third-gen-inspired design), the debut of ATTS torque-vectoring on the Type SH trim, and a simplified single-engine lineup. However, internal competition from the Accord Coupe and Integra, combined with rising prices ($23,595 MSRP base) and shifting consumer preferences toward SUVs, led to declining sales — just 58,118 units sold over five generations in the US, down from 98,627 for the 4th gen. The Prelude was discontinued after 2001, making the H22A4 the last DOHC VTEC engine offered in a USDM Honda production car until the K20A returned in the 2026 Prelude revival.

## 5th Generation Prelude (BB6) — Chassis Introduction

### Design Philosophy and Styling

The fifth-generation Prelude (chassis codes BB5–BB9) arrived for the 1997 model year, produced from November 1996 through October 2001. Designer Manabu Konaka led the project starting in 1993. The styling represented a deliberate return to the angular, squared-off proportions of the first three generations of Prelude, moving away from the softer, more rounded "liquefied" sheet metal of the controversial fourth generation (1992–1996). Fixed headlights replaced pop-ups (first done in the 4th gen), and the overall silhouette balanced retro cues with late-1990s aerodynamic refinement.

The BB6 chassis was 1,780 mm (70.1 in) long, 1,750 mm (68.9 in) wide, 1,315 mm (51.8 in) tall, with a 2,585 mm (101.8 in) wheelbase — slightly longer than the 4th-gen BB1 (which measured approximately 1,760 mm long). Curb weight ranged from 1,340 kg (2,954 lb) for the Base model to 1,380 kg (3,042 lb) for the Type SH. Weight distribution was 63% front / 37% rear.

The drag coefficient was 0.30 (noted as 0.6 in one enthusiast source, which appears to be a typo).

The USDM lineup was dramatically simplified compared to previous generations. Only two trims were offered: **Base** and **Type SH**. Both used the H22A4 engine. This was a strategic cost-cutting measure — Honda consolidated the entire USDM lineup under a single powertrain.

The Type SH ("Super Handling") was the flagship USDM trim, featuring Honda's world-first production Active Torque Transfer System (ATTS), an electromechanical torque-vectoring system that sent additional torque to the outside front wheel during cornering. ATTS added approximately $1,500 to the base price and a small amount of weight.

### Production Timeline

- **Launch:** November 1996 (1997 model year)
- **Facelift:** 1999 model year (5 hp increase on manual models: 195 → 200 hp; automatic remained at 195 hp)
- **End of Production:** October 2001
- **Total USDM Sales:** ~58,118 units (all markets combined, 1997–2001)
- **Annual Sales Decline:** From peak ~15,000/year (1997–1998) to ~3,500 (first five months of 2001)

## H22A4 Engine — Engineering Specifications and Origin

### Powertrain Overview

The H22A4 is a 2.2-liter (2,157 cc) DOHC 16-valve VTEC inline-four. It is derived from the H22A1 (USDM 4th gen) but incorporates several key changes reflecting the transition to the 5th-gen platform:

| Parameter | H22A1 (4th gen, 1993–1996) | H22A4 (5th gen, 1997–2001) | Change |
|-----------|---------------------------|---------------------------|--------|
| Displacement | 2,157 cc | 2,157 cc | None |
| Bore × Stroke | 87.0 × 90.7 mm | 87.0 × 90.7 mm | None |
| Compression Ratio | 10.0:1 | 10.0:1 | None |
| Power (1997–1999) | 190 PS (187 hp) @ 6,800 RPM | 195–198 PS (195 hp) @ 7,000 RPM | +5–8 hp, higher RPM |
| Power (1999–2001) | 190 PS (187 hp) @ 6,800 RPM | 200 PS (197 hp) @ 7,000 RPM (M/T) | +10 hp |
| Torque | 207 Nm (153 lb-ft) @ 5,500 RPM | 212 Nm (156 lb-ft) @ 5,250 RPM | +5 Nm, lower RPM peak |
| Block Deck Type | Closed deck | Open deck | Major structural change |
| Main Journal Diameter | 50 mm (1993–1996) | 50 mm (1997 only), 55 mm (1998+) | 1998+ increase |
| Valve Cover | Black | Black | Same color coding |
| ECU | P5M (AT) / P51 (MT), OBD1 | P13, OBD2, immobilizer | Major electronics upgrade |
| Intake | Single runner, 60 mm TB | Single runner, 62 mm TB | Slightly larger TB |
| Exhaust | 51 mm | 51 mm | Same |
| VTEC Engagement | ~5,200–5,500 RPM | 5,250 RPM | Similar threshold |
| Redline | 7,200 RPM | 7,400 RPM | +200 RPM |
| Cam Profiles | 288° IN / 308° EX, 11.38/10.54 mm lift | 288° IN / 308° EX, 11.38/10.54 mm lift | Same |

### Key Engineering Changes from H22A1 to H22A4

**1. Open Deck Block Transition (Major)**

The most significant structural change was the transition from closed-deck to open-deck block design. The 4th-gen H22A1 used a closed-deck block with solid material rings at the top of each cylinder bore, providing lateral support and torsional rigidity. The 5th-gen H22A4 switched to an open-deck design where coolant jackets fully encircle each cylinder sleeve from the bottom of the bore to the top of the block.

Reasons for the switch:
- **Improved cooling efficiency:** Open deck allows coolant to make full contact with the upper portion of the cylinder wall, improving heat dissipation from the combustion chamber area
- **Manufacturing cost reduction:** Open deck blocks are simpler and cheaper to cast
- **Weight savings:** Less material in the block structure (though marginal — typically 1–2 kg difference)
- **Sleeving compatibility:** Open deck blocks are easier and cheaper to resleeve for forced induction builds

Community consensus (PreludePower, Honda-Tech, EngineLabs): For stock and moderately modified NA applications, the open deck H22A4 is perfectly adequate and arguably cools better. The closed-deck advantage is primarily relevant for high-boost forced induction applications (>200 WHP with boost), where cylinder wall rigidity under extreme cylinder pressures matters. Most H22A4 owners never exceed the strength limits of the open deck — the ringlands typically fail before the sleeves.

**2. Main Journal Increase (1998+)**

- 1997 H22A4: 50 mm main journals (same as H22A1)
- 1998–2001 H22A4: 55 mm main journals (+5 mm increase)

The 1998 main journal increase was correlated with the open deck transition and likely driven by:
- Greater bearing surface area to compensate for reduced deck rigidity
- Improved oil film stability under higher loads
- Standardization across the H/F-series family (H23A DOHC VTEC also used 55 mm mains)

This required different main bearings (19.9 mm width maintained) and crankshaft. Bearing part numbers changed accordingly.

**3. ECU and Electronics Upgrade**

The H22A4 uses the **P13 ECU** (OBD2), replacing the H22A1's P5M/P51 (OBD1). Key differences:

- **Immobilizer integration (1997+):** The P13 includes a transponder-based immobilizer system, adding anti-theft security but complicating swaps and tuning
- **Different memory architecture:** The P13 uses K-series-like EEPROM architecture rather than the H8/300 microcontroller family of OBD1 ECUs. Fuel maps cannot be meaningfully modified even if the chip is removable
- **OBD2 diagnostics:** Standard OBDII connector, live data streams, readiness monitors
- **Tuning limitations:** Hondata S300 NOT compatible with P13; FlashPro has no official H22 P13 support. Recommended path for full tuning: convert to P28 (OBD1) via harness converter + TD-60U distributor

**4. Intake System Refinement**

- Throttle body increased from 60 mm (H22A1) to 62 mm (H22A4)
- Single runner plenum maintained (vs. Redtop's dual runner)
- IAB (Intake Air Bypass) secondary butterflies activate at 4,900 RPM
- Head chamber volume: 53.8 cc

**5. Power Output Evolution**

- **1997–1999:** 195 hp @ 7,000 RPM / 156 lb-ft @ 5,250 RPM (automatic: 195 hp / 156 lb-ft)
- **1999–2001 (facelift):** Manual transmission models gained +5 hp → 200 hp @ 7,000 RPM / 156 lb-ft @ 5,250 RPM. Automatic remained at 195 hp

The 1999 power bump is attributed to minor ECU recalibration and possibly improved intake/exhaust flow from the facelift.

## Market Positioning and Context

### Competitive Landscape (1997)

The 5th-gen Prelude entered a crowded and increasingly hostile competitive environment:

| Competitor | 1997 MSRP | Power | Notable Features |
|------------|-----------|-------|------------------|
| Honda Prelude Base | $23,595 | 195 hp | ATTS (SH), VTEC, 4WS (foreign) |
| Honda Prelude Type SH | $26,095 | 195 hp | ATTS torque-vectoring |
| Toyota Celica GT-S | ~$22,000 | 190 hp | VVT-i, proven platform |
| Mazda MX-6 | ~$22,000 | 160 hp | 4WS available |
| Ford Cougar | ~$22,000 | 147–200 hp | V6 options |
| Eagle Talon TSi AWD | ~$24,000 | 185–210 hp (turbo) | AWD, turbo options |
| Subaru SVX | ~$30,000 | 230 hp | AWD, flat-6 |
| Nissan 240SX | ~$23,000 | 200 hp | RWD, swap-friendly |

The Prelude's unique selling proposition was the ATTS torque-vectoring system on the Type SH trim — Honda claimed it provided "rear-wheel drive-like handling while maintaining a front-wheel drivetrain." Car and Driver tested the Prelude SH in their annual "Best-Handling Car for Less Than $30,000" comparison and crowned it the winner, beating BMW 3-Series, Chevrolet Camaro, and Mazda MX-5 Miata.

However, the Prelude faced serious headwinds:
1. **Internal cannibalization:** The Acura Integra GS-R (1997–2001) offered similar driving dynamics at a lower price point. The Accord Coupe (introduced 1998) offered more space and practicality
2. **SUV shift:** Consumer preferences were shifting toward sport utility vehicles
3. **Price creep:** The Type SH at $26,095 was approaching entry-level luxury territory
4. **Single-engine limitation:** Unlike competitors offering V6 or turbo options, the Prelude had only one engine choice

### Why the Prelude Died

Multiple factors converged to end the Prelude after 2001:

1. **Acura brand strategy:** Honda was consolidating its performance offerings under Acura. The Integra Type R (DC2/DC5) and upcoming S2000 (1999) absorbed the enthusiast positioning
2. **Accord Coupe competition:** The 1998+ Accord Coupe offered V6 power (200 hp), more interior space, and a lower price — directly competing with the Base Prelude
3. **Market shift to SUVs/crossovers:** The 1990s saw explosive growth in the SUV segment; sports coupes became a niche
4. **Complexity vs. simplicity:** The ATTS system was expensive (~$1,500 option) and rarely chosen. Most buyers opted for the simpler Base model, negating the Prelude's key differentiator
5. **Economics:** Development costs for a low-volume niche coupe could not be justified against the growing SUV margin opportunity

Car and Driver's 1997 long-term test wrap-up captured the sentiment: the Prelude SH won "Best-Handling Car for Less Than $30,000," but the interior received "high marks for functionality and low marks for cautious styling." The car was mechanically excellent but aesthetically unexciting — a compromise that resonated with enthusiasts but failed to attract mainstream buyers.

### Enthusiast Consensus

Community forums (Honda-Tech, PreludePower, PreludeOnline) consistently rate the 5th-gen Prelude highly among enthusiasts — far more beloved now than at launch. Key themes:

- **Driving dynamics:** The combination of H22A4 power, double-wishbone suspension, and (on SH) ATTS produces exceptional handling for a FWD sports coupe
- **Reliability:** The 5th gen is considered the most reliable Prelude generation (per CarBuzz reliability analysis), with 2000 being the least problematic model year
- **Collector appreciation:** Values have appreciated significantly. Classic.com reports average fifth-gen sale prices around $13,600, with pristine examples reaching $17,000–$21,000
- **Import legality:** All 5th-gen Preludes are now 25-year exempt and legal to import to the US, increasing availability of JDM variants (SiR, Type S)

### Historical Significance

The H22A4 holds a unique place in Honda history:

1. **Last DOHC VTEC in a USDM Honda until 2026:** The Prelude was discontinued in 2001; the next USDM Honda with DOHC VTEC didn't arrive until the 2026 Prelude revival (hybrid variant)
2. **Engineering showcase:** The 5th-gen Prelude was Honda's technological calling card — ATTS, VTEC, 4WS (in foreign markets), and advanced suspension geometry packed into a FWD sports coupe
3. **Open deck legacy:** The H22A4's open-deck block design influenced subsequent Honda engines, including the K-series family
4. **Enthusiast icon:** Despite commercial failure, the 5th-gen Prelude has achieved cult status among JDM enthusiasts, particularly the Type SH with ATTS

## Notes

### Conflicts & Uncertainties

1. **Power output discrepancy:** Sources vary between 195 hp and 200 hp for the H22A4. Resolution: 1997–1999 models were rated at 195 hp @ 7,000 RPM; 1999–2001 facelift manual models gained +5 hp to 200 hp @ 7,000 RPM. Automatic transmission models remained at 195 hp throughout. Wikipedia lists 195 hp (USDM) and 200 PS (Australian VTi-R), consistent with this timeline.

2. **Compression ratio:** QWEN.md and Wikipedia list 10.0:1. The Honda-Tech forum post by Accordian47 lists 10.1:1. The 10.0:1 figure is consistent across all OEM sources and the Helms manual; the 10.1:1 may reflect measured (not spec) values on a specific engine. Using 10.0:1 as authoritative.

3. **Main journal diameter:** Wikipedia states "55 mm (1997+)" but QWEN.md notes "1997-only H22A4 had 50mm main journals; 1998+ had 55mm." Community consensus on Honda-Tech and PreludePower confirms 1997 models retained 50 mm mains (shared casting with 4th gen), with the 55 mm increase arriving in 1998. Using 50 mm (1997) / 55 mm (1998+) as correct.

4. **Drag coefficient:** K Street Studio lists 0.6, which is clearly a typo (should be 0.30 for a modern sports coupe). No OEM source confirmed the exact Cd value.

5. **H22A4 vs H22A5 mechanical differences:** JustAnswer expert states they differ mainly in "intake, exhaust, and ECU tuning." Community consensus (PreludePower) confirms this — the H22A4 and H22A5 share the same block, head, valvetrain, and internals. Differences are limited to ECU calibration, emissions equipment, and minor intake/exhaust routing to satisfy regional standards.

6. **Open deck vs closed deck strength debate:** Community consensus (PreludePower user 98vtec, Honda-Tech moderator TheShodan) strongly disputes the notion that closed deck is significantly stronger for stock applications. Key arguments: (a) the "closed" portion is only ~0.5 inch at the very top of the cylinder, (b) the thrust point of the piston is nowhere near the top of the cylinder wall, (c) with proper sleeving, the original deck type is irrelevant. The closed deck advantage is real but marginal for NA applications and primarily relevant for high-boost forced induction (>400 HP with boost).

7. **Sales figures:** CarBuzz reports 58,118 total 5th-gen units sold (all markets). Ars Technica cites "826,082 Preludes sold in the United States" across ALL generations — this is a cumulative total, not 5th-gen only. The 4th-gen sold 98,627 units. The 5th-gen decline from ~15,000/year to ~3,500 (partial year 2001) is consistent across multiple sources.

### Key Takeaways

- The H22A4 is the sole USDM engine for the 5th-gen Prelude (1997–2001), offered in Base and Type SH trims
- Key engineering changes from H22A1: open deck block, 55 mm main journals (1998+), OBD2 P13 ECU with immobilizer, 62 mm throttle body, +5–10 hp gain
- The 5th-gen Prelude was Honda's attempt to revitalize a declining nameplate through ATTS technology and angular styling
- Internal competition (Accord Coupe, Integra) and SUV market shift killed the Prelude after 2001
- The H22A4 is now beloved among enthusiasts despite commercial failure — the last DOHC VTEC in a USDM Honda until 2026
- Reliability improves over the generation lifecycle — 2000 model year is the sweet spot

### Implications for Building/Modding

- **Open deck is adequate for stock/NA builds:** The community consensus is clear — ringlands will fail before sleeves on stock H22A4 blocks. No need to seek a closed-deck H22A1 for NA applications.
- **Forced induction caveat:** If targeting >300 HP with boost, consider an H22A1 (closed deck) or invest in a reinforced sleeve package (CSS/Darton with upper deck brace)
- **1998+ main journals:** 1998+ blocks use 55 mm mains — different bearings and crankshaft from 1997 and H22A1. Cross-compatibility requires careful parts matching.
- **ECU tuning challenge:** The P13 ECU is locked down — no Hondata S300 support. Full tuning requires either a P28 (OBD1) conversion via harness adapter or standalone ECU (AEM, Hondata FlashPro with custom tune).
- **1999+ power bump:** The 200 hp facelift version has minor ECU recalibration gains. Hardware is identical to 1997–1999.
