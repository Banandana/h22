## Summary

The H22A5 is the European-market DOHC VTEC engine used in the 5th-generation Honda Prelude (BB6/BB8 chassis) from 1997 to 1998/99. Officially rated at 185 PS (136 kW; 182 hp) at 7,000 RPM with 212 Nm of torque at 5,250 RPM, it was the bridge between the 4th-gen H22A2 and the later H22A8. Despite its modest official rating, community consensus and dyno evidence suggest many examples produced significantly more power — up to 200+ PS — due to loose ECU calibration and the use of European 95-octane fuel. The H22A5 shares the same open-deck block architecture as the USDM H22A4 but differs in ECU programming, emissions equipment, exhaust system, and throttle body sizing. This document compiles all available technical data, variant comparisons, market context, and community insights.

## Market Positioning

### European Context

The H22A5 occupied a unique position in Honda's European lineup:

1. **Bridge Between Generations:** It was the first H22 variant in the 5th-gen Prelude, bridging the gap between the 4th-gen H22A2 (185 PS, 1992–1996) and the later H22A8 (200 PS, 1999–2001).

2. **Insurance Strategy:** UK insurance classification placed the H22A5 in group 17. Community members theorized that Honda deliberately reduced output by ~15 hp to avoid pushing into insurance group 18. One forum member noted the "fiscal horsepower" figure of 10.99 — suspiciously just under the 11.00 threshold that would trigger a higher insurance bracket.

3. **Emissions Compliance:** The H22A5 was tuned to meet Euro 2 emissions standards. The detuning was achieved primarily through:
   - ECU remapping (richer fuel curves, conservative ignition timing)
   - More restrictive catalytic converter (shorter downpipe, different cat media)
   - Smaller throttle body (60 mm vs 62 mm on H22A8)
   - No knock sensor (allowing loose tuning but also limiting performance potential)

4. **Competitive Positioning:** Against contemporaries like the BMW 3 Series (323i, 328i), Mercedes-Benz C-Class (C230, C280), and Audi A4 (2.6, 2.8), the H22A5 offered superior specific output (~84 PS/liter) and the unique VTEC character. However, it lacked the refinement, luxury, and brand cachet of German competitors.

## Power Output Controversy

### The "Missing 15 HP" Debate

A persistent topic in European Prelude forums is why the H22A5 is officially rated at 185 PS when the USDM H22A4 is rated at 200 PS (despite both producing similar real-world output). Two dominant theories emerged from community discussions:

**Theory 1: Insurance Group Strategy**
- Honda targeted insurance group 17 in the UK (vs. group 18 for higher-output variants)
- Fiscal horsepower figure of 10.99 — suspiciously just under the 11.00 threshold
- 15 hp reduction appears deliberate to avoid a higher insurance classification
- Supported by: PreludeOnline (nuro, Prelude UK posts, 2002)

**Theory 2: Emissions Equipment**
- European catalytic converters more restrictive than US equivalents
- Different ECU mapping for Euro 2 compliance
- Larger/more restrictive CAT (34.5 cm downpipe)
- No knock sensor means conservative tuning
- Supported by: PreludeOnline (nuro, RZ Rob E-Spec posts, 2002); PreludePower (H22A8 post, 2007)

**Consensus:** Most experienced owners agree that the H22A5's internal components are identical to the H22A4/H22A8, and the power difference is entirely external — exhaust restriction, ECU calibration, and possibly minor intake differences. Many H22A5 owners report swapping to H22A8 components (larger throttle body, polished headers, improved exhaust) to gain 10–15 hp.

### Swap Compatibility

The H22A5 is highly interchangeable with other H22 variants:

- **Swap to H22A4 (USDM):** Direct fit — same block, same mounts. Requires OBD2-to-OBD1 harness conversion (Rywire ~$100–$150).
- **Swap to H22A8 (EDM):** Direct fit — same block, same mounts. H22A8 ECU provides better tuning headroom.
- **Swap to JDM Type-S (H22A Redtop):** Direct fit — same block family. Requires significant wiring harness work and potentially different motor mounts.
- **Swap to H22A7 (Accord Type R):** Direct fit — same block. Higher-compression pistons require 98+ octane fuel.

## Known Issues and Reliability

### Common Problems

| Issue | Severity | Description | Mitigation |
|-------|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Timing belt tensioner wear** | Critical | Hydraulic auto-tensioner (OBD1) prone to failure | Replace every 60,000 km; consider H23 manual conversion |
| **FRM liner oil consumption** | Moderate | FRM liners interact with piston rings over time | Regular oil changes (5,000 km intervals); synthetic oil |
| **PCV system oil ingestion** | Moderate | PCV system draws oil vapors into intake | Oil catch can upgrade; PCV breather valve modification |
| **Balance shaft seal failure** | Low-Moderate | Wet-sump scavenging limits under track conditions | Monitor oil levels; upgrade oil cooler |
| **Water pump leaks** | Low-Moderate | Common on 25+ year old engines | Replace proactively during timing belt service |
| **Electrical connector corrosion** | Low | Age-related corrosion on OBD1 connectors | Clean and protect connectors; dielectric grease |

### Strengths

- **Forged crankshaft and connecting rods** — strong bottom end, suitable for moderate power increases
- **Open-deck block with 55 mm main journals (1998+)** — improved rigidity over 4th-gen
- **Simple OBD1 ECU** — easy to tune, no immobilizer complications
- **No knock sensor limitation** — conservative tuning leaves headroom for gains
- **High parts availability** — shared architecture with H22A4/H22A8 ensures good aftermarket support

## Notes

### Conflicts & Uncertainties

1. **Power output discrepancy (185 PS vs 200 PS):**
   - Wikipedia lists 185 PS (136 kW; 182 hp) consistently
   - automobile-catalog.com lists 147 kW / 200 PS (ECE) for the same vehicle
   - QWEN.md lists 200 PS @ 7,000 RPM in the master table
   - Community consensus (PreludeOnline, PreludePower) supports 185 PS as the official figure, with real-world output often exceeding it
   - **Resolution:** 185 PS is the official CEE-rated figure. The 200 PS figure may reflect ECE-rated power (different measurement standard) or community misattribution. The true real-world output appears to be 183–200+ bhp depending on individual car condition.

2. **Compression ratio (10.0:1 vs 10.6:1):**
   - Forum member 97VTI lude 4WS confirms 10.0:1 for H22A5
   - Some community members speculate 10.6:1 (matching H22A8)
   - **Resolution:** 10.0:1 is confirmed by an H22A5 owner. The H22A8 introduced higher compression (10.6:1 or 11.0:1).

3. **H22A8 compression ratio ambiguity:**
   - Forum member H22A8 claims 11.0:1 for the H22A8
   - Forum member Anabasis claims 10.6:1 for their H22A5
   - Wikipedia does not specify H22A8 compression
   - **Resolution:** Likely varies by production batch. Early H22A8 (1999) may have been 10.6:1; later examples 11.0:1.

4. **Whether H22A5 had internal differences from H22A4:**
   - Forum member RZ Rob E-Spec (2002): "there is no known modification in the internal engine, that is for sure. it all comes down to exhaust (complete, header, cat, pipe, exhaust), intake and ecu."
   - Forum member nuro (2002): Marcucci suspects internal differences due to different engine designation
   - **Resolution:** No confirmed internal differences. All evidence points to external differences only (exhaust, intake, ECU, compression ratio).

5. **ATTS compatibility with H22A5:**
   - Forum member Anabasis (2007): Owns VTi-S + ATTS with H22A5 engine
   - Forum member Chief_Rocka (2007): H22A5 uses non-ATTS block casting
   - **Resolution:** ATTS-equipped H22A5 examples exist, but the block casting is the same non-ATTS variant. The ATTS unit bolts to the transmission regardless of block casting.

### Key Takeaways

- The H22A5 is the European-market 5th-gen Prelude engine (1997–1998/99), rated at 185 PS official / 183–200+ bhp real-world
- Internally identical to the H22A4 (USDM) — same block, crank, rods, pistons, cams
- External differences from H22A4/H22A8: ECU calibration, exhaust system, throttle body size, compression ratio
- The "detuning" was likely driven by insurance classification strategy (UK group 17) and Euro 2 emissions compliance
- High modification potential — bolt-on upgrades can recover the "missing" 15 hp
- Excellent swap candidate due to shared architecture with H22A4/H22A8/JDM variants
- Appreciating collector value, but currently undervalued relative to JDM Type-S and USDM Type SH
- Timing belt tensioner remains the single most critical maintenance item

### Implications for Building/Modding

- **Budget build path:** H22A5 → H22A8 ECU + 62 mm throttle body + polished headers + improved exhaust = +10–15 bhp for ~£800–£1,500
- **Maximum build path:** H22A5 block + JDM Type-S cams + single-runner intake + H22A8 pistons (11.0:1) + upgraded fuel system = +40–50 bhp potential
- **Swap recommendation:** H22A5 is an excellent donor for USDM H22A4 owners seeking OBD1 simplicity, and vice versa
- **Critical maintenance:** Replace timing belt tensioner every 60,000 km; consider H23 manual conversion for track use
- **Oil management:** Install oil catch can and upgrade PCV system to mitigate FRM liner oil consumption
