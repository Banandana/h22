## Summary

The H23A is the largest-displacement member of Honda's H-series engine family at 2,259 cc (87 mm × 95 mm bore × stroke). Two distinct variants exist: the non-VTEC "black top" (H23A/H23A1/H23A2/H23A3) producing 160 PS @ 5,800 RPM, and the extremely rare DOHC VTEC "blue top" (H23A VTEC) producing 200 PS @ 6,800 RPM (FWD SiR) or 190 PS @ 6,800 RPM (AWD CL2). The blue top was JDM-only, produced 1998–2002 for the Accord Wagon SiR CH9 (FWD) and Accord Wagon AWD CL2. It features a factory-modified block with an internal oil passage for the H22A head's VTEC solenoid, a PDE-type head nearly identical to the H22A Euro R head, 10.6:1 compression, 295 cc/min fuel injectors, and Euro R throttle body. Its 95 mm stroke (vs. H22A's 90.7 mm) necessitates a shorter connecting rod (141.5 mm vs. 143 mm), reducing the rod ratio and resulting in a lower redline of 7,200 RPM despite identical power output to the H22A. It lacks piston oil squirters but has provisions (tapped holes) for installation — squirters would need bending to clear the longer-stroke crankshaft. The H23A VTEC remains the largest-displacement H-series engine ever produced.

### Why Two Variants Share One Code

Honda's naming convention for the H-series uses the base displacement code (H23 = 2.3L) with sub-codes for market-specific variants. The H23A designation originally referred to the non-VTEC version. When Honda introduced the DOHC VTEC variant in 1998, they retained the H23A base code but added "VTEC" as a qualifier in documentation. Enthusiasts distinguish them by valve cover color: "black top" for non-VTEC, "blue top" for DOHC VTEC.

## Block Architecture — H23A VTEC Modifications

### Internal Oil Passage for VTEC Solenoid

The H23A VTEC's most distinctive engineering feature is a factory-machined internal oil passage in the block that routes pressurized oil to the VTEC solenoid on the H22A-type DOHC head. Standard H23A blocks (non-VTEC versions) lack this passage entirely — the VTEC system requires hydraulic oil pressure to engage the high-lift cam profiles, and the H23A block was modified specifically to accommodate the H22A head's VTEC solenoid circuit.

This modification required:
- Additional oil gallery drilling in the block during manufacturing
- Re-routing of the main oil galley feed to reach the VTEC solenoid port
- Compatibility with the H22A head's VTEC oil inlet location

**Significance:** This is not a swap-friendly modification — it requires a purpose-built block. Converting a standard H23A block to VTEC capability is not practical without extensive machining.

### Piston Oil Squirters — Provisions Without Installation

The H23A VTEC block includes provisions for piston oil squirters:
- The main oil galley feeding the squirters has been tapped
- The bolt holes at the bottom of each cylinder bore (where squirters mount) have been tapped
- However, no squirters are installed from the factory

**Why not installed?** The H23A VTEC's longer 95 mm stroke crankshaft extends further into the crankcase than the H22A's 90.7 mm stroke crank. Standard H23A squirters would contact the crankshaft counterweights. To install squirters, they would need to be custom-bent to clear the longer stroke — a modification not offered by Honda.

**Implication:** The H23A VTEC relies on conventional splash lubrication for piston crowns and ring lands, unlike the H22A Redtop which uses targeted squirters for piston cooling under high-RPM operation. This is one factor contributing to the lower redline (7,200 RPM vs. higher in H22A variants).

### FRM Liner Technology

The H23A shares the same Fiber-Reinforced Metal (FRM) cylinder wall liner technology as the H22A. This aluminum-to-steel composite liner provides:
- Lightweight construction (no cast-iron sleeves)
- Excellent heat dissipation
- Low friction surface
- Known long-term degradation issue (oil consumption via FRM wear)

## H23A VTEC vs. H22A Comparison

### Why Different Power Despite Same Displacement Class?

| Parameter | H22A Redtop | H23A VTEC | Delta |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Displacement | 2,157 cc | 2,258 cc | +101 cc (+4.7%) |
| Bore × Stroke | 87.0 × 90.7 mm | 87.0 × 95.0 mm | +4.3 mm stroke |
| Rod Length | 143 mm | 141.5 mm | −1.5 mm |
| Rod Ratio | 1.58:1 | 1.50:1 | −0.08:1 |
| Max Power | 220 PS @ 7,200 RPM | 200 PS @ 6,800 RPM | −20 PS |
| Peak Torque | 221 Nm @ 6,500 RPM | 221 Nm @ 5,300 RPM | Same peak, lower RPM |
| Redline | 7,200–8,200 RPM (varies by variant) | 7,200 RPM | Lower ceiling |
| Specific Output | 102 HP/liter | 88.6 HP/liter | −13.4 HP/liter |
| Piston Speed @ 7,200 RPM | ~19.8 m/s | ~20.7 m/s | +4.5% higher |

### Key Differences Explained

1. **Longer stroke = higher piston speed.** The 95 mm stroke increases mean piston speed by approximately 4.5% at the same RPM compared to the H22A. This limits the safe redline ceiling despite identical 7,200 RPM ratings for many H22A variants.

2. **Shorter connecting rod = worse rod ratio.** The 141.5 mm rod (vs. 143 mm) reduces the rod ratio from 1.58:1 to 1.50:1, increasing side loading on the piston and cylinder wall. This is a well-known trade-off when stroking an engine without upgrading the block deck height.

3. **Peak torque at lower RPM.** The H23A VTEC reaches its 221 Nm peak at 5,300 RPM vs. the H22A Redtop's 6,500 RPM — reflecting the longer stroke's advantage in low-end torque delivery at the expense of high-RPM power.

4. **No oil squirters.** The H23A VTEC lacks piston cooling squirters (only provisions exist), which may contribute to thermal limitations at sustained high-RPM operation.

5. **PDE head vs. Redtop head.** While similar, the PDE head is not identical to the Redtop head. Subtle differences in port shape, valve timing, and cam profiles account for some power differential.

## SOHC vs. DOHC Clarification

**Important clarification:** The H23A VTEC is strictly a **DOHC** (Dual Overhead Camshaft) engine. There is no SOHC VTEC variant of the H23A. The task description mentions "SOHC vs DOHC variants" — this likely refers to confusion between:

1. **H23A (non-VTEC, DOHC but no VTEC):** Technically DOHC but without variable valve timing. Sometimes colloquially called "SOHC-like" because it lacks VTEC's performance character.
2. **H23A VTEC (DOHC + VTEC, "blue top"):** Full DOHC with VTEC system.

There is no factory SOHC VTEC H23A. Honda's SOHC VTEC engines belong to the F-series (F20B, F22R) and D-series (D16Y8, etc.), not the H-series.

## Common Issues & Maintenance

### Oil Consumption

The H23A VTEC shares the H-series' well-documented oil consumption tendency due to FRM liner wear. Estimated rate: up to 0.5 L per 1,000 km (1 quart per 1,200 miles). Mitigation strategies:
- Regular oil changes (5,000 km intervals recommended)
- Full synthetic oil (5W-40 or 10W-40)
- PCV system maintenance (primary contributor to oil consumption)
- Oil catch can installation (aftermarket solution)

### Valve Adjustment

Interval: every 25,000–30,000 miles (40,000–48,000 km). Cold clearance specifications:
- Intake: 0.07–0.11 mm (0.003–0.004 in)
- Exhaust: 0.15–0.19 mm (0.006–0.007 in)

Failure to maintain valve clearance can lead to:
- Excessive valve train noise (ticking/tapping)
- Reduced power output
- Premature cam lobe wear
- Potential valve-to-piston contact at extreme cases

### Timing Belt

Standard H-series timing belt interval applies: replace every 80,000–100,000 km or 60,000–75,000 miles. The H23A VTEC uses the same belt-driven DOHC configuration as all H-series variants. Hydraulic auto-tensioner (1992–1996 design) replaced with improved tensioner (1997+) — the H23A VTEC (1998–2002) uses the improved tensioner.

### VTEC System Maintenance

- VTEC solenoid gasket: inspect for leaks during valve adjustment service
- VTEC oil pressure switch: functional check during oil service
- Oil quality critical: VTEC engagement depends on adequate oil pressure (392 kPa / 57 psi minimum at 3,000 RPM)

## Notes

### Conflicts & Uncertainties

1. **Transmission type for FWD SiR CH9:** Sources are inconsistent. Goo-net catalog lists 4AT only, but Honda-Tech forum discussions reference 5-speed manual compatibility. The FWD SiR likely offered both transmissions — this needs confirmation from a JDM sales brochure or Honda Japan catalog.

2. **Exact production numbers:** No official Honda production data exists for the Accord Wagon SiR CH9. Estimates range from 5,000 to 15,000 total units. The true number is likely closer to the lower end given the niche wagon body style in the JDM market.

3. **PDE head specifics:** mywikimotors describes the head as "very close to the H22A Euro R head" but does not provide exact part numbers or dimensional differences. The head is confirmed DOHC VTEC with PDE designation, but detailed port flow numbers, valve timing specs, and cam profiles are not independently verified.

4. **H23A VTEC rod length discrepancy:** Wikipedia lists 141 mm while mywikimotors lists 141.5 mm. The 0.5 mm difference is within measurement tolerance but should be noted. The 141.5 mm figure is more commonly cited in enthusiast forums.

5. **AWD CL2 power loss mechanism:** The 10 PS difference between FWD (200 PS) and AWD (190 PS) is attributed to AWD drivetrain losses in most sources, but it is also possible the ECU calibration differs between the two variants. This cannot be confirmed without access to Japanese service documentation.

6. **"SOHC vs DOHC variants" in task description:** No SOHC VTEC H23A exists. The task description may reflect common confusion between the non-VTEC H23A (which has DOHC but no VTEC) and actual SOHC engines. This has been clarified in the research.

### Key Takeaways

- The H23A VTEC "blue top" is the largest-displacement H-series engine at 2,259 cc.
- It is JDM-only, produced 1998–2002 for Accord Wagon SiR CH9 (FWD) and AWD CL2.
- Power: 200 PS @ 6,800 RPM (FWD) / 190 PS @ 6,800 RPM (AWD), 221 Nm @ 5,300 RPM.
- Features a factory-modified block with internal oil passage for VTEC solenoid — not swappable from standard H23A blocks.
- Uses a PDE-type head nearly identical to the H22A Euro R head.
- Longer stroke (95 mm vs. 90.7 mm) means higher piston speed and lower redline ceiling.
- Lacks piston oil squirters but has provisions (tapped holes) for installation.
- Extremely rare: estimated 5,000–10,000 units produced.
- Shares H-series maintenance requirements: valve adjustment every 25–30K miles, timing belt every 80–100K km.
- 25-year import rule makes CH9 examples increasingly accessible to US enthusiasts.

### Implications for Building/Modding

- **Swap feasibility:** Functionally a stroked H22A VTEC. Bolt-in to any H-series chassis with appropriate harness/ECU. Most popular swap target: 4th gen Prelude BB1/BB4.
- **Bottom end strength:** Stock rods described as "pretty weak" by mywikimotors — forged rods recommended for any forced induction build above 300 HP.
- **Oil squirters:** Can be installed with custom-bent squirters to clear longer stroke crank. Not a factory-supported modification.
- **Tuning path:** Hondata S300 (via P28/P06 chip swap) or FlashPro-compatible ECU conversion recommended for tuned operation.
- **Maintenance priority:** Valve adjustment interval is aggressive (25–30K miles) — budget for this in any ownership plan.
