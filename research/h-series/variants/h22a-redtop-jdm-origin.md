## Summary

The Honda H22A Redtop is the highest-output variant of the H22 engine family, developed exclusively for the Japanese Domestic Market (JDM). It debuted in September 1991 alongside the fourth-generation Honda Prelude (BB1 chassis), making it one of the first DOHC VTEC engines offered by Honda in any market. The Redtop was fitted to the Prelude SiR, SiR S-Spec, and Type S in Japan, as well as the Accord SiR sedan/wagon and the Torneo/Euro-R. It produced 220 PS (162 kW; 217 hp) @ 7,200 RPM and 221 Nm (163 lb-ft) @ 6,500 RPM, with an 11.0:1 compression ratio — the highest of any production H22 variant. Honda discontinued the Redtop in Japan in 2001 when Prelude production ended. The engine's design philosophy — high specific output, aggressive valvetrain, and closed-deck block — made it the benchmark against which all other H22 variants were measured.

## When Introduced

The H22A Redtop was introduced in **September 1991** as part of the fourth-generation Honda Prelude (BB1 chassis) launch in Japan. This was Honda's flagship sports coupe at the time, positioned above the Si (F22B, 160 PS) and Si (H23A1, 160 PS) trims. The Redtop-equipped SiR S-Spec and Type S represented the top-of-the-line performance tier.

The introduction coincided with Honda's broader strategy of establishing the **Honda Verno** dealership channel (alongside Honda Clio and Honda Primo), where the Prelude was sold as a premium sports coupe targeting younger buyers who wanted luxury features combined with genuine performance. The 4WS (four-wheel steering) option was available on SiR models, with 80% of Japanese buyers opting for it in the first year.

## Market Exclusivity

The H22A Redtop was **exclusively a JDM (Japanese Domestic Market) engine**. It was never officially sold in any export market. However, its design philosophy influenced several export variants:

### JDM Exclusive (True Redtop — 220 PS)

| Market | Availability |
|--------|-------------|
| Japan | Yes — Prelude SiR S-Spec, Type S, Accord SiR, Euro-R/Torneo Euro-R |
| United States | No — USDM received H22A1 (190 PS, Blacktop) in 4th gen, H22A4 (200 PS, Blacktop) in 5th gen |
| Europe | No — EDM received H22A2 (185 PS, Blacktop) in 4th gen, H22A5/A8 in 5th gen |
| Australia | No — AUS received H22A1/H22Z1 (190–203 PS, Blacktop) |
| Canada | No — CDN received H22A1/H22A4 (same as USDM) |

### Redtop-Derived Export Variants

| Variant | Market | Power | Connection to Redtop |
|---------|--------|-------|---------------------|
| H22A7 | Europe (Accord Type R) | 212 PS | Red valve cover, 11.0:1 CR, similar cam profile — Redtop-derived |
| H22A8 | Europe (Prelude VTi-S) | 200 PS | Red valve cover, Type S cams in H22A2 head |

### Why JDM Exclusive?

Several factors kept the Redtop exclusive to Japan:

1. **Emissions regulations:** The 11.0:1 compression ratio and aggressive cam profile required premium fuel (100 RON minimum) and could not meet US Tier-1 or Euro 2 emissions without significant detuning.
2. **Fuel quality:** Japanese premium fuel in the early 1990s was consistently 100 RON, whereas US premium was typically 87 AKI (~91 RON).
3. **Market positioning:** Honda reserved the Redtop for the Type S as a halo product to reinforce the Prelude's premium image in Japan.
4. **Right-hand drive:** The Redtop's applications (Prelude, Accord SiR, Torneo) were all RHD vehicles sold through Honda Verno dealerships.

## Notes

### Key Observations

1. **The H22A Redtop was the pinnacle of naturally aspirated inline-4 performance in the early 1990s.** At 220 PS from 2.2L, it achieved 100 PS/L specific output — meeting Honda's original engineering target set by Nobuhiko Kawamoto.

2. **The Redtop's 11.0:1 compression ratio was unique among mass-produced Honda engines of its era.** Only the later F20C (S2000, 11.3:1) exceeded it.

3. **The Redtop's influence extended beyond Japan.** The H22A7 (Accord Type R, Europe) and H22A8 (Prelude VTi-S, Europe) adopted the red valve cover, higher compression, and Type S cam profiles — effectively creating Redtop-inspired variants for European buyers.

4. **The Redtop was used in touring car racing.** Honda of Europe destroke the H22A block to 2.0L for Formula 3 (1994), then used it in BTCC (1995–1997) and JTCC (1996–1997), winning both championships in 1996 and 1997.

5. **The Redtop's closed-deck block (1992–1996) makes it more desirable for boosting** than the later open-deck 1997+ variants.

## Conflicts and Ambiguities

1. **Accord SiR (CD6/CF2) power output:** Some sources (Wikipedia, QWEN.md) list the CD6 Accord SiR and CF2 Accord SiR Wagon as receiving the 220 PS Redtop, while others list them at 190 PS. This conflict may reflect different model years within the CD6/CF2 production run — earlier units may have been 190 PS Blacktop, later units upgraded to 220 PS Redtop. Further verification needed against Honda service bulletins.

2. **H22A Redtop vs. H22A7 Accord Type R:** The H22A7 shares the red valve cover and 11.0:1 CR with the JDM Redtop but uses a single runner intake manifold (vs. dual runner on the JDM Redtop) and produces 212 PS vs. 220 PS. The H22A7 is a European-market variant, not a JDM Redtop, but is clearly inspired by the same design philosophy.

3. **VTEC engagement RPM:** Sources vary between 5,200 RPM and 5,500 RPM for Redtop VTEC engagement. This likely reflects the load-dependent VTEC window — engagement can occur as low as 5,200 RPM under heavy throttle or as high as 5,500 RPM under light load.

4. **Type S vs. SiR S-Spec distinction:** Both received the 220 PS Redtop. The Type S included ATTS (Active Torque Transfer System) and a hood spoiler; the SiR S-Spec had the same engine but lacked ATTS in most model years. Some sources conflate the two.
