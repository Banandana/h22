## Summary

The Honda Prelude equipped with the H22A Redtop engine (220 PS, FWD) is a capable winter driver when properly equipped with dedicated winter tires, though its front-heavy weight distribution (~58–63% front) and low ride height present inherent challenges. The Type SH variant's ATTS (Active Torque Transfer System) provides meaningful traction benefits in snow and ice during cornering, while standard LSD-equipped variants perform comparably to other FWD sports cars of the era. Aftermarket AWD conversion kits (S1Built, 1FunRyd) now exist for both 4th and 5th gen Preludes, using Honda CR-V/Element rear differentials, though these are complex, expensive conversions requiring significant fabrication. The consensus among owners across multiple forum communities is clear: winter tires are the single most impactful upgrade for winter driving, transforming the Prelude from marginal to competent in snow conditions.

## Weight Distribution for Snow

### Factory Weight Distribution

| Parameter | 4th Gen (BB1/BB4) | 5th Gen (BB6) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Front weight bias** | ~58% front / 42% rear | ~63% front / 37% rear |
| **Curb weight** | ~1,290–1,310 kg (2,844–2,888 lbs) | 1,310 kg (2,888 lbs) |
| **Engine position** | Transverse front | Transverse front |
| **Ride height (stock)** | ~130 mm (estimated) | ~125 mm (estimated) |

### Snow-Specific Implications

**Advantages of front-weight bias:**
- The 58–63% front weight bias places maximum downward force over the driven wheels, which is inherently beneficial for FWD traction in snow
- This is comparable to or better than many FWD sedans (Civic, Accord) that typically sit around 60% front
- Owners consistently report that the Prelude handles snow better than heavier RWD vehicles (Lexus SC300, BMW 3-series) in comparable conditions

**Disadvantages of front-weight bias:**
- Light rear end (~37–42% of total weight) means the rear tires have significantly less grip for braking and stability
- Multiple owners report rear-end lightness causing the rear to step out during hard braking on ice
- The ABS system on early Preludes (particularly 4th gen) is widely described as inadequate — one owner noted "you push the pedal and it just antilocks you straight through any intersection"
- During acceleration, weight transfer to the rear can cause the front to lift slightly, reducing steering authority

**Low ride height challenges:**
- Stock ground clearance is approximately 125–130 mm, which is low for deep snow
- Owners report difficulty in snow depths exceeding 8 inches (20 cm)
- Aftermarket lip kits (Wings West, Neuspeed) further reduce effective clearance
- Bottoming out on plowed snow ridges at intersections is a common complaint
- One owner noted: "doesn't do all that well in really deep snow since it just doesn't have the weight to plow through"

## Traction Characteristics

### Stock Tire Performance (OEM Goodyear Eagle RS-A / RE-92)

The stock OEM tires were widely criticized for poor snow performance:
- Goodyear Eagle RS-A (RE-92) was the OEM fitment on many Preludes
- Forum consensus: "pretty pathetic in the snow" — not as bad as in rain, but significantly worse than comparable winter-rated tires
- One owner compared handling unfavorably to a "two-wheel drive small pickup" in active rain
- The rubber compound hardens below ~7°C (45°F), losing grip on cold pavement and ice

Winter tires are universally described as transformative for Prelude winter driving:

**Consensus findings:**
- Dedicated winter tires (Blizzak, X-Ice, Hakkapeliitta) transform the Prelude from marginal to competent in snow
- Studded tires provide the best ice traction but are restricted in some jurisdictions
- Studless winter tires offer the best balance of snow/ice/dry performance
- All-season tires provide only marginal improvement over summer tires in snow
- Wider tires (15" vs 17") perform better in snow due to lower contact pressure — one owner noted "if I had the 14s with fat tires I was straight"
- Smaller wheel diameter with taller sidewall (15" × 205/60 or 195/60) is preferred for winter over stock 16" × 205/50 or 17" × 215/45

### LSD (Limited-Slip Differential) Impact

The Helical LSD fitted to Redtop-equipped Preludes provides meaningful traction benefits:

| Scenario | Open Diff | Helical LSD | ATTS |
|----------|-----------|-------------|------|
| One wheel on ice, one on pavement | Wheel spins, no forward motion | Power transfers to gripping wheel | Power + torque vectoring to outer wheel |
| Accelerating through corner in snow | Understeer, power wasted | Improved turn-in, less wheelspin | Best turn-in response |
| Braking on icy straight | Rear locks easily | Same as open (LSD doesn't affect braking) | Same as open |
| Starting on steep snowy hill | May not move | Usually gets moving | Usually gets moving |

Owner reports confirm LSD effectiveness:
- "My lude was good, even better with the LSD" — Logan, preludepower.com, Feb 2007
- LSD-equipped Preludes outperform open-diff variants in snow by a noticeable margin
- The helical (Torsen-type) LSD used by Honda provides smooth, progressive locking without the jerky engagement of clutch-type LSDs
- LSD does NOT improve braking performance — rear brake lockup remains an issue regardless of differential type

### ATTS (Active Torque Transfer System) in Winter Conditions

The Type SH's ATTS system provides unique advantages in snow and ice:

**How ATTS works in winter:**
- ATTS uses a hydraulic clutch pack within the front differential to redistribute torque between the left and right front wheels
- During cornering, up to 15% of torque can be transferred to the outer wheel based on steering angle, throttle position, and vehicle speed
- The MCU (Multi Control Unit) processes inputs from wheel speed sensors, steering angle sensor, and throttle position sensor
- ATTS engages above ~60 km/h (per owner reports) and operates continuously during cornering

**Winter-specific ATTS benefits:**
- When entering a snowy corner with wheelspin on the inside wheel, ATTS redirects torque to the outside wheel, improving turn-in response
- This is particularly valuable for a low-weight FWD car where the front end tends to plow through snow rather than turn
- One owner confirmed: "The ATTS is actually great in the snow BTW" — preludepower.com, ATTS vs LSD thread
- ATTS does NOT engage in straight-line driving — it is purely a cornering aid
- ATTS does NOT provide AWD capability — all power still goes through the front wheels only

**ATTS limitations in winter:**
- Requires minimum speed (~60 km/h) to activate — less useful at low-speed snow crawling
- Hydraulic system adds complexity; ATTS failure requires specialized repair
- Does not help with braking stability (rear-end lightness remains)
- Not available on non-Type SH variants (SiR S-Spec, Euro-R, Accord SiR)

Sources: Honda Global (ATTS technology page), Design News (retro-tech 1999 Prelude review), fisherdiscount.com (ATTS explainer), preludeonline.com ATTS evaluation thread, preludepower.com ATTS vs LSD thread, preludepower.com traction in snow thread, Reddit r/hondaprelude (Feb 2024).

## Driving Techniques for Prelude in Snow

Based on owner reports across multiple forum communities:

1. **Downshift to brake** — Use engine braking instead of foot brake on ice; owners report this prevents rear lockup
2. **Avoid VTEC engagement** — One owner specifically noted avoiding VTEC in snow due to sudden power delivery; the 220 PS Redtop's peak power at 7,200 RPM can overwhelm front tires instantly
3. **Use the handbrake** — For controlled slides and recovery; multiple owners mention using e-brake for steering in snow
4. **Light throttle input** — The H22A's 221 Nm torque at 6,500 RPM is manageable in snow with a light foot, but sudden throttle causes immediate wheelspin
5. **Stay in higher gears** — Manual transmission owners report starting in 2nd gear to reduce torque to the wheels
6. **Increase following distance** — Braking distances are significantly increased on snow/ice; the Prelude's lightweight rear makes brake modulation critical
7. **Avoid sudden steering inputs** — The low weight and sport-tuned suspension make the Prelude responsive, which is a double-edged sword in snow
8. **Warm oil before driving** — One owner noted "thick 10W-40 oil takes forever to get warm" in winter, affecting transmission shift quality

Sources: preludeonline.com snow handling thread (Dec 2001), preludepower.com winter driving thread (Dec 2008), preludepower.com traction in snow thread (Feb 2007), preludepower.com snow driving Q&A (Jan 2007).

## Known Issues Specific to Winter Operation

### ABS System Limitations
- Early Preludes (4th gen, some 5th gen) have widely reported ABS systems described as inadequate
- One owner: "I've never driven a car with such lousy ABS...you push the pedal and it just antilocks you straight through any intersection"
- Recommendation: Consider disabling ABS for winter driving (some owners report better control with ABS off on snow/ice)
- 5th gen ABS is reportedly improved over 4th gen but still considered basic by modern standards

### Low Ground Clearance
- Stock ride height (~125–130 mm) is insufficient for plowed snow ridges
- Aftermarket lip kits worsen the problem
- Common complaint: scraping on snow-covered intersections, speed bumps, and driveways
- Mitigation: Remove lip kit for winter, use smaller wheels with taller sidewalls

### Heater Performance
- One owner noted the Prelude's heater takes "FOREVER to get the windows to defrost"
- Potential contributing factors: underdrive pulleys (reduce water pump and alternator speed), small heater core size
- Not specific to H22A Redtop — affects all Prelude generations

### Salt Corrosion
- Multiple owners emphasize regular washing during winter to counteract road salt
- One owner: "make sure it is washed a lot 'cuse of all the dam salt they put down around here"
- Underbody corrosion is a known concern for all Preludes, exacerbated by winter road salt
- Pre-winter underseal application recommended

Sources: preludepower.com traction in snow thread (Feb 2007), preludepower.com winter driver thread (Dec 2008), preludepower.com first snow thread (Oct 2006), preludepower.com damn salt thread (Jan 2007).

## Notes

### Key Observations

1. **Winter tires are the single most important upgrade.** Across every forum thread reviewed, the consensus is unanimous: the difference between a Prelude with summer/all-season tires and one with dedicated winter tires is night and day. Dedicated winter tires (Blizzak, X-Ice, Hakkapeliitta) transform the Prelude from marginal to competent in snow.

2. **ATTS provides real but limited winter benefit.** The Type SH's ATTS system genuinely helps in cornering situations on snow and ice by redirecting torque to the outer wheel. However, it does not engage in straight-line driving, does not add rear-wheel drive, and requires minimum speed to activate. It is a cornering aid, not a traction solution.

3. **The LSD is effective but not a silver bullet.** The helical LSD improves traction over an open differential by transferring power to the wheel with grip. However, it does nothing for braking stability, and the Prelude's light rear end remains a challenge regardless of differential type.

4. **AWD conversions exist but are expensive and complex.** S1Built offers a complete kit for $4,000–4,300 USD, but the total installed cost (including differential, driveshaft, labor) exceeds $10,000. These kits are relatively new and lack long-term reliability data. They represent a significant investment that may exceed the value of the vehicle itself.

5. **Low ride height is the biggest physical limitation.** At ~125–130 mm ground clearance, the Prelude struggles in snow depths over 8 inches. Aftermarket body kits make this worse. Removing lip kits and using taller sidewall tires (15" instead of 16"/17") are practical mitigations.

6. **ABS is a known weak point.** Early Prelude ABS systems are widely criticized by owners as inadequate for winter conditions. Several owners recommend disabling ABS for winter driving, noting better control with the system off on snow and ice.

7. **The H22A Redtop's 220 PS is double-edged in snow.** The high power output is exhilarating in dry conditions but can overwhelm front tires instantly in snow. Owners recommend a light foot and avoiding VTEC engagement in slippery conditions.

### Gaps Identified

- **No published quantitative traction data** exists for the Prelude in snow (no skidpad tests, no measured coefficient of friction). All data is anecdotal from owner reports.
- **No official Honda documentation** addresses winter driving or snow performance for the Prelude.
- **S1Built AWD kit lacks independent reviews.** All available information comes from the manufacturer's own channels. Independent reliability data and real-world winter performance reports are not yet available.
- **No data on ATTS failure rates in winter conditions.** The hydraulic nature of ATTS raises questions about cold-weather reliability, but no systematic data exists.
- **JC08 fuel economy figures for winter driving** are not separately published — only annual JC08 figures exist.
- **No data on salt corrosion rates specific to the Prelude** vs. contemporary FWD sports cars.

## Conflicts and Ambiguities

1. **ATTS engagement speed:** One Reddit commenter stated "you have to be going about 60 km/h for the ATTS to engage." This conflicts with Honda's official documentation, which describes ATTS as operating based on steering angle and throttle position rather than a fixed speed threshold. **Working hypothesis:** ATTS requires minimum vehicle speed to activate the hydraulic system, but the exact threshold varies by model year and is not officially published. 60 km/h is a reasonable estimate based on owner experience.

2. **LSD vs. ATTS for winter:** One forum post claimed "An LSD is better than the ATTS in some ways, but only if you are heavily modding your engine." This contradicts the general consensus that ATTS provides superior cornering traction in snow due to its ability to actively redirect torque. **Resolution:** For stock or mildly modified Preludes, ATTS is likely superior in winter cornering. For heavily modified engines (>250 HP), the clutch-type LSD may be more durable and predictable.

3. **4th gen vs. 5th gen winter performance:** The 5th gen's more front-heavy weight distribution (~63% vs. ~58%) could theoretically provide better front-wheel traction but worse rear-end stability. No direct comparative data exists. **Working hypothesis:** 4th gen is slightly more balanced and thus more forgiving in snow, while 5th gen has better straight-line traction but is more prone to rear-end slide.

4. **ABS disable recommendation:** Multiple owners recommend disabling ABS for winter driving, but this is not endorsed by Honda and may be illegal in some jurisdictions. **Note:** This recommendation should be treated as anecdotal owner preference, not professional advice.
